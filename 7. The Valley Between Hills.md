## Scene 1 — "The Report"

The sun had been up for two hours and Nayeon had been awake for three.

She hadn't slept. Not really. She'd lain in bed after the meeting with Jihyo, closed her eyes, and spent the following hours in that in-between state where the body rests but the mind keeps working — reviewing decisions, anticipating consequences, mentally drafting a letter she still hadn't put to paper. The letter to the great-grandmother. Every sentence she constructed sounded like defeat, and every time she discarded it and started over, the crack in the north wing wall appeared behind her eyelids as a reminder that defeat had already happened and the letter was just the record that made it official.

At six she got up. Got dressed. Studied herself in the mirror with the clinical attention of someone evaluating a tool before using it: the right dress, hair in place, a face that wouldn't let on that inside, the heir of Twiceland was holding too many things with too few hands. Satisfied with the surface, she went down to her office.

She had a list. The list was what kept her standing — it always had been. As long as there was a next item, a pending decision, a problem to solve, Nayeon could function. The collapse came when the list ran out. That's why it never ran out.

Letter to the great-grandmother. Structural damage report. Status of the prisoners. Stable repairs. Council briefing — public version. Review of post-crisis guard assignments.

She was on the third item when someone knocked.

"Come in."

The door opened and what entered was the smell of smoke, hay, and something Nayeon would have recognized anywhere in the world: the scent of the stables that Jeongyeon wore in her clothes like a second skin, as much a part of her as the dry humor and the hands cracked from the reins.

Jeongyeon walked in with the posture of someone who has made this trip many times and knows exactly how many steps there are between the door and the point where protocol dictates stopping. She stopped. Made the bow — correct, brief, with the economy of movement of someone who respects the forms without enjoying them.

"Your Highness. I've come to report on the state of the stables."

Nayeon set her pen on the desk. The list could wait one more item.

"Go ahead."

Jeongyeon pulled a folded paper from her vest pocket — stained in one corner with something that could have been ink or could have been mud; with Jeongyeon you never knew — and consulted it with the seriousness of someone presenting a war briefing rather than a damage inventory.

"The fire affected the eastern half of the structure. Three stalls destroyed, two more with partial damage. The main section's roof needs full replacement — the secondary section decided to replace itself, so in a way it saved us the trouble." The pause was millimetric, timed for the punchline. "No animals injured. Flint refused to leave because he was asleep — I had to carry him. He has the emotional constitution of a rock."

"You carried a horse?"

"I pushed him. With great insistence and very little glamour. But technically yes, I carried him part of the way."

Nayeon felt something tugging at the corner of her mouth. Not a smile — she had no room for smiles this morning. But the ghost of one. The shadow of what would be a smile if Nayeon allowed it.

"Cricket?" she asked. For Chaeyoung's sake, but also for Jeongyeon's. Because Jeongyeon loved those horses with a ferocity most people reserved for their children, and losing one in a deliberately set fire would have broken her in ways her dry humor couldn't cover.

"Cricket was the first one I got out." Jeongyeon lowered the paper. Something shifted in her voice — the professional lightness pulled back an inch. "He was restless before the fire started. The other horses panicked with the smoke, but Cricket was already nervous. As if he knew."

Nayeon filed that away without commenting. Animal instincts weren't magic, but after last night, the line between the explainable and the inexplicable had gotten thinner than she liked.

"Good. Estimate for repairs?"

"Two weeks for the structural work, with the carpenters available. A month if you want it looking the way it was." Pause. "My recommendation is that it ends up better than it was. The stables have been begging for a renovation that nobody approves because horses don't vote on the council."

"Noted."

"Really?"

"Noted, Jeongyeon. I'll take it to the budget."

Jeongyeon looked at her one second longer than necessary. Not with surprise — with that attention of hers, the kind that wasn't calculated or strategic but simply present, like someone looking at something dear and unable to stop searching for the cracks.

And she found them.

Nayeon knew because she saw how Jeongyeon's eyes narrowed a millimeter, how her weight shifted slightly forward — the involuntary gesture of someone who wants to step closer and stops before the impulse becomes movement. Jeongyeon saw what no one else saw: the dark circles beneath the makeup, the tension in the jaw that wasn't the normal tension of an heir in crisis but something deeper, the way Nayeon was gripping the pen too hard, as if she needed to squeeze something to keep from letting go of everything else.

"Your Highness."

"Yes?"

"How many hours did you sleep?"

The question crossed the distance between them like something that shouldn't have been said and was said anyway. Jeongyeon had no right to ask that. A stable master doesn't ask the heir how many hours she slept. But Jeongyeon had never been very good with rights she didn't have — she exercised them anyway, with the ease of someone who doesn't know how to live any other way.

Nayeon could lie. She was good at lying — her whole life was an exercise in saying the right thing at the right time with the right face. But lying to Jeongyeon was like trying to lie to the mirror. It didn't fail because Jeongyeon caught her — it failed because the lie couldn't survive contact with someone who knew her from before the crown.

"Enough."

"So none."

"Jeongyeon."

"I know. It's not my place."

The phrase fell between them with the weight of something that's been repeated so many times it has its own groove in the conversation. _It's not my place._ The phrase Jeongyeon used as a wall, as a shield, as the distance she imposed on herself every time crossing that distance would have meant something neither of them could afford.

Nayeon closed her eyes. One second. Two. Something she wouldn't have allowed herself with Jihyo, or Sana, or the council, or anyone who wasn't this person standing in front of her desk smelling of smoke and hay and everything Nayeon wanted and couldn't have.

"It was a difficult night," she said. And it was the most honest thing she'd said since dawn.

Jeongyeon didn't ask why. Didn't ask what happened or how she could help or any of the things people ask when someone tells them they had a bad night. What she did was stay. One moment longer than the report required, one moment longer than protocol allowed, a moment that existed outside the rules because the rules had no room for what they were.

"The stables will be ready," Jeongyeon said. Soft. As if she were talking about stables and not something larger. "Everything that breaks can be fixed. It just needs time and someone willing to do the work."

Nayeon opened her eyes. Jeongyeon was looking at her with a face that wasn't the stable master's or Chaeyoung's friend's or the woman who joked about carrying sleeping horses. It was the face of someone who loved without conditions and lived with the single condition of never showing it.

The distance between them was a desk. A year and a half of polished wood. Nayeon's hands on the surface, knuckles white around the pen. Jeongyeon's hands at her sides, open, still, with the discipline of someone who has learned not to reach out.

One of them moved. Half a centimeter. Maybe Nayeon, whose hand released the pen and rested on the wood, a breath's distance from the desk's edge. Maybe Jeongyeon, whose weight leaned forward just enough for the window light to change the angle of her face. Half a centimeter that held everything they couldn't say and everything they couldn't stop feeling.

And it was Jeongyeon who pulled back.

Not abruptly. With the softness of a gesture that has been practiced until it became reflex — a half step back, shoulders squaring, the smile returning with slightly worn edges. The elegant retreat of someone who loves enough to leave and who breaks a little every time she does.

"I should get back to the stables." The professional voice returned, like armor closing. "The roof won't fix itself. And Flint needs someone to explain to him that fires aren't something you sleep through."

Nayeon nodded. The heir back in place too. The two people they were in public covering the two people they were in private with the efficiency of a costume change.

"Thank you for the report, Jeongyeon."

"It's my job, Your Highness."

Jeongyeon turned toward the door. Opened it. And in the doorway, half-turned, with the hallway light drawing one side of her face and leaving the other in shadow, she said:

"Take care of yourself. The kingdom needs you whole."

The word _whole_ stayed in the office after the door closed. It stayed the way things stay that mean more than they say — in the air, in the wood of the desk, in the space between Nayeon's hand and the corner where half a centimeter would have changed everything.

Nayeon went back to the list. The letter to the great-grandmother. She picked up the pen. Wrote the first line.

Her hand didn't tremble. But she held it tighter than before, because half a centimeter had reminded her of what she tried to forget every morning: that beneath the list, beneath the crown, beneath everything she carried, there was a woman who wanted someone to tell her she didn't have to be whole all the time.

And the only person who told her that was the one who always left.

## Scene 2 — "The Move"

The return to the royal wing was a matter of boxes and silences.

They hadn't brought much to the north wing — the emergency move, weeks ago, had been quick and functional: clothes, permitted books, the minimum objects a princess needs to inhabit a space that isn't hers. But things have a tendency to expand when a person stays longer than planned, and Mina had been in the north wing long enough for the space to bear her marks: the tea cup on the side table that wasn't the right side table, the books stacked with the symmetry Chaeyoung had seen her impose on any horizontal surface, the phantom impression of a presence that turned temporary rooms into something resembling a home.

And the other books. The three that weren't in any visible pile.

Mina took them out of the wardrobe while Chaeyoung checked that the hallway was clear. Wrapped in the same cloth from the library — the dark fabric that smelled of the restricted section, of old dust and bound secrets. She put them inside a bag with clothes on top: folded cloaks, a shawl, things no one would open to inspect. The gesture was practiced, efficient. As if she'd done it before. Because she had done it before.

"Ready," Mina said.

Chaeyoung took the bag. She didn't ask what was inside. Not because she didn't know — she knew exactly what weighed beneath the cloaks, had known since the night at the library when Mina asked her to help steal the books that were going to change her life — but because asking would have broken the layer of normalcy they were both building over what had happened. A thin layer, transparent, the kind that breaks if you look at it too directly. But it was what they had.

They walked through the hallways. Mina ahead, Chaeyoung one step behind with the bag on her good shoulder, sword at her hip, the guard posture that was no longer just posture but the way her body naturally organized itself when she was near Mina. The royal wing smelled different from the north wing — more polished, quieter, with that quality of air that spaces have where important people live and where the stone has absorbed centuries of decisions.

Mina's chambers were just as they'd left them. The servants had cleaned, changed the sheets, lit the candles. Everything in its place. As if the weeks in the north wing had been a parenthesis and the room had been waiting, patient, for its owner to return.

Mina entered. Stopped in the center of the room. Looked around with the expression of someone returning to a familiar place and finding it strange — not because it had changed but because she had changed. The same walls, the same bed, the same window overlooking the gardens. But the hands that last looked at those walls hadn't lifted two people off the ground.

Chaeyoung set the bag on the bed. Mina took out the books — quick, efficient — and placed them at the bottom of a drawer beneath clothes no one had any reason to move. The three spines disappeared as if they'd never existed. Mina closed the drawer. Chaeyoung said nothing. Complicity in its purest form: the kind that doesn't need verbal agreements because the agreement already exists somewhere else.

"I'm going to check the antechamber," Chaeyoung said.

The royal wing antechamber was larger than the north wing's. More space, a cot that was probably longer — though Chaeyoung wasn't going to test that until night, and with her luck it would still be too short. A small table. A hook for the sword. The water pitcher someone had already placed there, because the royal wing servants anticipated things the north wing ones didn't know needed anticipating.

And the door.

The door connecting the antechamber to Mina's room. Chaeyoung stood looking at it for a moment. Closed. The default position. The correct, professional position, the one that says _guard here, princess there, each in their own space._

She went back to Mina's room. Mina was organizing things on the desk — the normal things, the ones that could be organized in plain sight: inkwells, paper, the poetry book she read at night. She looked up when Chaeyoung entered.

They looked at each other.

The door wasn't mentioned. Neither of them said _open or closed?_ or anything that would acknowledge out loud that the position of a door had become something that meant more than security and protocol.

"Everything's in order," Chaeyoung said. "I'll set up next door."

"Good."

Chaeyoung returned to the antechamber. Put the sword on the hook. The water pitcher on the table. Sat on the bed, too short, of course, but at least the mattress didn't seem stuffed with good intentions and rocks, like the one in the north wing.

And the door, when Chaeyoung passed by it, was left ajar.

She didn't know who left it that way. She didn't remember pushing it. Maybe it had been Mina, passing by, with the naturalness of a gesture that was no longer a decision but a habit. Maybe it had been the wind, or the slope of the floor, or one of those explanations people invent for things they want to happen but don't want to admit they want.

The door was left ajar.

As always. Like everything between them.

## Scene 3 — "The Request"

### I.

Jihyo didn't even look up from the report.

"When?"

"In three days. The usual visit." Chaeyoung was standing in front of the office desk, in the posture she used for routine matters — relaxed but present, hands behind her back, weight on her right foot because the left one still hadn't forgiven the hours she'd spent standing the week before.

"Is Tzuyu going with you?"

"As always. She's bringing the medicines."

Jihyo signed the permit without pausing — a gesture she'd made dozens of times, because Chaeyoung went to Bryndell every few weeks and the paperwork was as routine as the changing of the guard. She blew on the ink. Held out the paper.

"Back before nightfall."

"Back before nightfall."

Chaeyoung took the permit. Tucked it away. Matter closed. The two easiest seconds in the entire chain that was about to get complicated.

### II.

She said it the way she always said it: professional, brief, with the efficiency of someone reporting a schedule adjustment. But something about the sentence felt different this time. Maybe it was the word _replacement_. Maybe it was that, after weeks of sleeping fifteen steps from Mina, after the awakening, after the "Mina" that floated between them like a ghost neither looked at directly, the idea of leaving her for an entire day with someone else tasted like something incomplete.

### III.

"Come in."

### V.

The antechamber was dark. The hallway torches had gone out a while ago, or been lowered, which amounted to the same thing, and the only light was what slipped through the high window: waxing moon, enough to see shapes but not details.

"Chaeyoung."

"Are you asleep?"

"Evidently not."

"My sister said yes."

Chaeyoung opened one eye. The silence that followed was the good kind. The kind shaped like two people who are fifteen steps apart with an ajar door between them and who don't need to cross it to be together.

"Don't thank me yet. I haven't met your mother."

There it was. The panic. The specific, silent, very Chaeyoung panic of _this person is going to see my house and my mother and the parts of my life that aren't armor and sword and service. The real parts. The parts underneath._

"My mother is going to adore you," Chaeyoung said, and she said it without thinking, which meant it was true.

A silence. Longer than the one before. When Mina spoke, her voice was softer.

"Good night, Chaeyoung."

"Good night, Your Highness."

The door stayed ajar. Chaeyoung closed her eye. She didn't sleep. Not immediately. She lay in the dark with the pulse of her shoulder and the certainty that in two days Mina was going to see Bryndell — the small house, the herb garden, the mother who spoke with her hands because her voice no longer reached. She was going to see everything Chaeyoung was when she wasn't being a guard, and that was more terrifying than any basilisk and more inevitable than any sunrise.

_My princess is going to meet my mother._

The thought stayed there, in the dark, and Chaeyoung didn't correct it. Not the _my_ or the _princess_ or anything it contained.

Some things grow better in the dark. Like seeds. Like secrets. Like whatever lived in the place with no name and no longer fit inside it.

### VI.

"Mina is coming to Bryndell with us."

"It's not a meet-the-parents, Tzuyu."

"Chaeyoung."

"What?"

Tzuyu set the jar on the table. Looked at her with the infinite patience of the younger sister who has spent her whole life watching the older sister do everything except the obvious.

"It's exactly a meet-the-parents."

"It's a visit. Mina wants to leave the castle. Bryndell is on the way."

"Bryndell isn't on the way to anywhere. Bryndell is the destination."

About the cloak. About the meet-the-parents. About everything.

## Scene 4 — "Beyond the Walls"

The east wall gate opened with a groan of old metal and damp wood that probably carried all the way to the kitchens. Two guards pushed it from inside — slowly, as if opening that door were a ceremonial act and not a procedure they carried out several times a week to let supply carts through.

Chaeyoung went first, with Cricket beneath her moving with the confidence of an animal who knows this road by heart. Tzuyu rode behind on a bay mare carrying saddlebags with the medicine jars. The two escort guards — Nayeon's condition, non-negotiable — waited thirty paces back, close enough to intervene and far enough not to intrude. Jihyo had chosen them personally: discreet, competent, with the specific instruction not to speak to the princess unless spoken to first.

Mina came out last.

Chaeyoung turned in the saddle to look at her, and what she saw made her forget for half a second that she was sitting on a horse.

Mina had stopped just past the threshold. The horse — a light gray mare that Jeongyeon had selected that morning with a care Chaeyoung didn't need to be a genius to interpret — took two steps forward and stopped as well, as if understanding that her rider needed a moment.

It was Mina's face. Not the composure, not the mask, not the carefully calibrated expression Chaeyoung had learned to read between the lines over weeks. It was something prior to all of that. Something that had no layers because it didn't know it needed them.

The road opened before her like a sentence that didn't end. Fields on both sides — green as far as the eye could see, splashed with yellow where the dandelions had decided to bloom without asking permission. The forest to the left, an irregular line of oaks and birches climbing the hill. And above, the sky. The sky without a ceiling, without a stone vault, without the frame of a window or a terrace cutting it into manageable fragments. The entire sky, horizon to horizon, so open it made you dizzy.

Mina lifted her face toward it. The morning sun hit her eyes and she didn't blink.

"Your Highness," Chaeyoung said, and her voice came out softer than she intended. "Everything all right?"

Mina didn't respond immediately. She breathed. One long, conscious inhale, as if she were cataloguing the air — every scent, every temperature, every particle that wasn't stone or library dust or candle wax. Wet earth. Grass. Something floral coming from the forest. And beneath it all, something that had no name: the absence of walls.

"It's bigger than I imagined," she said.

Chaeyoung didn't know if she meant the sky, the fields, the road, or the world. Probably all of it.

"Wait until we pass the forest. There's a valley after."

Mina's eyes found hers. There was something in them Chaeyoung had never seen — not the curiosity of someone who reads about a place, but the hunger of someone seeing it for the first time. The difference between knowing the sky is blue and feeling the blue on your face.

"Let's go," Mina said. And she touched the mare's flanks with a determination that was so Mina that Chaeyoung almost smiled.

They rode.

---

The road to Bryndell wasn't spectacular. There were no snow-capped mountains or turbulent rivers or anything deserving of an epic poem. It was a packed-dirt trail between farmland that eventually became a forest path and then opened again into rolling meadows. Chaeyoung had traveled it so many times she could do it with her eyes closed — she knew where the roots jutted out, where the mud pooled after rain, where you had to turn left where the road forked at a rock shaped like a sitting dog.

For Chaeyoung, it was routine. For Mina, it was everything.

Chaeyoung watched her from the corner of her eye as they rode. Mina looked at each thing as if she were reading it — with that careful attention she gave to pages, but applied to the world. The fields, the way the wheat moved in the wind like a slow wave. The butterflies crossing the path in pairs. A stream that ran beneath a stone bridge so narrow they had to cross single file. Mina stopped on the bridge. Looked at the water. Chaeyoung let her look.

"I've never seen a stream," Mina said, and she didn't say it with sadness — she said it in the same voice she used to read aloud the passages she liked. Careful wonder. Reverence for something that was new and deserved to be treated with respect.

"There's a bigger one near Bryndell," Chaeyoung said. "A wooden bridge crosses it that my father built. Or so my mother says. I never saw it whole — by the time I had memories, half the planks were already missing."

"And people cross it?"

"The people of Bryndell will cross anything if there's a tavern on the other side."

The sound Mina made wasn't a laugh. It was what she did instead of laughing — that quick exhale through the nose that Chaeyoung had learned to seek out as evidence she'd said something right.

Tzuyu rode behind them. Quiet. Watching. With the patience of someone who is seeing something she's been waiting a long time to see and who knows the best thing she can do is not interrupt it.

---

They entered the forest.

The change was immediate: the light fragmented into shafts falling between the branches, the air cooled two degrees, and the sound shifted from the openness of the fields to the enclosed acoustics of the trees — hooves muffled by leaves, birdsong closer, sharper.

Mina adjusted in the saddle. Chaeyoung noticed because she'd been noticing every one of Mina's movements on horseback since they left — the way she rode, correct but rigid, with the posture of someone who has taken lessons but has never ridden for more than an hour straight. The thigh muscles would start protesting soon. Chaeyoung knew from experience and because she knew that kind of pain the way she knew all physical pain: intimately.

"If you bend your knees a little, the saddle absorbs the impact," Chaeyoung said. She said it without looking at her, casual, as if it were a comment about the weather. "The trick is letting your body follow the horse instead of fighting against it."

"I'm not fighting against it."

"Your Highness, with all due respect, you're riding as if the horse were a book and you were looking for the right page."

From behind, Tzuyu made a sound that could have been a cough and could have been a laugh. Chaeyoung decided not to investigate.

Mina adjusted her knees. The difference was immediate — the shoulders dropped half a centimeter, the back loosened, and the mare, who had spent ten minutes trying to transmit by equine telepathy that her rider was too tense, snorted with something resembling relief.

"Better," Chaeyoung said.

"You didn't tell me it would hurt."

"It didn't hurt."

"My thighs disagree."

"That's normal. The first few kilometers are always the worst. After that the body adjusts."

"How many kilometers are left?"

"Quite a few."

Mina looked at her. Chaeyoung looked straight ahead with the calculated innocence of someone who knows exactly what she's doing.

"We can stop for a moment if—"

"No."

It was definitive. Not with irritation but with that silent determination Mina wielded the way others wielded swords. She wasn't going to be the princess who couldn't handle a ride on horseback. She wasn't going to be the reason they stopped. She wasn't going to confirm the idea that the world beyond the walls was too much for her.

Chaeyoung understood. She recognized the stubbornness because she carried it in her blood.

"Then bend your knees," she said. "And grip with your thighs, not your hands. The reins aren't for holding on — they're for talking to the horse."

"Talking?"

"The horse feels everything you feel through the reins. If you're tense, he knows. If you're calm, he knows. It's a conversation."

Mina looked at the reins in her hands. Loosened them. Not much — just enough. The mare shook her head, gently, like a nod.

"Does she have a name?" Mina asked.

"Brisa," Chaeyoung said. "Jeongyeon picked her for you. She's the calmest mare in the stable. Says she's the only one with patience for beginners and for princesses."

"Did she say princesses specifically?"

"She said 'people who've never stepped in mud.' The translation is mine."

Cricket swiveled an ear back, as if listening to the conversation. Chaeyoung ran a hand along his neck without thinking — an automatic gesture, natural, the softness she reserved for things that couldn't hurt her. The horse pushed his head against her palm.

Mina saw it. Of course she saw it. Mina saw everything, always, with that attention that was both her greatest virtue and her quietest way of loving. She saw how Chaeyoung touched Cricket — without armor, without caution, without the calculated distance she maintained with people. And she filed the image in the same place where she filed the fleeting smiles, the murmurs in sleep, the moments when the guard stopped being a guard and was just a person petting her horse as if it were the most important thing in the world.

---

"May I ask a question?"

Tzuyu's voice broke the comfortable silence that had settled among the trees. It came from behind, with the perfectly neutral tone Tzuyu used when she was about to say something not neutral at all.

"To me?" Mina said.

"My sister. But you can listen, princess"

Chaeyoung turned with the face of someone who recognizes an ambush too late to avoid it.

"Ask."

"Did you tell Her Highness about the time Cricket threw you in the river?"

"No."

"It was in her third month here," Tzuyu continued, ignoring the _no_ with the elegance of someone who has perfected the art of ignoring her older sister. "Chaeyoung insisted she knew how to ride. She got on Cricket. Cricket looked at her once, decided he didn't like her, and carried her straight into the stable creek."

"Tzu."

"She fell face-first. The water came up to her knees. Cricket stayed on the bank watching her."

"Tzuyu, I swear—"

"Jeongyeon says it's the only time she's ever seen a horse laugh."

Mina made the sound. The exhale. But this time it was more — it was something dangerously close to a real laugh, held behind the lips, escaping through the corners. Chaeyoung saw it from the corner of her eye and felt two things simultaneously: the warm embarrassment of the younger sister putting your most pathetic moments on display, and something else, something unrelated to embarrassment, that looked a lot like what she felt when the candle on the terrace made Mina's eyes look golden.

"Cricket and I reached an agreement after that," Chaeyoung said, with the dignity of defeat. "I give him carrots. He doesn't humiliate me."

"A reasonable agreement," Mina said.

"The best agreements involve bribery," Tzuyu said.

"Spoken like the voice of experience."

"Spoken like someone who's seen you bribe a horse with apples stolen from the kitchen at three in the morning."

"That was once."

"It was four times. I saw you all four."

Chaeyoung closed her mouth. Cricket advanced with a trot that, if horses could be smug, was smug.

Mina rode between the two sisters with something on her face that wasn't the composure of the castle. It was something more open. Simpler. The expression of someone who is exactly where she wants to be, hearing exactly what she wants to hear, feeling in her legs the ache of muscles that don't know how to ride and in the air the scent of forest she didn't know and somewhere in her chest something that expanded with every kilometer that carried her farther from the walls.

---

They came out of the forest.

The valley appeared without warning, the road climbed a gentle hill and suddenly the world opened like a book with its covers torn off. Green. All green, in twenty different shades, from the near-black of the distant pines to the pale yellow of the barley fields. A river crossed it like a silver scar. And in the distance, far but visible, a cluster of stone houses and wooden roofs huddled around a square that from there looked the size of a coin.

"Bryndell," Chaeyoung said.

Mina said nothing. She stayed at the top of the hill looking at the valley with wide eyes and still hands on the reins, and the heat in her palms that had been a reminder for days was now something else, it didn't pulse, it didn't burn, it expanded. As if what she held inside responded to the vastness, as if the energy that compressed against the walls in the castle here had room to breathe.

The grass at Brisa's feet swayed. There was no wind.

Chaeyoung didn't see it. She was looking at Mina. Looking at the way the sun caught her hair and the way her eyes reflected the entire valley and the way Mina, for the first time since she'd known her, seemed the size of the world.

_My princess._

The thought arrived without permission. Chaeyoung let it stay. She was starting to get tired of correcting it.

"Ready?" she said.

Mina looked at her. And smiled. Not the almost-smile Chaeyoung had spent weeks chasing, a real smile, with both sides of the mouth, small but complete, like something that had been stored away and had finally found a reason to come out.

"Ready."

They rode down the hill toward Bryndell. The sun climbed. Chaeyoung's shoulder throbbed with every step Cricket took and she ignored it the way she ignored all pains that weren't immediately fatal. Tzuyu rode behind with the jars clinking softly in the saddlebags. The guards followed at a distance, as invisible as Jihyo had instructed.

And Mina rode with her knees bent and her back relaxed and the reins soft in the hands that a week ago had shattered a room, and the world was large, and it was hers, and it had no ceiling.

## Scene 5 — "The House"

Bryndell was exactly what it looked like from the hill: a village that didn't pretend to be anything more than a village.

Twenty stone-and-wood houses, maybe twenty-five if you counted the smithy and the tavern as houses, which the people of Bryndell did because the blacksmith slept in the back room and the tavern keeper hadn't left his establishment in three years. A central square of packed earth with a well that worked and a fountain that didn't. A small chapel whose door never fully closed because the wood was swollen from the damp. Chickens. Dogs. Children playing in the street with sticks that were swords, who stood gaping at the horses when the three women entered the village from the north road.

Chaeyoung greeted them with a nod. Two of the children recognized her and ran off somewhere yelling something that sounded like "Miyoung's daughter!" The third stood frozen, staring at Cricket with the expression of someone who had just discovered that God existed and had four legs.

"Welcome to Bryndell," Chaeyoung said, and she wasn't entirely sure who she was saying it to.

Mina looked. She looked the way she'd looked the entire ride — with that silent attention that turned every detail into something worth remembering — but here it was different. It wasn't the awe of the valley or the vastness of the sky. It was something more specific. More personal. She looked at the houses as if she were reading the pages of a book someone had described to her but she'd never seen: _this is where she comes from._

Miyoung's house was at the end of the main road, where the village dissolved into the countryside and the last houses gave way to wooden fences and fruit trees. It was small. One door, two windows, a slate roof that had once been straight and now had a tilt suggesting the house had decided to lean slightly to the left, like a tired person.

And the garden.

The garden wasn't small. The garden was what happened when an herbalist who could no longer walk through forests turned three square meters of dirt into a pharmacy. Lavender. Rosemary. Mint. Sage. Plants Chaeyoung couldn't name but recognized by smell — the smell of her childhood, of her mother's hands preparing salves by candlelight, of the house in Hwaren before the fire consumed everything.

Miyoung was in the garden.

On her knees on an old cloth protecting her legs from the damp earth. Hands in the soil. Hair tied back with a scarf that had been white once and was now the indeterminate color that fabrics adopt when they've lived. She moved slowly — planting something, or transplanting, or simply touching the earth because touching the earth was one of the few things her lungs still allowed her to do without punishment.

She didn't hear them arrive. Or maybe she did, but people in Bryndell came and went and horses passed and Miyoung had learned long ago that not every sound merited the interruption of whatever she was doing.

It was Tzuyu who dismounted first. It was always Tzuyu first — a silent ritual the two sisters had established without discussing it: Tzuyu arrived, Miyoung saw her, Miyoung smiled, and that gave Chaeyoung the three seconds she needed to adjust. To take off the invisible armor. To stop being the lieutenant of the Royal Knights and start being the daughter who didn't visit enough.

Three seconds were never enough.

"Mom," Tzuyu said, and her voice had something it didn't have at the castle — something softer, younger, as if crossing the threshold of Bryndell gave her back the years the healing took away.

Miyoung raised her head. The smile was immediate — not on the mouth, which barely moved, but in the eyes, in the way her whole face reorganized itself around the joy of seeing her younger daughter. She wiped her hands on the apron. Stood slowly, with the care of someone who negotiates every movement with her body, and opened her arms.

Tzuyu hugged her. With the delicacy she used for her most fragile patients and the strength she used for the most important person in her life. Miyoung touched her face. Checked her eyes, her color, the dark circles — a mother running diagnostics with the tools she had left.

Her hands moved. Quick, fluid, with the eloquence of someone who has turned silence into a complete language.

Tzuyu answered in the same language. Both their hands conversing in a space that needed no sound. Chaeyoung caught fragments — _fine, yes, we brought, you look better_ — like someone listening to a song in a language they'd studied for two semesters and abandoned.

Then Miyoung looked toward the horses. She saw Chaeyoung.

The smile changed. It didn't widen — it deepened. The smile Miyoung had for Tzuyu was relief; the one she had for Chaeyoung was something more complex. Relief, yes, but also assessment, worry, and that note of ache mothers carry when they see the daughter who looks too much like the father who's no longer there.

Chaeyoung dismounted. The shoulder protested — a sharp note that traveled down her entire arm, which she absorbed without her face changing, because she'd learned to absorb pain in front of her mother the same way she'd learned to absorb it in front of everyone else: in silence, inside, where no one could use it to worry.

She approached. Knelt beside Miyoung, who was still standing with Tzuyu's help, and took her hands. Her mother's hands — smaller than hers now, thinner, with dirt under the nails and a strength that didn't match her body. The hands that once prepared medicines and were now her only voice.

"Hi, Mom."

Miyoung let go of her hands to answer. The signs were slow, deliberate — not because Miyoung couldn't go faster, but because she knew her older daughter needed time to follow.

Chaeyoung nodded. She understood _I missed you._ She understood _you're thinner._ What came next was faster and Chaeyoung lost it — something about the shoulder, or the arm, or the posture, because Miyoung had the eyes of a mother who'd spent twenty-four years reading her daughter's body and didn't need to be told where it hurt to know.

"I'm fine, Mom."

Miyoung's hands stopped. She looked at her. A look that needed no translation and said, with the clarity of someone who has heard that sentence too many times: _don't lie to me._

Chaeyoung swallowed. "I'm... improving."

Miyoung tilted her head. Not satisfied, but willing to accept the half-truth as progress. Her hands moved again — something Chaeyoung didn't fully catch but that ended in a sign she knew: _who?_

Not _who hurt you._ _Who stitched you._ Because Miyoung was an herbalist and the first thing she wanted to know about a wound was whether someone was treating it well.

"Tzuyu," Chaeyoung said. "Always Tzuyu."

Miyoung looked at Tzuyu. Tzuyu's hands answered something brief, efficient, probably the clinical summary — _shoulder, stitches, ten days, she's being impossible as always._ Miyoung exhaled through her nose with something that in another person would have been a laugh and that in her was the ghost of a laugh, a movement of the shoulders, a silent tremor that Chaeyoung knew the way she knew the smell of the garden.

And then Miyoung looked past Chaeyoung.

Mina was standing by Brisa. She hadn't come closer. She'd stayed three meters back, holding the reins with both hands, with the posture of someone who understands there are moments that don't belong to her and is willing to wait as long as necessary to be given room.

She wore no crown. No court dress or jewels or anything that distinguished her from any young woman in any village. Travel clothes — practical, simple, chosen that morning with a care Chaeyoung had noticed and hadn't commented on. Hair pulled back. Hands on the reins.

Miyoung looked at her.

Chaeyoung watched her mother evaluate Mina with the eyes of someone who has survived a fire, the loss of her voice, years of illness, and has therefore developed the ability to see people with a clarity that healthy people don't need. Miyoung didn't see a princess. Miyoung saw a young woman standing by a horse in her garden looking at her daughter with an expression Miyoung recognized, because it was the same expression Hajoon wore when he looked at her — the expression of someone who doesn't yet know what she feels but feels it anyway, with her whole body, defenseless.

Miyoung's hands moved. Directed at Chaeyoung. A gesture that was a question.

Chaeyoung understood. _Who is she?_

"Mom," Chaeyoung said, and her voice did something strange — it softened and tensed at the same time, like a bowstring before releasing the arrow. "This is Mina."

She didn't say _Your Highness._ Didn't say _the princess._ Didn't say _the person I protect_ or _the reason I have new stitches in my shoulder_ or any of the things that were true but weren't the whole truth.

She said Mina.

And Miyoung, who couldn't speak but listened better than anyone Chaeyoung knew, heard everything the way of saying that name contained.

Mina let go of Brisa's reins. Stepped forward. Walked the three meters with steps that were composure and nervousness in equal measure — the princess who knew how to enter ballrooms full of nobles but had never entered the garden of a small house in a village that appeared on no map.

She stopped in front of Miyoung. And before Chaeyoung could introduce her formally, before Tzuyu could intervene, before anyone could do anything — Mina bowed.

Not the courtesy bow a princess gives a subject out of politeness. A real bow. Deep. The bow of someone who recognizes another person as worthy of respect — not for title, not for rank, but for the simple fact of being the mother of someone who matters.

Miyoung blinked. Looked at Chaeyoung. Looked at Mina. Her hands moved to say something, probably _there's no need,_ but stopped mid-gesture.

Because Mina straightened, looked her in the eyes, and raised her hands.

## Scene 6 — "The Hands That Speak"

Mina's hands moved.

Not clumsily. Not with the hesitation of someone searching for words in a half-learned language. They moved with fluency — the fingers forming signs with the clean precision of someone who has practiced in silence, in private, probably with a book open in her lap and her hands rehearsing against the candlelight in a room in the castle where no one could see her.

What Mina's hands said was: _It's an honor to meet you._

Chaeyoung understood it. She knew that sign. It was one of the few she could recognize without effort because she'd attempted it so many times in front of her mother that the movements had been etched somewhere between muscle memory and guilt.

What she didn't understand — what left her standing in the garden with her mouth half open and her hands fallen at her sides like two useless things — was what came next.

Miyoung responded. Her hands moved fast, with the speed of surprise and emotion, and Mina followed every sign without blinking. And responded. And Miyoung responded to the response. And suddenly the two of them were conversing — the mother's hands and the princess's hands moving with a cadence that had its own rhythm, that had pauses and emphasis and something Chaeyoung could only describe as silent laughter, because at some point Miyoung made the sign that was the sign-language version of a belly laugh and Mina tilted her head with that exhale Chaeyoung knew, and here, in this context, it was different — more open, freer, as if speaking with her hands removed a layer that speaking with her voice didn't.

Chaeyoung stood there.

Standing. In her mother's garden. Watching the two most important people in her life speak a language she barely understood.

The sun was high. The bees hummed among the lavender. The smell of rosemary was so strong it seemed tangible. And Chaeyoung stood like a rock in the middle of a river, with the water flowing past her sides, unable to enter the current.

Mina's hands. The same hands that a week ago had generated a pressure that cracked the wall of a room. The same hands that burned with a heat that had no name. The same hands Chaeyoung had taken in the darkness of that room to bring her back. Now they moved with a delicacy that was almost musical — the fingers forming words Chaeyoung couldn't read but could feel, the same way you feel music without understanding the score.

Miyoung moved toward the garden bench, inviting Mina to sit. Mina followed. They sat. And the conversation continued — slower now, more intimate, the hands closer, the gestures smaller. Miyoung was showing her something in the garden, pointing at a plant, and Mina was asking a question with her hands, and Miyoung was answering with the patience of someone teaching something she loves, and Mina was nodding with the attention of someone learning something that matters.

Chaeyoung caught fragments. _Lavender. Lungs. Before. Hwaren._ Loose words floating in a sea of signs she couldn't follow. It was like hearing a conversation through a wall — the tone came through, the content was lost.

The shame came slowly. Not like a blow but like a tide — rising through her feet, her legs, filling her chest. The shame of not knowing. Of not having learned. Of having had years — years — to sit with her mother and practice and memorize and understand, and having chosen every time the sword, the training, the mission, the duty. There was always a reason not to stay. There was always a monster to kill or a shift to cover or an excuse that sounded like responsibility and tasted like cowardice.

And Mina — Mina, who lived between walls and books and silences — had found the time. Had found a book, or a tutor, or the right pages in the infinite library that was her world, and had learned. Not out of obligation. Not out of duty. She had learned an entire language for which she had no practical need — a language spoken by displaced herbalists and healers from villages that didn't appear on maps and people who had lost their voices in fires that history didn't remember.

Mina had learned a language that was only useful if someday you met someone who spoke it.

And today she met someone who spoke it.

Chaeyoung didn't know if Mina had learned sign language before knowing that her guard's mother was mute. Didn't know if it was coincidence or curiosity or something more deliberate she didn't dare name. It didn't matter. What mattered was the image: Mina on the garden bench, with dirt on her knees from the bow she still hadn't brushed off, speaking with hands that destroyed and healed and now said things Chaeyoung couldn't hear, and her mother responding with the silent joy of someone who had spent years communicating with a daughter who only understood half and who suddenly had before her a person who understood everything.

Miyoung laughed. The laugh she made with her shoulders — the silent tremor, the crinkled eyes, the mouth open in a laugh that had no sound but had everything else. Mina caused it. Something she said with her hands made Miyoung laugh for real, head thrown back, and the image was so unexpected and so perfect that Chaeyoung had to look away.

She didn't look away for long. She couldn't. It was like trying not to look at the sun.

Tzuyu appeared at her side. Chaeyoung hadn't heard her approach, which said more about Chaeyoung's state than about Tzuyu's ability to move silently.

Tzuyu said nothing. She stood beside her older sister, watching the same thing she was watching: Mina and Miyoung on the bench, hands moving, conversation flowing in a language Tzuyu did understand but didn't interrupt. Because Tzuyu knew when things needed space to be what they were.

A few minutes passed. Could have been ten. Could have been an hour. Chaeyoung had lost the ability to measure time at some point between Mina's bow and her mother's laugh.

"What are they saying?" Chaeyoung asked. Her voice came out strange — lower than normal, rougher, as if the words had to pass through something tight to get out.

Tzuyu watched Miyoung's hands for a few seconds. Read. Translated internally.

"Mom's telling her about the garden. About the properties of lavender for sleep." Pause. "And about you."

Chaeyoung tensed. "What about me?"

Tzuyu read a little more. Miyoung's hands moved with the cadence of a story, something longer, more elaborate than the short phrases from before.

"That as a child you ate an entire mint plant because you thought it would make you run faster."

"I was six."

"And that you threw up green for two days."

"Tzuyu."

"It's not me. It's Mom."

Chaeyoung looked at the garden. Mina was making the sound. The exhale. But her shoulders were moving too, the contained tremor of someone who is truly laughing but has too much composure to let it fully out. Her eyes, however, betrayed her: they shone.

And they were looking at Chaeyoung.

Just one second. A second in which Mina's eyes drifted from Miyoung and found Chaeyoung's across the garden, and in that second there was something Chaeyoung recognized because she felt it in the mirror every time she looked at Mina without Mina knowing: tenderness. The specific tenderness of seeing someone in their real context, in the place they come from, surrounded by the things that shaped them, and loving each of those things simply because they shaped that person.

Mina returned to Miyoung. The hands continued.

Chaeyoung kept standing.

And in the place with no name, that place that had been growing for weeks, that didn't fit in the words Chaeyoung knew, that didn't obey the categories of duty and service and protocol, something moved. It didn't break. It didn't explode. It wasn't dramatic or grand or worthy of an epic poem. It was something more like a door opening a little wider. A root finding water. The difference between almost and all the way.

"Chaeyoung," Tzuyu said. Quietly. With the voice she used when she wanted to say something important and didn't want it to sound important.

"What?"

"Are you okay?"

Chaeyoung opened her mouth to say _yes, I'm fine, I'm always fine, it's the only thing I know how to say._

What she said was:

"I don't know."

Tzuyu nodded. Didn't ask more. She put her hand on Chaeyoung's arm, the good one, not the shoulder, and left it there. The touch of someone who has spent her whole life caring and who knows that sometimes healing doesn't require medicines or bandages or jars labeled in precise handwriting. Sometimes it just requires a hand on an arm and permission not to be okay.

On the garden bench, Miyoung was showing Mina how to sign a word. She took Mina's hands in hers and adjusted the position of the fingers — a gesture so natural, so mother-teaching, that Chaeyoung felt something in her throat that wasn't crying but lived in the same neighborhood.

Mina let Miyoung move her fingers. She didn't pull away, didn't tense, didn't activate the composure. She let the hands of a mother who wasn't hers touch her with the familiarity of someone who had already accepted her — not as a princess, not as the person her daughter protected, but as the person who was sitting in her garden learning the names of the plants and laughing at stories about a girl who ate all the mint.

"Tzuyu," Chaeyoung said.

"Yes?"

"You were right."

Tzuyu didn't ask about what. They both knew. About the cloak, about the meet-the-parents, about everything.

"I know," Tzuyu said. And she squeezed her arm once before letting go, and went into the house to prepare Miyoung's jars, leaving Chaeyoung alone in the garden with the sun and the bees and the image of Mina speaking with her mother in the language Chaeyoung didn't know and that was now the only thing she wanted to learn.

## Scene 7 — "What Your Mother Said"

Tzuyu stayed inside with Miyoung. The medicines — the jars she'd brought, the treatment review, the daughter-healer ritual she performed every visit with the meticulousness of someone for whom love and science were the same thing. From the garden, Chaeyoung could see the silhouettes through the window: Tzuyu opening jars, Miyoung seated in the chair by the fireplace, both their hands moving as Tzuyu explained the dosage adjustments. A language within a language — sign language, but also medicine, the daughter who cares and the mother who lets herself be cared for.

Chaeyoung was sitting on the garden bench. The same bench where Mina and Miyoung had spent the last hour. The wood was still warm from the sun.

Mina sat beside her.

Not close. Not far. At the exact distance they'd found on the terrace after weeks of nightly tea — close enough for the silence to be shared rather than solitary, far enough that no one had to explain anything.

For a while they didn't speak. The garden made its own noise: bees, wind through the herbs, the drip of a faucet Chaeyoung had spent three visits promising to fix. In the distance, the sounds of the village — a cart, a dog, children playing. The world kept existing outside of what had just happened. Chaeyoung found it hard to believe.

"Where did you learn?" Chaeyoung asked. The question had taken her the entire time to formulate — not because she didn't know what to ask but because she was afraid of the answer. Afraid Mina would say _for you_ and afraid she wouldn't.

"In the library." Mina said it naturally. As if it were the most obvious thing in the world. "There's a treatise on manual languages on the second shelf of the linguistics section. It's old — from before standardization, so some of the signs are different from what's used now. But the foundation is the same."

"When?"

"A long time ago. Before you arrived." Pause. "I started because it was a language I didn't know. I don't like languages I don't know."

Chaeyoung looked at her. Mina was looking at the garden — the plants, the bees, the dripping faucet. Looking at everything except Chaeyoung, which meant she was carefully choosing what she was going to say next.

"Later," Mina said, "I kept going because I learned that your mother spoke it."

There it was.

Chaeyoung said nothing. She couldn't. The sentence was simple and held a weight measured not in words but in hours — the hours Mina had spent alone in her room, with a book open and her hands rehearsing signs for a conversation she might never have with a woman she might never meet, simply because that woman was the mother of someone who mattered to her.

"Your mother asked me to tell you some things," Mina said. Her voice changed — not in tone but in texture. More careful. As if she were handling something fragile.

Chaeyoung pressed her hands against her knees. "What things?"

Mina turned toward her. Looked at her. And Chaeyoung saw in Mina's eyes something she recognized from the night of the awakening — the same honesty without armor, the same willingness to be present in a difficult moment without hiding behind composure.

"That she knows you lie to her."

The silence that followed had weight. Physical. Like a hand on the chest.

"The letters," Mina continued. "Every letter starts with _I'm fine, don't worry._ Your mother keeps them all. She counts them. She knows exactly how many times you've written that sentence." Pause. Mina chose her words. "She says every _I'm fine_ hurts more than the truth. Because when you write _I'm fine,_ she reads everything you're not saying."

Chaeyoung breathed. The air came in rough, as if someone had put sand in her throat.

"I don't want her to worry," she said. And she said it the way she always said it — as a fact, as a policy, as the line she'd drawn between her life at the castle and the small house in Bryndell where her mother grew herbs and waited for letters that lied with good intentions.

"I know," Mina said. "Your mother knows too. That's why it hurts. Not because you lie to her, but because she understands why you lie. And that's worse."

Chaeyoung didn't respond. Mina continued:

"She says silence worries more than words. That she'd rather know you have new scars than spend her nights imagining the worst. That a mother's imagination is crueler than any truth."

Something in Chaeyoung's chest tightened. Not the shoulder — something deeper, older, harder to stitch with sutures.

"And she says..." Mina stopped. For the first time since she'd started speaking, she hesitated. Not because she didn't know what to say but because she knew exactly what to say and understood what it would cause. "That her greatest fear isn't dying."

Chaeyoung looked at her. Something in her face must have changed because Mina held her gaze with a firmness that was gentleness disguised as determination.

"Her greatest fear is that you die first. Chasing monsters to pay for her medicines."

The sentence landed in the garden of Bryndell with the brutal softness of something that had always been true but no one had said out loud. Chaeyoung felt it arrive — not like a blow but like a crack, slow, opening in a wall she'd spent years building. The wall of _I'm fine._ The wall of _don't worry._ The wall behind which she stored every dangerous mission, every unreported wound, every night she fell asleep thinking _if I die protecting someone at least it will have been worth something_ without considering that on the other side of that thought there was a woman in a garden who couldn't call out her name if she needed her.

"Chaeyoung."

Mina's voice. Close. Closer than it had been a second ago — or maybe the same distance, but with fewer layers between them.

Chaeyoung hadn't realized she was looking at the ground. She raised her eyes. Mina was looking at her with that expression Chaeyoung had learned to identify as the real one — not the composure, not the social mask, not the princess. The person. The one who read stolen books at night and learned languages she didn't need and sat in other people's gardens to translate the words a mother couldn't say to her daughter because her daughter had never learned to listen.

"There's something else," Mina said. And she said it softly, so softly the wind almost carried it away.

"What."

"She asked me to take care of you."

Chaeyoung blinked.

The sentence was impossible. Not because of the content — because of the direction. Chaeyoung was the guard. Chaeyoung was the one who cared. Chaeyoung was the one who stood between Mina and danger, who didn't sleep so Mina could sleep, who bled so Mina wouldn't bleed. That was the structure. That was the order. Chaeyoung took care of Mina. Not the other way around. Never the other way around.

And her mother — her mother, who knew her better than anyone, who knew exactly how she was built and exactly where the cracks were — had asked Mina to take care of her. Not Jihyo, who was her superior. Not Tzuyu, who was her sister. Mina.

Because Miyoung saw what Chaeyoung still wouldn't name. She saw the way Chaeyoung said _Mina_ when she introduced her. She saw the way Chaeyoung watched her from across the garden while the two of them talked. And she saw, with the eyes of someone who once had a man who worried about her in the same way — a man who also ran toward danger and also said _I'm fine_ and one day didn't come back — that the only person who could make her daughter stop destroying herself was the same person her daughter was willing to die for.

The inversion was perfect. And it was terrifying.

"And what did you tell her?" Chaeyoung asked. Her voice came out scraped. Not broken — scraped. Like metal against stone.

Mina didn't hesitate. "Yes."

Silence. The bees. The faucet. The world.

Something in Chaeyoung loosened. It didn't resolve — things that have been clenched for years don't resolve in a garden one afternoon. They didn't heal. But they loosened. A millimeter. A degree. Enough for air to enter a space that had been compressed since Hwaren, since the fire, since the first time she wrote _I'm fine_ knowing it was a lie.

Mina didn't touch her. Didn't take her hand, didn't put a hand on her arm the way Tzuyu had, didn't make any of the gestures people make when someone needs comfort. What she did was stay. Sitting on the bench, at the exact distance, looking at the garden, breathing the same air, asking for nothing and offering nothing but her presence.

And her presence was enough. It had been since the terrace. Maybe since before.

"I'm bad at things that aren't swords," Chaeyoung said. Not as an apology. As fact. As the naked truth of someone who can kill a basilisk but can't tell her mother _I miss you_ in the language her mother speaks.

"Then I'll teach you."

Chaeyoung looked at her.

"Sign language," Mina said. "After tea. If you want."

Simple. No extra weight. Without the burden of a debt or a favor or the gravity of everything they'd just said to each other. An offer that said: _I have something you need and I want to give it to you._ Not because it was her duty or her obligation or part of any agreement between princesses and guards. Because she wanted to.

Because she was Mina.

Chaeyoung looked at the garden. Her mother's herbs. The small house. The village that appeared on no map. And beside her, sitting on the bench with dirt on her knees and her hands still in her lap, the hands that destroyed and spoke and now offered to teach, the person who made the place with no name grow until it couldn't fit inside any silence.

"I'm a bad student."

"I very much doubt that."

The almost-smile. Not the courtesy one or the composure one. The real one. The one that tugged at one side of the mouth and didn't quite become a full smile but was more honest than most laughs. The one Mina looked for. The one Mina caused.

"After tea tjhen…" Chaeyoung said.

"After tea."

They stayed on the garden bench afterward, with the sun lowering and the bees and the dripping faucet, not touching and not needing to touch, because what they were building didn't need contact to be real. It just needed this: two people on a bench, one who had learned a language to be able to speak with the other's mother, and the other who was learning that letting someone take care of you isn't weakness.

It's the hardest thing she's ever done.

And it's only just beginning.

PARTE DONDE TZUYU HABLA CON SU MAMA A DENTRO DE LA CASA

Tzuyu called them from the door. "Mom says you're staying for lunch or don't bother coming back."

It wasn't an invitation. It was a sentence.

The table at Miyoung's was small, four chairs, one of which had a leg shorter than the rest, which Chaeyoung stabilized with a folded piece of cloth with the familiarity of someone who'd done it a hundred times. The food was simple: bread, cured cheese, herb soup that smelled of rosemary and something sweeter Chaeyoung couldn't identify, fruit from the neighbor's orchard that Miyoung traded for arthritis salves. It wasn't a banquet. It was what there was. And Miyoung set it on the table with the dignity of someone who doesn't apologize for what she can offer.

Mina ate without protocol. Without the posture of the royal table, without the silverware at the correct angle, without any of the layers the castle placed between a person and her food. She broke the bread with her hands. Blew on the soup. Said something to Miyoung in sign language that made the woman tremble with that silent shoulder-laugh, and Chaeyoung caught the final sign — something about _delicious_ — but missed the rest.

They ate. Tzuyu talked with Miyoung about the treatment adjustments — the moonpetal dosage, the frequency of the ironbark, things Chaeyoung listened to as comforting background noise because they meant someone competent was caring for her mother. Chaeyoung ate slowly, with the distracted hunger of someone who has too many things in her head to pay attention to her stomach.

And then Miyoung set down the spoon. Wiped her hands on the apron. Looked at Mina with the expression of someone who has been patient for an entire hour and has decided patience is over.

Her hands moved. Slowly. Deliberately. A question.

Mina answered. Chaeyoung caught _castle_ and _work_ but the rest was too fast.

"What did she say?" Chaeyoung asked.

"Your mother wants to know what I do," Mina said, with the ease of someone answering a perfectly normal question. "I told her I work at the castle."

"You work at the— Mina, you don't work at the castle."

Mina looked at her. A look that said _keep talking and we're going to have a problem_ with a politeness only twenty-one years of royal etiquette could produce. Chaeyoung closed her mouth.

Miyoung hadn't noticed the exchange — or had noticed it and filed it away, because her hands were already moving again. Another question. More specific.

Mina answered. Chaeyoung saw the signs for _library_ and _books_ and something that could have been _study._ Mina was saying she worked with books. Which was technically true. In the same way that saying the ocean is a big puddle is technically true.

Miyoung nodded, satisfied. A young woman who worked with books. That explained the careful hands, the way she sat, the sign vocabulary broader than most people's. Miyoung evaluated her with new eyes — not as the stranger her daughter had brought home but as the book girl. The intellectual. The one who was probably too smart for Chaeyoung and yet was sitting in her kitchen eating herb soup.

Miyoung's hands moved again. Faster. With the cadence of someone who has found a thread and is going to pull it until the whole fabric unravels.

Mina answered. And for the first time, Chaeyoung saw something on Mina's face she had never seen: color. In the cheeks. A subtle but unmistakable blush rising from the neck that Mina tried to hide by tilting her head over the soup.

Chaeyoung looked at Tzuyu. Tzuyu was breaking bread with a concentration that suggested the piece of bread contained the secrets of the universe.

"Tzuyu."

"Mm?"

"What's Mom asking?"

Tzuyu chewed. Swallowed. Took her time. When she spoke, her voice had the clinical neutrality she used for giving diagnoses she knew weren't going to go over well.

"She wants to know how you met."

Chaeyoung blinked. "How we...?"

"How you met." Pause. "And whether you eat enough, because she says you're thinner than last time."

Miyoung didn't wait for them to finish arguing. Her hands were already moving again, directed at Mina, with the warm intensity of an interrogation that didn't feel like an interrogation but like kitchen conversation — because for Miyoung it was kitchen conversation. She wasn't interviewing a princess. She was getting to know her. The book girl her daughter brought home. The girl who spoke her language. The girl who had bowed in her garden.

Mina answered. She told her — Chaeyoung pieced together the puzzle from the fragments she caught and Tzuyu's involuntary translations — that they met when Chaeyoung was assigned as guard. That they'd been together for weeks. That Chaeyoung accompanied her during the day and had a room next to hers.

This last part made Miyoung raise both eyebrows. A gesture that needed no translation in any language.

"It's an antechamber," Chaeyoung said, even though no one was talking to her. "It's standard. All personal guards have one."

Miyoung looked at her. Looked at Mina. Back to Chaeyoung. The eyebrow was still up.

"It's protocol, Mom."

Miyoung made a gesture with her hand that Chaeyoung recognized: _I wasn't asking you._ And turned back to Mina.

The sign conversation took on a rhythm Chaeyoung couldn't follow but could feel — the rhythm of a mother extracting information. Miyoung asked with the efficiency of someone who has raised two daughters and knows that the important answers aren't in the direct questions but in the surrounding ones. She didn't ask _what do you feel for my daughter_ — probably. Instead she asked about the day-to-day: what they did, what the routine was like, whether Chaeyoung ate well, whether she slept enough, whether she was as stubborn at work as she was in everything else.

Mina answered carefully. Chaeyoung watched her navigate the questions the way she navigated the castle hallways — with grace, with attention to every corner, choosing what to say and what to omit with a precision that was less lying and more editing. Yes, Chaeyoung accompanied her. Yes, they spent time together. No, Chaeyoung didn't eat enough. No, she didn't sleep enough either. Yes, she was exactly that stubborn.

Miyoung nodded with each answer. Filing. Building a portrait of her daughter's life from the eyes of someone who looked at her every day — and who, Miyoung was noticing with each answer, looked at her with an attention that went well beyond the professional.

Chaeyoung caught _terrace_ at some point. And _night._ And something that could have been _tea_ or could have been _together._

"Mina."

"Yes?"

"What are you telling her?"

"That we have tea on the terrace at night." Perfectly composed. Perfectly red. "It's an objective fact."

"You say it like it's a report."

"Your mother asked questions. I'm answering."

"All of them?"

Mina didn't answer that. She took a sip of water.

Miyoung looked at the two of them with an expression that was the adult version of the face Tzuyu had made when she said _it's exactly a meet-the-parents_ — quiet satisfaction, the patience of someone who doesn't need what she already knows confirmed. Then her hands moved again. A shorter question. Direct.

Mina went still. Hands halfway to a response. The blush, which had been oscillating between subtle and noticeable, settled firmly on noticeable.

Tzuyu lowered her eyes to her plate.

"What did she ask?" Chaeyoung said.

"Nothing relevant," Mina said.

"Tzuyu."

"Don't drag me into this."

"You're literally sitting there understanding everything."

"I'm eating."

"Tzuyu."

Tzuyu sighed. Looked at Mina as if apologizing in advance. Looked at Chaeyoung as if asking not to be blamed for what was about to come out of her mouth.

"She asked if Mina has someone waiting for her."

Silence.

"Waiting for her," Chaeyoung repeated. "Waiting how?"

"How do you think, Chaeyoung."

The silence that followed was of a very specific quality — the silence of three young women sitting in the kitchen of an older woman who had just asked, with the subtlety of a battering ram against a door, whether the girl her daughter had brought home was available.

Mina answered. Chaeyoung didn't see the sign because she was staring at the soup with the concentration of someone who had just discovered that soup was the most interesting thing that had ever existed. But she saw her mother's face upon receiving the answer — the way Miyoung's eyes softened, the way the smile shifted from curious to something warmer, gentler, more like permission.

Miyoung nodded. Once. And looked at Chaeyoung with an expression that said, in any language, _and what are you waiting for?_

Chaeyoung focused on the soup.

Miyoung did the shoulder-laugh. A long one. The longest Chaeyoung had seen from her in months — the silent tremor shaking her whole body, crinkling her eyes shut. She wasn't laughing at the question or the answer. She was laughing at the entire spectacle — her older daughter, who could face basilisks without flinching, sitting in her kitchen unable to look at the girl who obviously mattered more to her than anything with a blade. And the girl, red to the ears, answering questions with careful truths that left out exactly the things Miyoung least needed to be told because she could already see them.

The joy on Miyoung's face was so genuine, so free of the shadow that the damaged lungs and the lying letters and the years of silence put over her, that Chaeyoung decided she didn't care about not understanding half of what they'd said if the price was seeing her mother laugh like that.

After the meal, Miyoung rose from the table with her usual care — one hand on the chair back, a second for her lungs to accept the change in position — and went straight to Chaeyoung. She touched her shoulder. The bad one. With the exact pressure of someone who knew where it hurt without needing to be told.

Chaeyoung tensed.

Miyoung's hands moved. Quick, authoritative. _Let me see._

"Mom, I'm—"

Miyoung gave her a look. A single one. The look she'd used when Chaeyoung was six and ate all the mint, and when she was ten and fell off the roof, and when she was fourteen and came back from training with her knuckles raw. The look that said _don't finish that sentence._

Chaeyoung went quiet. She unfastened the collar of her shirt enough for Miyoung to see the shoulder — Tzuyu's stitches, the old scar beneath, the skin still a color that wasn't right.

Miyoung examined it. Not like Tzuyu — not with the clinical objectivity of the healer but with the hands of the herbalist who had treated wounds before Tzuyu was born. She touched around the stitches. Pressed once, gently. Chaeyoung clenched her jaw.

Miyoung's hands turned to Tzuyu. A brief, technical exchange — mother and daughter on their own terrain. Tzuyu nodded: "The stitches are mine, ten days old, and she’s being impossible as always."

"I'm not impossible."

"I just told her you had a sword fight with ten-day-old stitches."

"That's confidential medical information."

"It's mother information. It has no security classification."

Miyoung let go of the shoulder. She went to the kitchen shelf, slowly, with short steps, and came back with a small jar. Salve. Old. Handmade, with the unmistakable smell of things Miyoung prepared: earth, herbs, something bitter underneath. She placed it in Chaeyoung's hand and closed the fingers around it with the firmness of someone who doesn't accept returns. Her hands said something Chaeyoung understood without needing translation: _twice a day, no excuses._

"Thanks, Mom."

Miyoung touched her face. Her cheek. A gesture so brief and so full that Chaeyoung had to blink more than normal.

Then Miyoung turned to Mina, who had watched the entire examination from the table with her hands in her lap and something on her face that wasn't composure — it was the expression of someone who has just watched the strongest person she knows go still and silent while her mother touches her cheek. And who understands, with a clarity that hurts, where the way Chaeyoung cares for others comes from: from here. From this kitchen. From these hands.

Miyoung's hands moved one last time. Directed at Mina. A slow, deliberate sign, without the speed of the interrogation or the lightness of the garden conversation. Something that had weight. Something that was a charge.

Mina nodded. Once. Slowly. With the gravity of someone accepting something that had nothing to do with titles or crowns or any of the things Miyoung didn't know about her — and everything to do with the things Miyoung did know, which were the only ones that mattered.

Chaeyoung didn't ask what she'd said. She already knew.

_Take care of her._

## Scene 8 — "The Road Back"

The farewell was brief. Not because there weren't things to say but because Miyoung and Chaeyoung always said goodbye the same way — a hug that lasted exactly as long as Miyoung's lungs allowed, the mother's hands moving to say _come back soon,_ and Chaeyoung nodding with the promise they both knew was more wish than certainty.

This time there was something different. Miyoung hugged Mina.

It wasn't a formal embrace or a courtesy farewell. It was the hug of a mother who has just adopted someone — not with papers or ceremony but with the silent authority of a woman who has decided that the person in front of her now belongs to her, the same way her daughters belong to her and her garden and the small house that leans to the left. Mina went still for a second — half a second — and then returned the hug with care, with the precision of someone unaccustomed to being touched like that and not wanting to break it.

Miyoung signed something to her as they separated. Something short. Mina nodded. She didn't translate. Chaeyoung didn't ask.

Tzuyu said goodbye last. A kiss on Miyoung's forehead — a gesture that was both the daughter and the healer saying _until next time, drink the tea, don't overdo it._ Miyoung stroked her hair with the expression of someone who knows her daughters are leaving but has learned to let them go with open fingers instead of closed ones.

She stood at the garden gate watching them mount. Chaeyoung turned once in the saddle to look at her. Miyoung raised her hand. Not as a farewell — as a blessing.

---

They rode.

The road back was the same as the way there but didn't feel the same. The sun was going down — a golden line on the horizon turning the fields to copper and the trees to silhouettes. The shadows stretched. The air cooled. Everything had the tone of things that end and leave a taste that's neither sad nor happy but something in between — the taste of a day that was good and that's storing itself in memory before it's finished passing.

Chaeyoung rode in silence.

Not the uncomfortable silence or the professional silence. The silence of someone who has too many things inside and needs time to sort them before they overflow. Miyoung. The letters. The _I'm fine_ that was a lie. Her mother's fear. The request. Mina's hands speaking with her mother's hands. The interrogation. The question they didn't translate for her. The salve in the saddlebag. The cheek where her mother touched her.

Mina rode at her side. In silence too. But her silence was different — it was the silence of someone who knew the person beside her needed space and was willing to give it without being asked. Mina understood silence as language because it was the language she'd lived in for twenty-one years. She knew that sometimes the most generous thing you can do for someone is not fill the void.

The guards followed at a distance. Hooves against earth. The clink of the empty jars in Tzuyu's saddlebags, lighter now that the medicines had stayed in Bryndell.

They crossed the forest. The light fragmented between the branches the way it had on the way there, but now the angles were different — more horizontal, more orange, with that quality sunset light has of making everything seem softer and older and more true.

Tzuyu brought her mare alongside Cricket. She rode beside Chaeyoung for a while without speaking — the younger sister syncing her pace with the older, a gesture as natural as breathing that meant _I'm here if you need me and if you don't need me I'm still here._

"She has pretty hands," Tzuyu said.

Chaeyoung looked at her. "What?"

"Mina. She has pretty hands for sign language. Long fingers. Good angles." Pause. The Tzuyu pause that always preceded something that was going to cost. "For other things too, I imagine."

Chaeyoung punched her in the shoulder. Soft — not because she wanted to be soft but because the bad shoulder wouldn't allow anything else. Tzuyu didn't flinch. Neither did her mare.

"I was referring to playing instruments," Tzuyu said, with the immaculate innocence of someone who was not referring to playing instruments.

"Sure you were."

"What did you think I meant?"

"Tzuyu."

"It's a clinical observation. I'm a healer. I notice hands."

"I notice you're about to fall off your horse."

"You haven't knocked me off a horse since we were twelve."

"I can make an exception."

Tzuyu smiled. The small smile, the one Chaeyoung rarely saw and that always meant the same thing: _I love you, you're an idiot, the two things are not mutually exclusive._ Then she let her mare fall back again, returning to her position behind them, and the silence returned.

But it was a different silence now. Lighter. As if the punch on the shoulder and the joke about hands had opened a valve that released enough pressure for what remained to be bearable.

---

They came out of the forest. The hill. The valley in reverse — now the castle was ahead, not Bryndell. The towers visible against the darkening sky, the walls that from here looked like what they were: walls. Walls that contained and protected and imprisoned, depending on who was looking at them.

Mina looked at them.

Chaeyoung watched her look. And saw the exact moment something in Mina's face changed — not sadness, not resignation, but adjustment. The recalibration of someone who has breathed air without walls for an entire day and now sees the walls approaching and knows she has to fit inside them again.

"Your Highness?"

Mina looked at her. Something about the formality of the title — the _Your Highness_ that Chaeyoung used as armor and that here, after Bryndell, after her mother's kitchen, after just _Mina_ in front of Miyoung, sounded more hollow than ever — made Mina tilt her head.

"Not today," Mina said. Quietly. "Today my name is Mina."

Chaeyoung looked at her. The castle behind. The sky above. The day ending.

"Mina," she said. Just that. The name with nothing around it. And it sounded the way it had sounded in the shattered room — like something that was true before the mouth said it.

Mina nodded. Once. And touched Brisa's flanks, and the horse moved forward, and they began the ride down the hill toward the walls.

---

Jeongyeon was in the stables.

Of course she was in the stables. Jeongyeon was always in the stables — it was her kingdom, her territory, the place where she was more herself than anywhere else in the castle. She was brushing a chestnut horse when she saw them arrive, and something in her face rearranged: first the professional evaluation (the horses, the state of the tack, whether Brisa was limping, whether Cricket was lathered), and then the personal evaluation, which was faster and more precise.

She looked at Mina. Looked at Chaeyoung. Raised an eyebrow.

Chaeyoung dismounted without returning the look. Handed over Cricket's reins with the automatism of someone who's made that gesture a thousand times. Jeongyeon took them, stroked Cricket's neck, and waited.

"Good trip?" she asked, with the neutrality of someone asking something entirely different.

"Yes."

"Your mother doing all right?"

"She's fine."

"Mina?"

The name without a title. Said with the ease of someone who has neither time nor patience for protocol and calls people by their name because that's what they're called. Chaeyoung looked at her.

"She's fine."

Jeongyeon nodded. The eyebrow was still raised. It was an eyebrow that contained multitudes — the same eyebrow she'd raised the first time she saw Chaeyoung talk about Mina with a tone of voice she didn't use for anything else, the eyebrow that was an entire question disguised as a facial expression.

Mina passed through the stables on her way to the castle. She stopped. Gave Jeongyeon a nod — not the bow of a princess but the greeting of someone who recognizes another's territory.

"Thank you for Brisa," Mina said. "She's an extraordinary mare."

Jeongyeon looked at her. Something in her face softened — not much, because Jeongyeon wasn't one to soften in public, but enough for Chaeyoung to notice. "You chose well. Brisa has a good eye for her rider."

Mina nodded. Left. Tzuyu followed, with the empty saddlebags and the expression of someone who's had a long, good day and is thinking about a bed.

The stables fell quiet. Just Chaeyoung and Jeongyeon and the horses.

"So," Jeongyeon said, putting away the brush with a slowness that was pure theater, "you took her to meet your mother."

"She asked to go."

"Uh-huh."

"It's the truth."

"I didn't say it wasn't the truth. I said uh-huh." Jeongyeon patted Cricket. "Did Miyoung approve?"

Chaeyoung didn't answer. She didn't need to. The absence of an answer was the answer, and Jeongyeon read it with the fluency of someone who'd spent years reading Chaeyoung's silences like newspaper headlines.

"Good," Jeongyeon said. And she smiled. A smile that was small and that held years of friendship and a single unspoken word that sounded a lot like _finally._ "Go on. You're going to be late for tea."

Chaeyoung left the stables. The castle received her with the smell of stone and torches that was her normal. The walls closed behind her. The day ended.

But what she brought back from Bryndell — the salve, her mother's words, the image of Mina speaking with her hands, the _today my name is Mina,_ the place with no name that was no longer a place but an entire landscape — that didn't stay outside the walls. That came in with her. And it wasn't going to leave.

## Scene 9 — "The First Lesson"

The terrace. The candle. The tea.

The ritual had formed without either of them naming it — the way all important things form between people who don't know how to talk about the important things. It started as coincidence (both awake, both in the same space) and became habit (the tea Mina prepared, the chair Chaeyoung never used because she preferred the floor with her back against the wall) and now it was something more than habit. It was the place where days ended. Where the layers loosened. Where Chaeyoung stopped being a guard and Mina stopped being a princess and what remained was two people who felt more real with each other than with anyone else in the world.

Tonight the terrace felt different. Not physically — the same stone columns, the same view of the kingdom in the dark, the same sky without a ceiling that now, after Bryndell, held new meaning. It felt different because the two of them were different. Bryndell had changed them in ways that still hadn't finished settling, like earth after rain — wet, soft, taking a new shape.

Mina poured the tea. Chaeyoung received it with both hands, as always. Their fingers brushed in the transfer of the cup, as always. The difference was that this time neither of them pretended it hadn't happened.

"Ready?" Mina said.

"No."

"Good. Let's begin."

Mina sat on the floor. Not in the chair, not on the stone ledge — on the floor, across from Chaeyoung, legs crossed, hands on her knees. At Chaeyoung's level. In her territory. The gesture was so deliberate and so Mina that Chaeyoung felt something loosen in her chest before the lesson even started.

"The alphabet first," Mina said. "You already know most of it. We're going to clean up what you know and fill in what's missing."

She raised her right hand. Formed the first letter. Slowly. With the precision of someone who knows that teaching is an act of patience and that patience isn't waiting but being present in every second of the wait.

Chaeyoung copied the sign. Good. The second letter. Good. The third — the fingers jammed. The position wasn't natural; it required a flex of the pinky that Chaeyoung's hand, accustomed to closing around sword grips, resisted making.

"Pinky further in," Mina said.

"It is in."

"It's halfway. Look." Mina repeated the sign. The fingers moving with that fluency that was what Tzuyu had called _pretty hands_ and what Chaeyoung was internally calling, with a vocabulary she had no intention of sharing with anyone.

Chaeyoung tried again. The pinky half-cooperated.

"Better," Mina said. "Again."

"How many times?"

"As many as it takes."

"That sounds like a lot."

"Sign language is a language, Chaeyoung. You don't learn it in one night."

"I was hoping you'd say I'm a natural prodigy and in twenty minutes I'll be giving speeches."

"If that's what you need to hear to keep practicing, you're a natural prodigy."

"That doesn't sound sincere anymore."

"It's not. Again. The pinky."

Chaeyoung repeated. The pinky yielded. The letter formed — not with Mina's elegance or Miyoung's speed, but correct. Recognizable. Functional.

"Good," Mina said, and the word held a weight that didn't correspond to a single letter of the alphabet.

They went through the letters. Chaeyoung formed them with the concentration she normally reserved for studying combat formations — the furrowed brow, the pressed lips, the intensity of someone who treats everything as if it were life or death because that's how she treats everything. Mina corrected her calmly. "Thumb higher." "Fingers together, not apart." "That's G, not J. The difference is the twist."

"The twist?"

"Of the wrist."

"How much twist?"

"A quarter. Not half. Half is a different letter."

"Which letter?"

"One you don't need yet. Focus."

Chaeyoung focused. The G came out as G. They moved to greetings — _hello, good night, thank you, please._ Chaeyoung knew the basics; Mina polished the edges. "The movement starts from the chin, not the mouth." "Slower. Speed comes later."

And then they reached the phrases.

"Let's try something longer," Mina said. "Repeat after me."

Mina's hands formed a phrase. Slowly. Each sign clear, isolated, like words written with space between them so Chaeyoung could read them.

Chaeyoung repeated. Her hands moved — clumsy where Mina's were fluid, rigid where Mina's were soft, but moving. Trying.

"Almost," Mina said. "The second sign is inverted. Like this."

Mina made the sign again. Chaeyoung tried. The fingers couldn't find the position.

"No," Mina said. "It's— look, the index finger has to..." She stopped. Assessed. And then did something they'd both been orbiting all night like planets around a star.

She took Chaeyoung's hand.

Not the way she'd taken the tea cup. Not like an accidental brush during the transfer of an object. She took it with intention — Mina's fingers wrapping around Chaeyoung's, guiding them into the correct position with the same delicacy she used to turn the pages of her books. Mina's thumb pressed the back of Chaeyoung's hand to rotate it. Mina's index finger adjusted Chaeyoung's pinky. The contact was technical. Necessary. The most legitimate excuse in the world.

And it was absolutely unbearable.

Chaeyoung stopped breathing. Not dramatically — it wasn't a gasp or a visible hold. Simply, the air stopped entering and leaving for the three seconds Mina's fingers were on hers, because everything her body could process in that moment was the point of contact. Mina's hands. The hands that spoke and destroyed and now taught. The hands that had spoken with her mother in the garden of Bryndell. The hands Miyoung had taken to adjust the fingers the same way Mina was now adjusting Chaeyoung's — and the symmetry of that, the circularity, the way the hands repeated like a musical theme, was almost too much.

"There," Mina said. She let go. "Try it."

Chaeyoung tried. The sign came out correct. She didn't remember making it.

"Good," Mina said. "Next."

They continued. Their hands touched again — because they had to touch, because that's how you teach a manual language, because fingers need guidance and guidance requires contact. Every time Mina took her hand to correct her, Chaeyoung lost half a second of consciousness and gained a memory that was going to last longer than most things she'd lived.

Mina knew. Chaeyoung knew that Mina knew. And Mina knew that Chaeyoung knew that Mina knew. And neither of them said anything, because saying something would have broken the excuse, and the excuse was the only thing allowing them to be like this — on the terrace floor, with their knees almost touching and their hands meeting every few minutes and the tea going cold because neither remembered to drink it.

"I want to teach you a specific phrase," Mina said, after a while.

"Which one?"

Mina looked at her. In the candlelight, her eyes had that golden tone Chaeyoung had learned to search for and never found anywhere else. The candle flickered — not from the breeze, which there wasn't, but from the other thing, the thing that lived in Mina's palms and that here, on the terrace, in the night, with her hands millimeters from Chaeyoung's, stirred gently like something responding to closeness.

"I'm fine," Mina said. And her hands formed the signs.

Chaeyoung looked at her. The phrase that began every letter. The phrase her mother read as a lie. The phrase that was the only thing she knew how to say and the only thing that was never true.

"Repeat," Mina said.

Chaeyoung raised her hands. Formed them. Slowly. Carefully. With the same attention she gave to cleaning her sword or checking the straps of her armor — the attention reserved for things that matter.

_I'm fine._

Mina watched her hands. Nodded. "Correct. The position is correct."

Silence. The candle. The stars.

"Now," Mina said, and her voice was softer than before, lower, with the weight of something that had been waiting to be said, "when you say it to your mother, make it true."

The sentence landed on the terrace like a seed in soft earth. No visible impact. No sound. But with roots already reaching down.

Chaeyoung looked at her. Mina looked back. And between them — in the space that was the candle and the cold tea and the hands that still held the memory of contact — there was something that wasn't a promise because promises are made out loud and this was quieter than that. It was an intention. A direction. The first step of a road that was going to be long and difficult and that Chaeyoung wouldn't have traveled for anyone else.

"Another day," Chaeyoung said.

The phrase from always. The _another day_ that had started as evasion in Chapter 3 and softened in Chapter 6 and that now, here, on the terrace after Bryndell, sounded like something completely different. Not evasion. Not postponement. A promise. A real promise, made with the voice of someone who is learning that promises don't require swords or oaths or blood — sometimes they only require two words on a terrace without a ceiling.

Mina heard it. Measured it. Accepted it.

"Another day," Mina said. As confirmation. As a pact.

They stayed on the terrace. The cold tea. The candle flickering with the pulse of something that wasn't wind. The stars that were the same ones that had been there the first night, when Chaeyoung met Mina on this very terrace and the world was simpler and hands were just hands and the place with no name didn't exist yet.

After a while, Mina said:

"Sana's birthday is next week."

The change of subject was so abrupt that Chaeyoung needed a second to readjust. "I know. Nayeon's been organizing something for days."

"Something is an understatement. I think she's planning an event the size of a coronation." Mina paused. "Sana wants it to be intimate. Nayeon wants it to be memorable. They've been negotiating for three days."

"Who's winning?"

"Sana. Sana always wins. Nayeon just doesn't know it yet."

Chaeyoung made a sound that was almost a laugh. The sound surprised them both.

"You should do that more often," Mina said. Quietly. As if she were saying it to herself and it slipped out.

Chaeyoung didn't respond. She didn't know how to respond to something that was so simple and so enormous at the same time. So she picked up the cold tea and took a sip and let the night cover them both, and the candle kept flickering, and Chaeyoung's hands formed the first letter of the alphabet once more in the dark — alone, without Mina seeing, practicing in silence the way Mina had once practiced in a room in the castle with a book open and her hands rehearsing a language she didn't yet need.

The antechamber door was left ajar that night.

As always.

But the space between almost and all the way was a little smaller than before.
