## Scene 1 — "The Last Thread"

Momo arrived at Jihyo's office before dawn, which was notable for two reasons: first, that Momo was rarely awake before dawn by choice; second, that she arrived without food.

Jihyo noticed that before anything else. Momo without something in her hand, an apple, a piece of bread, a cookie of dubious origin, was like a sky without stars. Technically possible, but an unmistakable sign that something had changed in the natural order of the world.

"Sit down," said Jihyo.

Momo sat. Backwards in the chair, as always, with her arms crossed over the backrest and her chin resting on her forearms. But there was no restlessness in her body this time. Not the usual rocking, not the eyes wandering around the room looking for something interesting to stare at. Momo was still. Focused. The version of herself that only appeared when something truly mattered.

"I followed him," said Momo. "Last night. After we talked."

Jihyo leaned forward. The office was dim, the only light a candle that had been burning for hours and the first gray of dawn filtering through the narrow window. On the desk, the castle map with the lines she'd traced during sleepless nights. The routes, the schedules, the patterns. The threads that touched but didn't knot.

"He went to the south wall," Momo continued. "After the second shift change. Not through the main corridor. He took the service route, the one the servants use to carry firewood. Nobody patrols there between two and four because the south wall shift covers the outer perimeter, not the internal access points."

Jihyo didn't interrupt. Momo speaking about routes and shifts with that precision wasn't something you interrupted. It was something you listened to with the same attention you give to the creak of wood before the floor gives way.

"There's a loose stone. In the inner wall, at the height of the third buttress." Momo uncrossed one arm and pulled something from inside her uniform. She placed it on the desk with more care than Jihyo had ever seen her use with any object that wasn't food. "He took something out of it. A small scroll, wrapped in waxed cloth. He read it by the torchlight in the corridor. And then he burned it."

Jihyo looked at what Momo had placed on the table. A stone. Fist-sized, gray, with edges that didn't correspond to natural weathering but to tools. Someone had shaped it to fit into the hole and come out with ease. Careful, patient work. The work of someone who'd been using it for a long time.

"I couldn't recover the message," said Momo. "But I went back to the hole after he left."

Jihyo picked up the stone. Turned it under the candlelight. On the inner face, the one that faced the hole, there were residues. Melted wax in the crevices. Dark stains that could be ink. And something else: wear marks from repeated use. This wasn't the first time someone had left a message there. Or the second. Or the tenth.

"A dead drop," said Jihyo.

"That's what I thought."

"How long do you think he's been using it?"

Momo considered the question with the seriousness of someone being asked something fundamental. "The stone is worn. Not by weather. By fingers. If he takes it out and puts it back once a week..." She did the math with her lips moving silently. "Months? Since before the attack. Maybe since before Chaeyoung arrived."

Jihyo set the stone on the map. Exactly on the point where the south wall connected with the internal access points of the east wing. The piece fit with the precision of something that had always belonged there.

Three threads.

The first: the lot mark on the knife. A specific commission, military production, paid in cash. The blacksmith only worked with officers ranked knight or above. Callen was a knight.

The second: the movements. Callen in the east wing corridor three times in two days. Callen passing through the cell area just after the shift change. Callen mapping the guard schedules with the patience of someone who plans, not someone who strolls.

The third: the dead drop. Active communication between Callen and someone outside the castle. A channel that had been operating for months. Messages received, read, and burned. Information flowing in and out of the castle through a loose stone in a wall that nobody guarded from the inside.

The three threads touched. And now, for the first time, they knotted.

"There's something else," said Momo.

Jihyo looked at her.

"On my way back, I saw General."

"The cat?"

"General was in the east wing corridor. Meowing at a door. Sir Bryce's quarters."

"The squire?"

"No. Sir Bryce moved last month. Now two squires sleep there. New ones. They arrived with the autumn rotation." Momo paused. "I've seen them with Callen. Never alone. Always in pairs."

Jihyo felt something cold settle in her stomach. Not surprise. Confirmation. The kind of cold you feel when a suspicion stops being a suspicion and becomes certainty.

"How many more?"

"Two more. Also arrived with the autumn rotation. They're assigned to the south wing, but I've seen them in the east wing more than they should be." Momo scratched the back of her neck. "I don't like them."

"Why?"

"Because they walk like you."

Jihyo blinked. "Like me?"

"With purpose. Like every step is part of a plan. Normal people don't walk like that. You do. Chaeyoung sometimes. Them, always." Momo looked at her with that clarity that appeared in her eyes like lightning between clouds. "People who always walk with purpose are doing something they don't want you to notice."

The silence that followed was the densest the office had held in five days.

Four. Not one. Four internal allies, besides Callen. All arrived with the autumn rotation, all in discreet positions, all operating under the radar while Jihyo searched for external infiltrators.

They hadn't gotten in. They were already inside.

"Names?"

Momo gave them. Four names that Jihyo wrote down on a scrap of parchment in the small, precise handwriting she used for the things that mattered. Four names she would cross-reference with the autumn rotation records, with the post assignments, with the shifts each one covered.

But she already knew what she was going to find. She knew it with the certainty that comes from convergence, when the lines on the map stop being lines and become an arrow pointing to a single place.

"Good work, Momo."

Momo didn't smile this time. What she did was nod, once, with something that resembled gravity. The Momo who ate stolen apples and named cats was still there, somewhere beneath the surface, but this morning the one sitting across from Jihyo was the other Momo. The one who saw patterns no one else saw and walked without getting lost when the path mattered.

"Get some rest," said Jihyo. "It's going to be a long day."

Momo stood up. At the door she stopped.

"Captain."

"Yes?"

"They're dangerous. All four. They walk like us but they're not like us." Another pause. "The ones who run are a nuisance. The ones who believe in something are the ones who are scary."

And she left. Without a sound. Without bumping into anything.

Jihyo was left alone with the map and the stone and the four names and the dawn coming through the window as if this were any other day.

It wasn't.

She stood up. Put on her cloak. Took the parchment with the names and folded it twice. She tucked it into the inner pocket, against her chest, where she'd feel it with every breath.

The corridor toward the royal wing was empty at this hour. The servants didn't begin their rounds until the sun touched the stained glass of the main corridor, and the night shift guards walked with the slowest pace of the early morning, the pace of those who know their relief is near. Jihyo passed them without stopping. It wasn't discourtesy. It was contained urgency, the kind of hurry that doesn't show in the feet but in the jaw.

Nayeon would receive her. At any hour, for any reason, if Jihyo appeared outside of schedule it meant the reason justified the interruption. That was part of the tacit agreement between them, forged over four years of captaincy and the kind of trust that isn't built with words but with results.

The door servant looked at her with contained surprise. Jihyo gave him the message in three words: "The Captain. Urgent."

She waited. Not in the garden this time. Not in the corridor. In the antechamber of the private audience hall, standing, her back against the wall and Momo's stone weighing in her pocket like something alive.

She didn't have to wait long. The door opened and the servant gestured her in.

Nayeon was standing by the window. Dressed, hair done, with a cup of tea that Jihyo suspected was there more out of habit than thirst. At five in the morning, the Crown Princess of Twiceland was already functioning. Jihyo had never known whether that was discipline, insomnia, or the inability to let the kingdom exist without supervision during sleeping hours.

"Report," said Nayeon. Without turning. Without preamble.

"I have a name." Jihyo took the parchment from her pocket. "And four followers."

Nayeon turned.

Jihyo told her everything. The dead drop in the south wall, the shaped stone, the months of communication. The four names of the infiltrated knights, arrived with the autumn rotation, positioned at strategic points in the castle. Callen as the central axis, the link between the external order and the internal pieces.

Nayeon listened without interrupting. Her face didn't change during the report. Half lit by the candle, half in shadow. The face of someone who processes information the same way others breathe: with no visible effort, with absolute efficiency.

"Five traitors," said Nayeon when Jihyo finished. It wasn't a question. It was the weight of the phrase, spoken aloud to give it reality.

"Five that we've identified. I can't rule out more."

"Do you think there are more?"

"I think these five are the operatives. Callen leads, the four execute. If there are others, they're passive. Sympathizers, not agents." Jihyo chose her next words carefully. "But there's something that worries me more than the numbers."

"The message."

"The message. Callen read it and burned it last night. If he received instructions, there may be a planned operation. We don't know when."

"Today?"

"I don't know. But we can't wait to find out."

Nayeon set the cup on the table. The sound of porcelain against wood was small, precise, final. The sound of a decision being made.

"What's your plan?"

"Move tonight. Before they do. Detain Callen first, privately, away from the four. Once we have him, the four lose their coordination. We take them one by one."

"Risks?"

"If they find out before we act, they disappear. Or worse: they accelerate whatever they're planning." Jihyo held Nayeon's gaze. "I need everything to look normal today. Regular routines, guards at their posts, nothing to alert them. Tonight, when the castle is quiet, we move."

Nayeon looked at her a moment longer. Evaluating. Not the information, Jihyo. Measuring whether the captain standing before her was building on rock or on sand.

"Do it."

Two words. Enough.

"And Jihyo."

"Yes?"

"End this."

It wasn't an order. Orders have protocols, signatures, chains of command. This was something older. An heir telling the person she trusted most that the castle they both served had a disease, and that the surgery couldn't wait.

Jihyo nodded. Once. She left.

The corridor received her with the dawn light now pouring fully through the stained glass, projecting colors onto the stone. Reds, golds, blues. The castle waking with the innocence of something that doesn't know what's coming.

_Tonight_, thought Jihyo. _Tonight it ends._

She walked toward her office with the parchment against her chest and five names beating beneath the fabric like a second heart. The plan was solid. The pieces were in place. The variables were manageable.

What Jihyo didn't know, what she couldn't know, was that the five names on her parchment had plans of their own. And that those plans wouldn't wait for nightfall.

## Scene 2 — "Before the Silence"

Mina had dark circles.

Not the subtle kind that hide behind posture and good lighting, the kind royal composure had been designed to conceal. These were honest. Dark. The dark circles of someone who had set out to sleep at a reasonable hour, had opened a book to read "just one more chapter," and had emerged four hours later with the candle reduced to a pool of wax and the certainty that the world was bigger and more terrifying than she'd thought.

Chaeyoung saw it the moment the door opened.

The scan of every morning; face, hands, posture, look for what changed since last night, had become something she did before thinking, like breathing or reaching for the sword upon waking. But today the scan stopped at the eyes. More specifically, at the shadow beneath them, which told a story Mina probably hadn't intended to narrate.

"Good morning, Your Highness."

"Good morning, Chaeyoung."

The usual exchange. The names with their habitual asymmetry, Mina bare, Chaeyoung in armor. But something in the air was different. Not tense. Not uncomfortable. Something more like the electricity that lingers after a storm, the sky is clear now but you can feel that something happened.

Last night they had stolen books together. They had crossed the castle in darkness, had whispered between shelves, had shared a silence in a doorframe that neither of them quite wanted to close. And now they were here, in the morning light, with normalcy rebuilt and the secret living beneath it like an underground current.

Mina closed the door. They walked toward the dining hall. And for the first thirty seconds, the silence was the professional one as always: Chaeyoung half a step behind, eyes on the corridor, hand near the sword.

It was Mina who broke it.

"I didn't sleep," she said. Quietly, almost conversational, looking ahead as if speaking to the corridor and not to the person behind her.

Chaeyoung considered several responses. The professional one _You should rest, Your Highness_, the neutral one _I understand_, the one Jihyo would have given _Sleep is not optional_. What came out was something different.

"It shows."

Mina turned her head. Just slightly. Enough for Chaeyoung to see the corner of something that wasn't offense but amused surprise.

"That bad?"

"Con todo respeto, su majestad, I've seen corpses with better color."

The sound Mina made wasn't a laugh. It was the precursor to one, that nasal exhale that's the closest some people get to laughing in a corridor at seven in the morning. But it was real. And Chaeyoung felt it land in that nameless place that was filling too fast, next to the smile in the doorframe and the fraction of a second in the dark corridor where their eyes had met at the distance of a secret.

_Stop._

"The book," said Mina, and the word _book_ came out loaded with everything she couldn't say in a corridor with guards forty meters away. "The first one. The one we found on the upper shelf."

_On the Nature of the Unborn Power_. Chaeyoung didn't know the exact title, but she knew which one. The one Mina had pulled from the shelf with trembling hands, the one she'd opened with eyes ablaze.

"What did you find?"

Mina lowered her voice. The shift was subtle, not a dramatic whisper but a reduction of volume that turned the conversation into something that existed only between the two of them, a private acoustic space within the public space of the corridor.

"Descriptions. Of what I've been..." Mina searched for a word that wouldn't say too much. "Of what I've been feeling. Exact. As if someone had been through the same thing and documented it."

Chaeyoung processed that. Not the content, she didn't understand ancient energies or powers or whatever it was that Mina studied in secret by the light of dying candles. What she processed was the tone. The mix of relief and fear that Mina was holding behind her composure, like water behind a dam with exactly enough cracks to let drops through.

"Good or bad?" **Chaeyoung looked at her with a mix of genuina curiosidad y ligera preocupación.**

"Both." Mina looked ahead again. Her hands, walking at her sides with their usual trained grace, briefly closed. Fingers against palms. "It's good because it means I'm not..." _Crazy. Imagining it. Alone in this._ The sentence hung suspended, incomplete. "And it's bad because what it describes is... bigger than I expected."

Chaeyoung didn't ask how big. Someone else would have. Jihyo would have, because Jihyo needed data to function. Momo would have, because Momo asked the first thing that came to her head. But Chaeyoung had spent two weeks learning to listen to what Mina didn't say, and what Mina wasn't saying was more important than what she was.

What she wasn't saying was: _I'm afraid. Of what I found. Of what it means. Of what I am._

And Chaeyoung, who had no answers for any of those things, did what she knew how to do: be there. Present. Half a step away, which this morning felt like half a step too few.

"Is there more? In the other two." The knight asked gently.

Mina looked at her. Not the half-turn from before, a full look, over her shoulder, and Chaeyoung saw in her eyes something she hadn't seen until now: the need to talk. Not the composed need of someone who organizes information before sharing it. The raw need of someone who has been alone with something too big and who for the first time has someone to not be alone with.

"The second one is more theoretical. But the notebook..." Mina lowered her voice another degree. Now it truly was a whisper, the kind that only works at the exact distance they were at. "The notebook was written by someone who went through the same thing. It's not a treatise. It's a diary. And whoever wrote it was..." She searched again. "Scared. Like me."

_Like me._ Chaeyoung registered the admission with the care of someone receiving something fragile. Mina didn't say _I'm afraid_. Mina said _like me_, in reference to someone else, letting the comparison do the work that direct words couldn't.

"Did they find answers? Whoever wrote the notebook?"

"Some. Partial. There are references to other texts we don't have yet. And there's..." Mina stopped. Not out of caution, out of something more visceral. "There's a section about what happens when it's not understood. When the... whatever it is... grows without direction."

"And what happens?"

Mina didn't answer immediately. They walked three steps in silence. Four. Five. Chaeyoung gave her the time because time was the only thing she could give in a corridor at seven in the morning.

"Nothing good," said Mina finally. Soft. Like someone closing a book on a page they don't want to reread.

Chaeyoung nodded. Not because she understood the content. Because she understood Mina. And what Mina needed right now wasn't someone to explain what the book's descriptions meant or tell her not to be afraid. She needed someone who would walk beside her knowing there was something big and dark in the pages and keep walking anyway.

"Then we keep looking." Chaeyoung declare. Simple, honest.

Two words. No excessive weight, no grand promise. _We keep looking._ As if the next midnight incursion to the restricted section were already a given, as if last night's complicity hadn't been a one-time event but the first chapter of something that continued.

Mina looked at her again. And Chaeyoung saw the exact moment something loosened behind the princess's eyes, a knot coming undone not because it disappears but because someone helps you hold the weight.

"Thank you, Chaeyoung." Soft. Honest. Without the layers of formal courtesy that usually wrapped her gratitude.

"Don't thank me, Your Highness. Sólo tratemos de que el Capitán no se entere,If Jihyo finds out about last night, or the books are going to be the least of my problems."

"Does she really give such agonicing speechs?What would be the biggest?"

"The speech. Jihyo gives speeches when she's furious. Long ones. With numbered points." Por un segundo, la voz de Chaeyoung tembló levemente a media oración.

Mina's smile was small, quick, hidden by turning her face toward the wall. But Chaeyoung saw it. Saw it the same way she saw changes in air currents and shadows that didn't belong to the landscape: with a sense that had no name and that worked best when it came to this specific person.

_Stop. Seriously._

They turned the corner toward the central wing corridor. The stained glass windows began here, and the morning light fragmented into colors that painted the stone floor like a living mosaic. The castle dressed itself as they drew closer to the heart where the crown lived. Servants appeared in the corridors, the morning activity stretching awake with the routine of the everyday.

Mina recomposed herself. The transformation was subtle but complete: the shoulders rose a millimeter, the jaw aligned, the steps found the measured cadence of a princess who knew these flagstones by heart. The Mina who whispered about forbidden books and admitted to being afraid tucked herself behind the Mina who walked through royal corridors with the grace of someone who'd been born to be watched.

And Chaeyoung returned to her half step. Eyes on the corridor. Hand near the sword. Royal guard. What she was.

They reached the dining hall. Stopped before the doors.

And Mina, instead of entering, turned. A full turn, facing Chaeyoung, with the urgency of someone who has one more thing to say before the mask becomes necessary.

"There's something in the notebook," she said, her voice barely audible. "A phrase. About silences."

Chaeyoung waited.

"It says that the first sign something is changing between two people is that the silences change shape." Mina was looking at her directly. Without the filter of a window, without the reflection, without the excuse of a book or a shelf between them. "I read it last night and I thought..."

She didn't finish. Because the dining hall door opened from the inside.

No. The dining hall door didn't open. Someone opened it. And that someone was Nayeon.

The Crown Princess of Twiceland appeared in the doorway with the ease of someone who opens a door expecting to find an empty corridor and finds, instead, her younger sister and her personal guard standing at a distance that wasn't the distance of a guard and a princess. It was the distance of two people who had been talking about something private and whom the world had just interrupted.

Nayeon stopped. An instant. Less than a second. But that less than a second contained a complete scan: Mina's position (turned, facing Chaeyoung, not walking toward the door), Chaeyoung's position (half a step closer than regulation, her body oriented toward Mina rather than the corridor), both their faces (Mina's open, halfway through saying something; Chaeyoung's with that attention that wasn't vigilance but something else).

Nayeon saw everything. Filed everything. And what reached her face was the smile that wasn't a smile: facial diplomacy in its purest form.

"I didn't know my sister and her guard had so much to discuss before breakfast."

The sentence landed with the elegance of something that could be a joke, an observation, or a warning, depending on who was listening and how much they wanted to hear. Nayeon had that ability: every word was a door with three rooms, and you chose which one to enter.

Chaeyoung chose the room of protocol. The transition was instant, — so fast it would have been invisible to anyone not watching. The back straightened the missing millimeter. The right foot stepped half a pace back, restoring the correct distance. The hand that had been resting at her side found the regulation position by the sword pommel. The face closed like a window in winter.

"Good morning, Your Highness." Formal. Impeccable. Knight Chaeyoung, in service to the Crown.

Mina felt the transition as something physical. As if the air between them, which three seconds ago had been warm and conspiratorial and smelled of shared secrets, had gone cold all at once. Chaeyoung was there, in the same spot, at the same distance as a second before, but the person inhabiting her had retreated behind layers of training and duty like an animal pulling back into its burrow at the sound of a noise.

_No_, thought Mina with a clarity that surprised her. _Don't do that. Don't leave._

But she didn't say it. What she said was:

"We were discussing the day's schedule, sister. I was asking Chaeyoung about the guard shifts."

The lie came out smooth. Perfect. With the ease of someone who has lived her entire life in a court where truth is a luxury and plausible explanations are common currency. Mina hated how easy it was. Hated that her first reaction before her sister was to hide what mattered behind something that didn't.

But that was how their world worked. And Mina was, before anything else, someone who knew exactly how her world worked.

Nayeon looked at the two of them. At Mina with that expression that said _I've known you since you were born and I know exactly when you're lying, but I'll choose not to point it out this morning_. At Chaeyoung with something harder to read, — not suspicion, not disapproval, but a deeper assessment. The same look Nayeon used with the council's maps: _where are the pieces and where are they moving_.

"The guard shifts," Nayeon repeated. "Fascinating. You'll have to tell me the more interesting details at the table."

She entered the dining hall. Mina followed. Chaeyoung entered last, a professional ghost.

The dining hall was set with the forced normalcy of recent days, — fresh flowers, teapot in place, breakfast impeccable. But today something was different. Nayeon hadn't been sitting there waiting for them. Nayeon had arrived at the same time, which meant the heir's morning had started somewhere else. The meeting with Jihyo? An advisor? The nobleman Sana had mentioned? Mina registered the anomaly and filed it alongside all the other things she was storing without processing.

They sat. Mina poured herself tea. This time she didn't lose herself in the pour, — she controlled her hands with the determination of someone who has decided that today her hands will do exactly what they're told.

"Did you sleep well?" Nayeon asked. The same question every morning since the attack. The same lie waiting to be used.

"Well."

"You're a terrible liar, Mina.And how did you actually sleep?"

Mina looked up. Nayeon was watching her with something that wasn't the heir's assessment or the older sister's control. It was something more tired. More honest. As if Nayeon had also slept little and was too exhausted to keep all her layers running at once.

"I didn't sleep much," Mina admitted. "I was reading."

"Anything interesting?"

"Cartography." Another smooth lie. Another counterfeit coin that clinked like gold.

Nayeon sipped her tea. She didn't press. But her gaze slid once, brief, to where Chaeyoung stood against the wall, and Mina could see the calculation behind her sister's eyes. Nayeon wasn't thinking about books. She was thinking about what she'd seen at the door: two people who talked as if the world belonged only to them.

The door opened.

"Good morning! What did I miss?"

Sana. Lavender dress, hair half-pinned, the smile that entered rooms before she did. Today it was real, — Sana had slept well, which meant this was one of those days where the sun wasn't a mask but simply Sana being Sana.

She dropped into her chair. Found the pastries. Attacked a berry one with her usual dedication. And then she stopped, the pastry halfway to her mouth, her eyes moving between the three presences in the dining hall with the speed of someone who reads rooms like others read books.

"Okay," said Sana, setting the pastry on her plate. "What happened?"

"Nothing happened," said Nayeon.

"Nayeon. the room feels more tense than it usually is.You're here at the same time as us. That doesn't happen. Mina looks like she didn't sleep. And your guard," she pointed at Chaeyoung with the pastry, "looks like someone added three extra layers of formality this morning. It's like her back is straighter than usual, and that's already saying something."

"Sana." Mina intercept.

"I'm just making observations." An inocent comment from Sana.

"You're making noise." Nayeon said simply.

"Noise and observations are the same thing when no one else is talking." Sana bit the pastry. Chewed with the calm of someone who knows she's just thrown a stone into a pond and is enjoying the ripples. "But fine. Don't tell me. I'll find out anyway."

Mina hid something behind her cup. Not a smile, — something prior to a smile, the contained impulse. Sana had the ability to disarm entire rooms with the precision of someone who uses lightness as a scalpel. And the worst part was that she was right: she would find out. Sana always found out.

"We were talking about the guard shifts," said Nayeon with the voice of someone repeating a joke that isn't hers.

"The guard shifts," Sana repeated. "Right. I'm sure Mina is fascinated by guard shifts. It's her favorite topic, right after classical poetry and just before medieval cartography."

Mina sipped her tea. Said nothing. Nayeon sipped hers. Sana smiled with the satisfaction of someone who has scored a point without anyone admitting there was a game.

Breakfast found its rhythm. Safe topics: the autumn harvest, the trade delegation Nayeon had received that morning (there it was, — the reason for her late arrival), the distant preparations for Sana's birthday that Sana mentioned with the subtlety of someone leaving breadcrumbs toward a castle of gifts.

And beneath all of it, the currents.

Nayeon observing Mina with the peripheral attention of someone who has registered an anomaly and is deciding what to do with it. Sana observing everyone with the smile of someone who collects information like others collect coins. Mina concentrating on her tea and on not looking to where Chaeyoung stood.

Because if she looked, Nayeon would notice. And if Nayeon noticed again, the next question wouldn't be about guard shifts.

Chaeyoung was at her post. Back against the wall. Eyes on the room. The thirty-second sweep running with mechanical precision: door, windows, corridors, repeat. Professional. Impeccable. So perfectly correct that the perfection itself was suspicious, because nobody is that correct unless they're compensating for something.

Mina knew it. She knew because she could tell the difference between the Chaeyoung who watched out of duty and the Chaeyoung who watched to keep from doing something else. And this morning, the Chaeyoung behind her was the latter.

Breakfast ended.

Nayeon left first, — the trade delegation required attention, or the council, or any of the hundred things being heir demanded of her. She said goodbye to Mina with a kiss on the forehead that was new since the attack, a gesture that said more than Nayeon would ever say with words.

Sana stayed a moment longer. She nibbled the last pastry with the dedication of someone who respects food enough not to leave it alone. And before leaving, she leaned toward Mina.

"Nice dark circles," she whispered. "Must be a very interesting book."

And she winked. At Mina. Who felt the blood rise to her neck with the speed of something that had nothing to do with books.

Sana left with the step of someone who knows exactly the effect she leaves behind.

Mina stayed seated. Empty cup. Warm cheeks. The certainty that Sana knew more than she showed and that this was simultaneously terrifying and comforting.

She stood. Smoothed the dress. And for the first time all morning, she allowed herself to look at Chaeyoung directly.

Chaeyoung was looking at her.

Not the sweep. Not the vigilance. She was looking at _her_, and when their eyes met there was an instant, — half a second, maybe less, — where the layers fell away. Where they weren't the princess and the guard but the two people who had whispered between shelves at one in the morning and shared a laugh in a doorframe and walked back through dark corridors with a secret that was only theirs.

Half a second.

Chaeyoung blinked. Her eyes returned to the corridor. The guard returned to her position.

But Mina had seen.

"The library," said Mina. And she walked toward the door.

Chaeyoung followed. Half a step behind. As always. As it should be.

But the half step felt different this morning. Shorter. Warmer. As if the distance between them had decided on its own that the regulations were a suggestion and not a law.

And in Mina's palms, so faint it could have been imagination, the warmth pulsed. Constant. Patient. Like something that waits.

## Scene 4 — "Fire"

The sun was halfway to the horizon when the world split in two.

Chaeyoung was in the library. Mina was reading — or pretending to read, with those notes between the pages of the cartography book that Chaeyoung already recognized as the disguise for something more serious. The afternoon had unfolded with normalcy: lunch in the dining hall, an hour in the library, the rhythm of routine that had become theirs over the past two weeks.

She was doing it well. She was being exactly the guard she always was, — half step, thirty-second sweep, hand on the sword. And if between sweeps her gaze lingered a second too long on Mina's table, on the way the princess bit the end of a quill while thinking, on the unconscious gesture of closing and opening her hands that Mina made every time something in the notes unsettled her, — well. That was a problem for later.

The first sign was the smell.

Not the library candles or the old leather or the dust that was practically part of the furniture. Something more acrid, more alive. The smell of something that shouldn't be burning.

Chaeyoung raised her head. Her nostrils flared, searching for the direction. Southeast. Distant but present. Growing.

Smoke.

The second sign was the sound. Far away, muffled by the stone walls, but unmistakable to someone who had grown up in Hwaren and had learned to read the language of fire before learning to read: the deep crackle of something large burning. Not a fireplace. Not a fallen torch. Something structural.

The third sign was the bells.

The castle alarm bells had a sound Chaeyoung had heard exactly twice in her life: once during a drill Jihyo had organized in her first year as captain, and once the night of the attack on Mina. It was a sound designed to be impossible to ignore, — three quick strikes, pause, three quick strikes. The rhythm of institutionalized panic.

Mina looked up from the book. Her eyes found Chaeyoung at the same time Chaeyoung found her.

Chaeyoung was already moving. She crossed the library to the nearest east-facing window and opened it. The smoke was visible: a dark gray column rising from the east wing, from the stable area, thickening as it fed on whatever was burning below. From here she could see the first flames, orange against the blue of the afternoon, licking the roof of one of the side structures. The hay. If the hay had caught, things were going to get ugly fast.

The stables.

The next thought wasn't tactical. It was a name. _Cricket._ The horse was there. Somewhere in those structures starting to burn, Cricket was locked in his stall with the door that always jammed if you didn't open it at the exact angle Jeongyeon had shown her, — the door Chaeyoung had asked her three times to fix and that Jeongyeon always answered with "tomorrow, tomorrow."

_Jeongyeon is in there._ It was a workday for the stable master. She'd be in the stables, or nearby. She'd be getting the animals out. She'd be fighting with jammed doors and smoke and terrified horses that don't understand fire and refuse to move toward the exit because instinct tells them to stay where they are.

Chaeyoung closed the window. Turned. Mina was on her feet beside her table, the book abandoned, her notes visible on the surface. There was no panic on her face. There was something worse: the taut stillness of someone who waits for instructions and hates needing them.

"We need to get back to the north wing," said Chaeyoung.

"What's happening?"

"The stables are on fire. I don't know more."

"Let's go."

They left the library. The corridors were a river of people moving toward the fire, the human current that fire always generates: those running toward it to fight it and those running away to avoid being reached. Chaeyoung moved against the current, one hand on the sword and the other finding Mina's arm without thinking, guiding her, keeping her close. The contact was instinct, — fingers closing over fabric, firm, the kind of grip that says _you're with me_ without needing words. Mina didn't pull away. Her eyes scanned every face that passed, every uniform, searching for the four without knowing if she'd recognize them in time.

_Don't think about Cricket. Don't think about Jeongyeon. fighting with the stall door that jams. Don't think about hay that burns fast and horses that refuse to move. Focus._

The corridor from the central wing to the north wing was emptier. Most of the guard had moved east, toward the fire, leaving the rest of the castle with a skeleton crew. The emptiness made her uneasy. Something about a fire that pulled every guard to one end of the castle while the rest stood hollow, — something about that felt wrong in a way she couldn't name yet.

They reached the north wing. The two door guards were at their posts, alert, tense from the bells but in position.

"Inside," said Chaeyoung. She opened the door to the chambers. Checked the interior, — windows closed, room clear. Forty-five seconds of routine that today felt like an eternity.

"Clear."

Mina entered. She turned toward Chaeyoung in the doorway, and Chaeyoung saw on her face the question that didn't need to be asked aloud: _Are you leaving?_

"I have to go. They might need help."

She didn't say _this might be a distraction and there are five traitors moving through the castle right now_. She didn't say _Jihyo's plan may have broken and I need to be where she needs me_. She said what Mina could hear without the full truth crushing her.

Mina nodded. Once. And then she said something Chaeyoung didn't expect:

"Go."

Not _be careful_. Not _come back_. _Go._ Said with the voice of someone who understands that staying here, safe, while others fight the fire and whatever the fire hides, is what falls to her but not what she wants. Said with the voice of someone who is tired of being the person others bleed for and can't do anything about it except let them go.

"Don't leave this room," said Chaeyoung. "No matter what."

"I promise."

Chaeyoung nodded. Turned. Took three steps toward the corridor.

"Chaeyoung."

She stopped. Didn't turn. If she turned and saw Mina's face now, with the smoke filtering through the corridor windows and the bells counting down the time, she didn't know if she could leave.

"Please, don't hurt yourself."

The sentence crossed the corridor like an arrow. Not the polished version Mina would have composed if she'd had time, — refined, layered, with the calculated ambiguity she used as a shield. This one was raw. Direct. An anchor thrown in flight by someone who needed the other person to have a reason to come back.

Chaeyoung felt the sentence land in her chest. Next to everything else. In the nameless place.

"Tendré cuidado. Your Highness," she said. Without turning.

And she ran.

The castle corridors were a labyrinth of smoke and movement. Chaeyoung navigated them by memory, cutting through service routes and side stairs that saved her time and encounters. The shoulder protested with every stride, the impact of foot against stone traveling up the arm like a repeated electric shock, but Chaeyoung had a long and well-established relationship with pain that consisted basically of ignoring it until it stopped screaming or until she stopped functioning, whichever came first.

She didn't know where she was going. That was the truth beneath the running, a truth she didn't examine because examining it would have slowed her down. Mina was secured. The guards were at the door. Protocol said she should report to the nearest officer and await orders. Protocol said a lot of things that made sense in training halls and fell apart the moment the world started burning.

Through the window of a central wing corridor she caught a glimpse of the stables. The fire was worse than it had seemed from the library. The flames had taken the roof of the main structure and two of the side buildings. The smoke was black now, dense, the kind that means what's burning is no longer just wood and hay but something more. She could see figures moving around, water brigades forming chains, servants carrying buckets, and amid the chaos, a figure Chaeyoung recognized by the way she moved: Jeongyeon, inside the stables, prying open a stall door with an iron bar while a horse shrieked in terror on the other side. Another person pulled at the reins of two horses that resisted, eyes wild, hooves digging into the ground because every instinct told them the fire was outside and safety was inside, exactly the opposite of the truth.

_Cricket. Had he gotten out? Was he still inside?_

She couldn't know from here. She couldn't go. Not until she found someone with a picture bigger than hers, someone who knew whether this was a fire or something worse.

_And Mina is alone._

The thought arrived uninvited, sharp, with the precision of something that had been waiting for a gap in the action to slip through. Not alone, with guards. Two guards. Standard door rotation, competent, Jihyo-trained. Mina was safe. Mina had promised to stay. Mina was behind a closed door with two armed soldiers and there was no reason, no logical reason, for the cold that was spreading through Chaeyoung's chest like ink in water.

She kept running. The cold came with her.

She found Jihyo in the corridor connecting the central wing to the east wing.

The Captain wasn't giving orders. She was moving, fast, silent, with two officers flanking her and Momo half a step behind. The Momo who moved without bumping into anything, the one Chaeyoung had learned to recognize as the version that mattered. Jihyo saw Chaeyoung and something crossed her face, not surprise but recalculation, the expression of someone whose mental board just gained a piece she hadn't placed there but could use.

"Teniente. Good." Then, immediately, the question that was an order: "Where's Princess Mina?"

"North wing. Two door guards. Room secured, windows closed." The report came out clean, automatic, the language of protocol that lived in her mouth like muscle memory. But something in the way she said _two door guards_ must have carried more weight than she intended, because Jihyo looked at her a half-second longer than the information required.

"She's covered," said Jihyo. Not reassurance, assessment. The Captain confirming a position on the board before moving to the next.

"What do you need?" said Chaeyoung.

"Fall in. I'll explain on the move."

Jihyo dismissed the two officers with a gesture, perimeter duty, east wing exits, and kept walking. Chaeyoung fell into step beside her, Momo on the other flank. The three of them moved through the corridor with the fluidity of people who'd spent years reading each other without words.

"The fire is a distraction," said Jihyo. Low. Fast. The voice she used when time was a currency she couldn't afford to waste. "The traitor is Sir Callen. He has four allies inside the castle, lower-ranked knights, aparently arrived with the autumn rotation. Momo identified them this morning."

Chaeyoung processed that while her legs kept moving. Five. Not one; five. Internal. The geography of the castle reconfigured in her head, safe points becoming question marks, corridors becoming ambush routes.

And then the reconfiguration reached the north wing, and the cold in her chest turned to ice.

_Two door guards. Standard rotation. Against people who've been inside the castle for months, who know the shifts, who walk with purpose._

"Since this morning?" The question came out before she could stop it. Not accusation, the reflex of someone realizing the ground had been shifting under her feet all day and she'd been walking through it blind. She'd been in the library. Watching Mina bite the end of a quill. While five traitors moved through the castle she was supposed to protect.

"Since before dawn," said Jihyo. The honesty was quick, surgical, no room for guilt or apology. "Momo tracked Callen to a dead drop in the south wall last night. Active communication with someone outside the castle. I briefed Nayeon at five. The plan was to move tonight, after the second shift change. Detain Callen privately, then sweep the four."

"And they moved first."

"They moved first." Three words that contained hours of planning turned to ash. "Momo had eyes on Callen. Lost him when the chaos started."

Chaeyoung looked at Momo. Momo didn't return the look. She was scanning the corridor ahead with that stillness that meant she was tracking something, not with her eyes but with whatever instinct lived in the place where other people kept logic.

"I know where he's going," said Momo. Simple. No bravado. "South wall corridor. The service route."

"And the other four?" asked Chaeyoung. The question that mattered most, the one whose answer could mean everything.

"Three are with him. Momo confirmed visual," said Jihyo. Then the pause, small, controlled, but Chaeyoung heard the weight in it. "The other two aren't."

Two. Unaccounted for. Somewhere in a castle on skeleton crew, while half the guard fought a fire on the opposite end.

Chaeyoung didn't say _Mina_. She didn't have to. Jihyo saw it, the way her stride changed for a fraction of a second, the way her eyes cut toward the corridor that led northwest, the involuntary calculation of how long it would take to run back to the north wing at full speed. Jihyo saw all of it with the precision of someone who had commanded this particular soldier for years and knew exactly where her lines were drawn.

"Chaeyoung." Jihyo's voice dropped half a degree. Still the Captain, but the Captain who knew when to speak to the person inside the armor. "Callen is the head. We cut him, the four lose coordination, including the two we can't see. That's how we protect everyone. Including her."

The logic was clean. The logic was right. Jihyo was always right about the order of things, and the order said: neutralize the command structure, collapse the network, protect the whole by removing the center. It was textbook. It was correct. It was the thing Chaeyoung had been trained to do for every year of her service.

It also meant running away from Mina while two people who wanted to take her were somewhere in this castle.

Chaeyoung held Jihyo's gaze. One second. Two. And then she nodded, not because the fear had gone anywhere, but because she trusted Jihyo's mind more than she trusted her own terror. That was what discipline meant. Not the absence of the impulse but the choice to hold it.

"How's the shoulder?" Jihyo asked without breaking stride.

"Functional."

"Functional isn't the same as fine."

"It's what I've got."

Jihyo's jaw tightened. Two seconds of calculation behind her eyes, risk against need, the body against the objective.

"Right hand only. If I see you forcing the left for anything that isn't keeping you alive, I'm pulling you out."

"Understood."

Momo spoke without turning her head. "All four are good. Not good like the castle soldiers. Actually good. If we give them a chance to fight together, we're going to have problems."

"That's why we go for Callen first," said Jihyo. "Separate the head."

Momo stopped. Her hand came up, the silent signal the three of them had used since their second year together. _Contact ahead._

"South corridor," Momo whispered. "Three of them. Moving fast."

Chaeyoung drew her sword. The weight in her right hand was familiar, solid, the one thing in the world that always felt like it belonged there. The left hand hung at her side, useless by order, and the shoulder beneath it throbbed with the patience of something that knew its time would come.

_Cricket is in those stables. Mina is in that room. And I'm here._

She pushed it down. All of it. The horse, the princess, the cold in her chest. She pushed it into the place where Chaeyoung kept the things that couldn't help her right now, the place that would open later, in the dark, on the cot, in the silence after everything, and let her feel what she couldn't afford to feel in a corridor with a sword in her hand.

Momo looked at both of them. And her face wore that expression that appeared so rarely it always caught you off guard: completely serious.

"Nobody fights alone," she said.

Chaeyoung nodded. Jihyo nodded.

They moved.

They found him in the east wing corridor, exactly where Momo had predicted. Not alone. Two of the knights flanked him, one on each side, moving with the coordination of people who have rehearsed this route.

The three figures advanced through the corridor at a pace that was quick but not desperate. With purpose. The step Momo had described, the step of people who know where they're going and don't want you to notice.

Momo appeared from a side corridor. Soot on her face, breathing controlled, sword already drawn. She'd circled around.

"Three," confirmed Momo. Unnecessary, they could all see. But Momo confirmed things the way other people breathed: out of instinct.

"Momo, the one on the left. Chaeyoung, the right. I take Callen" The Captain ordered.

Callen saw them at the distance.

It was his face Chaeyoung registered before anything else. The surprise, real, unacted, the face of someone who's been discovered and who in a fraction of a second has to decide between surrendering and fighting. Chaeyoung had seen that face before. On the basilisk. When Callen saw the creature and chose to run.

This time he didn't run. Maybe because he had nowhere to go, with Jihyo closing off the escape route and Momo emerging from the flank like something that shouldn't have been possible. Or maybe because the message from the dead drop had given him something he didn't have the night of the basilisk: a purpose strong enough to overcome cowardice.

Callen drew his sword.

The two knights drew a fraction of a second later. A fluid, rehearsed, simultaneous movement. The three blades gleamed in the orange light pouring through the corridor windows, the low sun mixed with the glow of the fire, and for an instant the metal seemed ablaze.

"Sir Callen," said Jihyo. Command voice. Low volume. The same voice she used to give orders in combat: no emotion, no noise, only the naked authority of someone who doesn't ask, informs. "You are under arrest for treason against the Crown of Twiceland. Lower your sword."

Callen didn't lower his weapon. What he did was look at Jihyo, then at Chaeyoung, then at Momo, and calculate. Chaeyoung could see the math in his eyes: three against three, closed corridor, no escape route.

"What we're doing is necessary," said Callen. His voice was steadier than Chaeyoung expected. Not the voice of the basilisk's coward. The voice of someone who believes. _The ones who believe in something are the ones who are scary._ "The prophesied one is a threat to the entire kingdom. We are the only ones with the courage to act."

"You can explain that to the council from a cell," said Jihyo. "Last chance. Lower your sword. All of you."

Callen attacked.

Not Jihyo. Chaeyoung. The smallest, the one with the injured shoulder, the one he'd assessed as the weakest link of the trio. Chaeyoung saw it coming, not because it was fast (it was fast) but because Callen's right shoulder dropped half a centimeter before his arm moved, the same micro-movement she'd read in the infiltrator the night of the attack, the body language the body speaks before the mind gives the order.

She dodged. Callen's sword cut the air where her neck had been. Chaeyoung pivoted on her right foot, letting Callen's momentum carry him one step past where he wanted to be, and counterattacked. A quick cut, diagonal, with her right hand. The blade found the side of Callen's armor and slid off, campaign armor, thicker than it looked.

To her left, Momo became something else.

There was no transition. There was no moment where clumsy Momo transformed into lethal Momo. It was as if they were two different people occupying the same body who simply switched places. One second Momo was standing in the corridor with soot on her face and the sleepy eyes she always had. The next she was moving with a fluidity that didn't seem to belong to a human body.

The knight she'd been assigned attacked first. A quick strike, coordinated, the kind of attack that would open most opponents' guard. Momo didn't open her guard. Momo didn't have a guard. What she had was an instinct that worked like water: it found the spaces between strikes the same way water finds cracks in rock, without thinking, without deciding, simply flowing.

She dodged the first blow by tilting her torso three centimeters. Just three. Barely enough. The second blow she deflected with the flat of her sword, a move that looked careless and redirected the attacker's force into the wall. And the third, the third never came, because Momo was already inside the other woman's combat range, too close for the short sword, and what she did was simple and brutal: a pommel strike directly to the sternum that sent her three steps backward with the air stolen from her lungs.

Chaeyoung caught it from the corner of her eye while blocking another strike from Callen. The part of her that assessed combat automatically, the one trained by her father and by Jihyo and by every fight she'd survived, registered what she was seeing with a clarity that was almost admiration: Momo fought as if combat were her native language and everything else were the clumsy translation into a foreign one.

But she had no time to admire. Callen was on her.

The man was strong. Chaeyoung already knew that. What she hadn't calculated well was the desperation. Callen fought like someone who knows the alternative to winning is a cell, and that certainty gave him a ferocity that the basilisk's cowardice wouldn't have let her predict. Every blow was heavy, committed, the kind of attack that sacrifices defense for brute force.

Chaeyoung managed it. Right hand only, as she'd promised, using the corridor to her advantage. The walls were allies: they limited Callen's arcs, which needed space to use his size, and favored Chaeyoung's compact movements, which could turn and cut in spaces Callen couldn't reach.

But the shoulder was there. Present. Waiting.

Callen threw a descending blow. Chaeyoung blocked it with the sword in her right hand, feet planted, absorbing the impact with her legs and back. The clash reverberated through her arm, her chest, her spine. She held. Pushed. Deflected Callen's blade toward the wall, where the metal screeched against stone sending up orange sparks that mixed with the light of the fire.

And then Callen did something smart. Instead of recovering his sword from the clash, he switched hands. His left fist, the free one, flew straight at Chaeyoung's left shoulder.

The blow connected.

Not at full force, Chaeyoung saw the fist coming and turned to absorb part of the impact. But what landed was enough. Pain exploded from the shoulder like a white star, erasing the world for a fraction of a second, and Chaeyoung felt her body do what pain demanded: fold, protect, retreat.

_No._

_Resist._

She wasn't on the ground. She was still standing. But the shoulder screamed with a voice that drowned out everything else, and her left hand, the hand she wasn't supposed to use, closed over the wound by instinct, leaving her with one hand on the sword and her back against the wall.

Callen saw the opening. He raised his sword for the blow that would end this, and Chaeyoung watched the blade rise against the orange light of the corridor.

And then something changed.

Not in the corridor. In her.

The pressure. Subtle at first, like a shift in temperature you notice before understanding why. But growing. Fast. The same pressure from the corridor the night of the attack, the invisible compass pointing at Mina, but multiplied by ten, by a hundred, by something that had no scale.

The world sharpened.

Edges became crisper. Colors more intense. She could see things that a second ago hadn't existed: the pulse in Callen's neck, beating wildly. The microscopic tremor in his right wrist, muscle fatigue, thirty seconds before the grip would loosen. The exact direction of the incoming blow, read in the micro-movements of shoulder, hip, forward foot, as if Callen's body were screaming its intentions before he carried them out.

_Adrenaline_, thought Chaeyoung. _It's the adrenaline, like the other night._

It wasn't adrenaline. Somewhere in her, in a place older than thought, something knew it wasn't adrenaline. But that place didn't have a voice yet, so Chaeyoung did what she knew how to do: she used what she had.

She dodged Callen's blow. Not backward, forward. Inside the arc of the sword, where the blade couldn't cut her because she was too close to the body wielding it. The enhanced vision showed her the exact point: the gap between the side armor and the arm armor, where the leather was thinner, where the flesh was exposed.

Her sword found the point. Controlled. Just enough for Callen to feel the steel entering and know that the next inch would be the one that mattered.

Callen froze. His sword fell from his hand. The sound of metal against stone was the sound of something surrendering.

Chaeyoung was panting. The shoulder burned. The enhanced vision flickered, turning on and off like a candle in a draft, and with each flicker the normal world returned, less sharp, less legible, more diffuse.

But something else arrived with the exhaustion. Something new.

A pull.

Not in the shoulder. In the chest. Deep, magnetic, pointing northwest with the precision of a compass someone had ripped from a map and nailed inside her ribs. The north wing. Mina's direction. And what she felt wasn't a sensation, it was a silent scream. A pressure that vibrated at a frequency only she could hear, and that said, with a clarity that admitted no interpretation:

_Something is very wrong._

To her right, Jihyo finished with her opponent. Clean, efficient, the knight was on the ground with her arm twisted at an angle that wasn't natural and her sword out of reach. Jihyo wasn't even breathing hard.

To her left, Momo. Her opponent was on the ground too, overwhelmed, as if Momo had found every gap in her defense not through analysis but through instinct, one after another, until there were no gaps or defense or will to keep fighting left.

Three against three. Victory.

But Chaeyoung wasn't celebrating. The pull in her chest was growing. The invisible pressure pulsed with a rhythm that wasn't hers, that came from somewhere else, from someone else.

"Jihyo." Her voice sounded different to her own ears. Distant. Urgent. "The other two. They weren't with Callen."

Jihyo looked at her. Chaeyoung saw the exact moment the Captain made the calculation Chaeyoung had already made: five traitors, three neutralized, two loose. Two who weren't in this fight because they were somewhere else. Doing something else.

"Mina," said Chaeyoung. And the word came out with a weight that wasn't logic or deduction. It was certainty. The kind of certainty that lives in the bones and needs no explanation.

"How do you know?"

Chaeyoung didn't have an answer for that. Not one that fit into words, not one Jihyo would accept as data. What she had was a compass nailed into her chest and blood that begged her to run.

"I know," she said. And she ran.

"Chaeyoung!"

She didn't stop. She heard Jihyo's voice behind her, an order or a name or a curse, but the sound was lost in the heartbeat growing inside her, in the compass pointing north, in the corridors blurring around her as she ran faster than the shoulder allowed, faster than her legs should carry her after a fight, driven by something that wasn't muscular force but pure need.

_Mina._

The name beat in time with her steps.

_Mina. Mina. Mina._

And somewhere in the north wing, five hundred meters and fifteen steps away, something was waking up.

## Scene 6 — "The Other Two"

Mina had promised to stay.

She'd meant it. Every syllable, every letter, spoken looking at Chaeyoung with the certainty that a promise was the only thing she could give someone who was about to run toward danger. _I promise._ And she'd kept it. She'd closed the door. She'd sat on the bed. She'd listened to the bells and the shouts and the chaos filtered through the walls of the north wing like a language she didn't speak but understood perfectly.

And she'd waited.

Waiting was what Mina did best. Her entire life had been an exercise in waiting: waiting to be called for dinner, waiting for the council meeting to end, waiting for someone to tell her what was appropriate to want. Waiting was the most refined skill of the _silent princess_, polished by years of practice until it resembled patience but was actually something else. Something closer to resignation dressed as elegance.

But this wait was different.

This wait had claws.

Every sound from outside scraped at her skin. The bells had stopped ringing minutes ago, but the silence they left behind was worse, an active silence, full of the sounds the bells had concealed. Distant shouts. The creak of wood. Footsteps running through corridors that should have been empty at this hour. And beneath all of it, like a bass line, the murmur of the castle reacting to something Mina couldn't see from this room with no east-facing windows.

She stood up. Sat down. Stood up again.

The books were in the wardrobe. All three from the restricted section, wrapped in the cloth Chaeyoung had given her, hidden between layers of clothing. Mina had glanced at them upon entering, an automatic reflex to verify they were still there, that her biggest secret was safe. They were. But the safety of the books didn't matter to her now. What mattered was the smoke filtering under the main door and the absence of sounds on the other side of the antechamber door.

Chaeyoung wasn't there. For the first time since the attack, the antechamber was empty. The too-short cot, the water jug, the sword Chaeyoung left leaning against the wall, no, the sword wasn't there. She'd taken it. Of course she'd taken it. Chaeyoung didn't go anywhere without her sword, the same way Mina didn't go anywhere without a book, and the absence of that sword against that wall was more eloquent than any alarm bell.

_She's fine. Chaeyoung knows what she's doing. She's the best sword in the kingdom. Captain Jihyo is probably with her._

The justifications arrived in single file, orderly, rational, like entries in a catalogue. And like a catalogue, they were useful for organizing information but useless for calming the panic growing beneath the composure like roots under stone.

Mina looked at her hands. The warmth was there. Stronger than this morning. Stronger than last night. Her palms radiated something that wasn't body temperature, something deeper, more electric, as if the skin were the lid of something boiling underneath. She clenched her fists. Opened. Clenched.

_The unborn power is not the absence of power. It is power that waits._

The words from the book floated in her mind with the clarity of something freshly read. _It sleeps in the blood like a seed in dry earth, and when it wakes, it does so in response not to will or instruction, but to emotion._

Emotion. Fear. The fear of being alone in a room while the castle burned and the person who slept fifteen steps from her was somewhere between fire and swords.

The candle on the nightstand flickered.

There was no draft. The window was closed. The door was closed. There was no reason for the flame to move.

It flickered again. Harder. The flame stretched upward, twice its normal height, and Mina's shadow on the wall distorted like a reflection in disturbed water.

Mina watched it. Opened her hands. The candle stabilized.

_It responds to emotion._

She sat on the bed. Breathed. Once, deep. Twice. She built the mental boxes she used to contain the things she couldn't control, the box for fear, the box for waiting, the box for Chaeyoung that had no label but was fuller than all the others, and closed them one by one with the discipline of someone who has practiced the art of composure until it became muscle.

Her palms cooled. One degree. Two. Enough.

_I'm fine. This is just waiting. I know how to wait._

The knock on the door pulled her out of the breathing.

Not the soft knock of a servant or the two taps of the code Chaeyoung used. Three hard, fast knocks, with knuckles that weren't asking permission.

"Your Highness." The voice of the door guard. Tense. "There's a fire in the east wing stables. Captain Jihyo has ordered reinforcements to the area. We need to move."

Mina stood. Her heart beat in her temples, but the voice that left her mouth was the princess's: measured, clear, the voice she used in council.

"Does Chaeyoung know?"

"Lieutenant Chaeyoung has been assigned to another position, Your Highness. We have orders to hold the guard here."

Mina nodded. She approached the door. She didn't open it. Something, not logic, not training, something older; told her the closed door was all she had.

"Who's out there?"

"Sir Hale and myself, Your Highness."

Mina knew the guard by his voice. She'd seen him during the north wing shifts over the past few days. A young man, competent, trained by Jihyo. The normalcy should have been reassuring.

But normalcy didn't explain why her palms were burning again.

What happened next was fast.

The sound of metal against metal on the other side of the door. A cut-off shout, not of pain, of surprise, the sound someone makes when something hits them before they can react. A body falling. Another. And then a silence that lasted exactly two beats of Mina's heart before the door opened.

Not with a key. Not forced. Opened with the kind of efficiency that indicates training, not brutality. The mechanism turned and the door swung inward, and what Mina saw on the other side weren't the guards.

They were two women. Light armor, hair pulled back, short swords in hand. Faces Mina didn't recognize, or did recognize, vaguely, the way you recognize someone you've seen in the corridors without registering their name. Castle knights. Correct uniforms, insignias in place, everything proper. Except the eyes. The eyes belonged to people who have decided to do something irreversible and are no longer measuring the consequences.

Behind them, in the corridor, the two guards were on the floor. Not dead, Mina could see one breathing, the involuntary movement of the other's arm. Unconscious. Neutralized with the speed and precision of someone who knows exactly where to strike to shut a person down without killing them.

"Princess Mina." The one who spoke was the taller of the two. Her voice was calm, almost courteous, as if she were inviting her for a stroll through the gardens. "You need to come with us."

Mina stepped back. One step. Two. The bed hit the backs of her knees.

"No."

The word came out before the thought. It wasn't the elegant refusal of a princess or the calculated resistance of someone with a plan. It was something more primitive. The response of a body that refuses to be taken.

The second woman entered. Closed the door behind her. The sound of the latch falling was the sound of a trap closing.

"I’m afraid you don't have a choice, Your Highness." The first one drew closer. In her free hand she carried something Mina recognized with a delayed jolt of horror: ropes. The same kind of ropes Jihyo had found on the infiltrators from the attack. The ropes that meant kidnapping, not murder. They wanted to take her alive.

_The prophesied one is a threat to the entire kingdom._ Mina hadn't heard those words. But something in these women's eyes told her the same story: they looked at her the way you look at something dangerous. Not with hatred, with conviction. They believed they were doing the right thing. And that made them infinitely more terrifying than someone acting out of malice.

The first woman advanced.

Mina did something she'd never done in her life: she fought.

Not with technique. She had no technique. Not with grace or the efficiency that Chaeyoung or Jihyo or Momo had engraved in their muscles. What she did was more basic: when the woman grabbed her arm, Mina twisted, wrenched her arm free with the strength that desperation gives, and pushed. The woman, who hadn't expected physical resistance from a princess who read poetry and drank tea, stumbled half a step.

Half a step. Half a second. It wasn't enough.

The second woman took her from behind. Arms around the waist, professional, containing. Mina writhed, elbows searching for space, legs pushing against the floor. She wasn't strong. It didn't matter. She fought with something that wasn't strength but refusal, the absolute refusal to be turned into a package, a piece, the thing they'd always said she was: something to be protected, to be handled, to be carried.

_No. Not again. No._

The first woman returned. She held the ropes. She approached Mina's wrists with the efficiency of someone who has practiced this gesture. Her hands grabbed Mina's left wrist and pulled.

And the world changed.

Not gradually. Not like the candle flickering or the warmth coming and going. This was a switch. A dam breaking. The seed in dry earth that the book described, waking not with the gentleness of a plant germinating but with the violence of something that has been compressed too long and that finds, in an instant of fear and rage and refusal, the crack through which to escape.

The warmth in her palms exploded.

Mina felt it first as a pulse. A wave that was born in the center of her chest and expanded outward, through the arms, the hands, the fingers, as if every nerve ending ignited simultaneously. The woman holding her wrist let go, not by choice but because Mina's hand had become unbearably hot, the kind of heat that doesn't burn the skin but vibrates at a frequency the body recognizes as danger before the mind processes it.

The second woman, the one holding her from behind, loosened her grip. Not enough. But enough for Mina to break free, turn, and raise her hands.

She didn't know why she raised her hands. It wasn't a conscious decision. It was the gesture of someone pushing something away from themselves, the oldest reflex of the human body when faced with a threat: _get away_.

But what came out of her hands wasn't a push.

## Scene 7 — "The Awakening"

The air compressed.

Not like a breeze or a current. As if the entire room had held its breath and refused to let it go. The pressure was instantaneous, an invisible force that expanded from Mina like a wave in water, except the water was air and the wave didn't dissipate but concentrated, focused, aimed.

The two women felt it before they understood it. The first, the one with the ropes, the closest, took an involuntary step back, as if the floor had tilted beneath her. Her eyes widened. Not with fear yet. With confusion. The confusion of someone who feels the rules of the world have just changed and doesn't understand how.

The second retreated toward the door. Her hand reached for the sword, because the sword was the answer her training gave her to any threat. But there was nothing to swing a sword at. There was no visible attacker, no weapon, nothing except a princess with her hands raised and her hair loose and eyes that were beginning to look different. Not different in color, different in depth. As if behind Mina's eyes there was something more, something peering out for the first time.

The pressure increased.

The first woman stopped retreating. Not because she wanted to stop but because she could no longer move. Her feet were pinned to the floor, not by a visible mechanism but by the same force compressing the air, as if the gravity in her small portion of the world had multiplied by five. The sword fell from her hand. Her knees buckled. Her face went from confusion to fear in the time it takes a heart to beat.

The second tried to open the door. Her hands found the latch. She turned it. The door didn't move. It wasn't jammed, it was held. Something kept it shut with a force that had no physical origin, the same way something kept the first woman pinned to the floor, the same way something was changing the temperature of the room, degree by degree, as if winter were arriving from inside the walls.

And Mina was at the center of all of it.

She wasn't controlling it. That was the first thing anyone who'd seen her would have understood. The raised arms weren't a gesture of command, they were the arms of someone trying to stay standing in a current that was dragging her. The open eyes weren't watching the room, they were watching something farther, something deeper, something that existed in a place where normal sight doesn't reach. And the expression on her face wasn't power.

It was terror.

_I don't know how to stop. I don't know how to stop. I don't know how to stop._

The thought spun in her head like an animal in a cage. She could feel the power pouring out of her like blood from an open wound, hot, constant, impossible to stop with willpower because willpower had nothing to do with this. This was emotion. Fear. Rage. The accumulation of twenty-one years of being the silent princess, the one who waits, the one who stays, the one who lets others bleed for her, exploding through a channel she didn't know existed until thirty seconds ago.

The two women rose from the floor.

Slowly. Centimeter by centimeter. Not like something floating, like something being lifted. The invisible pressure that had pinned them to the floor reversed direction, from down to up, and their bodies responded with the helplessness of objects that have ceased to belong to them. Feet peeled off the stone. Legs hung. Arms spread at angles that weren't natural, like puppets whose strings are pulled by an inexperienced hand.

The first woman tried to scream. What came out was a compressed sound, squeezed, as if the air in her lungs were being wrung slowly. The second didn't try to scream. She was staring at Mina with the eyes of someone who has just confirmed her worst fears, the prophesied one was real, the threat was real, and they were suspended in the air of her room as living proof of what they had come to prevent.

Mina saw them and didn't see them. Her perception had split in two: one part registered the room, the women floating, the books falling from the wardrobe shelf, the candle burning without a flame, a white light, pure, sprouting from the wick as if someone had replaced fire with something more fundamental. The other part was somewhere else. A hot place, deep, without edges, where the power had no shape or name but only direction, and the direction was _out, everything out, everything that threatens me away from me_.

The pressure increased. The women groaned. A sound Mina registered from far away, like someone hearing rain through a thick window. Ribs. The creak was subtle but unmistakable to someone who'd read about anatomy, the sound of bones bending beyond their tolerance. Not broken. Not yet. But bowing. Yielding. Approaching the point where flexion becomes fracture.

_Stop. Stop. You have to stop._

She couldn't. It was like trying to close a faucet with wet hands, willpower slipped over emotion without finding a grip. Every time she tried to focus on stopping the flow, the fear distracted her, and the fear fed the power, and the power grew, and the cycle repeated like a snake biting its own tail.

The walls creaked. A crack appeared in the stone beside the window, thin as a hair, extending upward with the slowness of something that has all the time in the world. The furniture vibrated. The water jug on the nightstand moved three centimeters toward the edge, four, five, and fell to the floor shattering with a sound that was the most normal thing that had happened in this room in the last sixty seconds.

Mina wanted to cry. She couldn't. Tears required a kind of unraveling that competed with the unraveling already consuming her, and her body didn't have the capacity for both.

So she stayed there. Arms raised. Eyes open. Holding two people in the air whom she'd begun to crush and didn't know how to release.

_Somebody help me._

_Somebody._

---

Then, the door opened. With a steady pressure, centimeter by centimeter, like someone pushing against a current. And the first thing that entered wasn't a body but a sound: a breath. Ragged, broken, the breathing of someone who has run farther than they can and hasn't stopped.

Chaeyoung.

She was in the doorway. Her nose was bleeding, a red thread running from her left nostril to her lip, bright against her skin. There was no visible wound. The blood came from inside, drawn out by the pressure filling the room like water in a tank. The same pressure that had pinned the women to the floor and that now pushed against everything crossing the threshold.

Chaeyoung felt it. Mina could see how it hit her, shoulders curving forward, knees bending, the body reacting to a force it couldn't see but that crushed it with the insistence of something that doesn't accept resistance.

Any other person would have stepped back.

Chaeyoung stepped forward.

One step. The pressure increased. The blood from her nose dripped onto the stone floor, three red drops that fell with the slowness of something moving through a medium denser than air.

Two steps. The muscles in her neck tensed. Her jaw clenched. The left shoulder, the injured one, buckled under the added pressure and Chaeyoung made a sound, not the contained hiss of the nights in the antechamber but something more audible, more honest, the sound of pain meeting determination and losing.

Three steps.

Four.

Mina watched her approach. From the place where she was, the hot, edgeless place where the power lived, she watched Chaeyoung walking toward her the way you walk against a hurricane: leaning, slow, each step a conquest against something that should be impossible to cross. And in Chaeyoung's eyes there was no fear. There was no confusion. There wasn't the _what is happening_ that any other person would have.

What there was, was recognition.

As if Chaeyoung were seeing something she'd felt before, in the corridor the night of the attack, in the pressure in her chest that guided her like a compass, in the nameless instinct that had made her run here before knowing why. As if a part of her, a part that lived in the same ancient place where Mina's power lived, had been waiting for this moment. Not with fear. With the patience of something that always knew it would come.

Five steps. Six.

The distance between them shrank to a meter. The women still floated on either side, groaning, but Mina no longer saw them. All she saw was Chaeyoung advancing through something that should have stopped her, should have crushed her, should have made her flee like any person with a functioning survival instinct would.

But Chaeyoung didn't have a functioning survival instinct. What Chaeyoung had was something stronger: the absolute inability to leave someone she cared about alone with something that was destroying her.

She took her hands.

Chaeyoung's fingers closed around Mina's fingers. The hands that wielded swords and clenched into fists against pain found the hands that turned pages and held teacups and burned with a fire that had no name. The contact was a shock, not electric, not physical, something more. As if two frequencies that had been vibrating separately met and discovered they had always been part of the same melody.

"Mina."

One word. Without title, without armor, without the distance Chaeyoung kept like a wall between the guard and the person. Just the name. Spoken with the voice of someone who is bleeding from the nose and has a broken shoulder and just walked through something impossible and would do it again, without thinking, without hesitating, because the alternative was leaving Mina alone and that wasn't an alternative.

Mina came back.

Not all at once. Gradually, like someone surfacing from water: first the surface, then the air, then the world regaining its edges. Her eyes found Chaeyoung's, there, centimeters away, with the blood on her lip and the broken breathing and that look that asked for no explanations and offered no solutions. A look that said: _I'm here. It's enough. I'm here._

The pressure gave way.

Like a wave retreating. Like a hand opening. The power that had filled the room like a toxic gas pulled back, inward, back toward the hot edgeless place, and with it went the force that held the two women in the air.

They fell.

The bodies struck the floor with a sound Mina felt in her teeth. They didn't move much after — one curled up, hands over her ribs, breathing whistling with the sound of something not working as it should. The other lay on her back, eyes open, staring at the ceiling with the blank expression of someone who has just been through something their mind cannot process.

Alive. Both alive. Broken, but alive.

Chaeyoung released one of Mina's hands — only one — and moved. Quick despite everything, despite the shoulder, despite the blood, despite the exhaustion weighing on every muscle. She took the ropes the first woman had brought and used them to bind them. Wrists, ankles, with the efficiency of someone who has restrained people before and knows that the knots matter more than the force.

She returned to Mina. Took her hand again. The one she'd released. As if the contact were what kept the world in place.

Mina was shaking.

Not the fine tremor of cold nights or the controlled tremor that hides behind composure. She was shaking with her whole body, from the shoulders to the knees, a tremor that came from the center and wouldn't stop with willpower or breathing or any of the tools she'd spent twenty-one years refining.

She looked at her hands. The hands Chaeyoung held. The hands that had lifted two people off the ground and crushed them against the air as if they were made of paper.

"I..." Her voice didn't work. It came out broken, splintered, unrecognizable. "I didn't... I don't know how..."

"I know," said Chaeyoung. Soft. Unhurried. As if they had all the time in the world in a room that smelled of cracked stone and fear. "I know."

Mina searched for something in Chaeyoung's face. Fear. Revulsion. The look the books said people would have when they saw what she was — _the prophesied one is a threat, a weapon, something to be feared and contained_. She searched for the moment Chaeyoung would step back, the moment her fingers would loosen, the moment the professional distance would return like a wall between what Mina was and what Chaeyoung could accept.

She didn't find it.

What she found was Chaeyoung looking at her with the same face she wore every morning when the door opened. With the same attention she had in the library when she thought Mina wasn't watching. With the same expression she'd had in the restricted section when she said _lit up_.

There was no fear in Chaeyoung's face. There was something Mina couldn't name that was bigger than fear and more solid than compassion and more real than any word she'd read in any book.

Mina's legs gave out.

Chaeyoung caught her.

Not as a guard, without technique, without correct positioning, without the protocol for how to support someone who falls. She caught her the way you catch something you can't let drop. With everything she had. The good arm around her shoulders, her body as an anchor, the two of them going down to the floor together because Chaeyoung wasn't going to let her fall even if that meant falling with her.

They stayed like that. On the floor of the room, among the displaced furniture and the fallen books and the crack in the wall and the two bound women who breathed with the whistle of ribs that weren't where they should be. The last candle, burned with a normal flame. Small. Yellow. Trembling, like everything else in this room.

Mina didn't cry. She didn't speak. What she did was close her eyes and let Chaeyoung's presence, the warmth, the smell of leather and sweat and blood, the solidity of a body that was there not because it had to be but because it couldn't be anywhere else, bring her back, piece by piece, to the world that had just broken and now had to be put back together.

And in her palms, against Chaeyoung's fingers, the warmth pulsed.

But slower. Calmer. Like an ember that finds, in the contact with another skin, the reason to stop burning.

---
## Scene 8 — "Ashes"

Jihyo arrived before anyone else.

Chaeyoung heard her before she saw her, the quick step, the weight of metal, the cadence that couldn't be confused with anyone else's because Jihyo walked the way she commanded: without hesitation, without pause, every footfall a decision. And then the voice, in the corridor, giving orders at that volume that never needed to be loud to be absolute.

"Secure the north wing perimeter. No one enters without my authorization."

The door to the room was open. Chaeyoung hadn't closed it. She hadn't moved from the floor, in fact, she was still there, with Mina, the two of them on the stone floor among the displaced furniture and the bound infiltrators who breathed with that broken whistle that meant ribs out of place. She hadn't moved because moving would have meant letting go of Mina, and Mina was still shaking, and the part of Chaeyoung that made tactical decisions was very far away, buried beneath the part that held someone with everything she had left.

Jihyo appeared in the doorway.

She stopped.

Chaeyoung knew Jihyo's gaze the way she knew her own, she'd seen it on battlefields, in meeting rooms, in interrogations. The gaze that absorbed an entire scene in two seconds and filed it without a single emotion crossing the surface. Jihyo looked at the room: the books on the floor, the crack climbing the wall like a petrified lightning bolt, the furniture displaced from their marks in the dust, the candle burning normally now but having left a pool of wax twice its capacity, the two women bound with their own ropes breathing with difficulty. She looked at Mina on the floor, motionless, hands clenched. She looked at Chaeyoung, the dried blood on her upper lip, the nose that had stopped bleeding, the shoulder hanging at an angle that wasn't good, the absence of any wound that explained the blood.

Filed. Didn't ask.

"Status?"

Chaeyoung's voice came out raspy, like something dragging over stone.

"Two infiltrators. They arrived during the fire. Took out the door guards." Pause. "Mina stopped them."

Jihyo didn't ask how. Her gaze dropped to the infiltrators, the marks on the ribs, the way they curled over themselves, the expression of something deeper than pain on their faces, and returned to Mina. And Chaeyoung saw the exact moment Jihyo understood that _how_ wasn't a question for this corridor or this hour.

"Are they alive?"

"Yes."

"Her?"

Chaeyoung looked at Mina. Mina didn't lift her head. But she nodded, a tiny movement, barely perceptible, the nod of someone operating on the minimum.

"She's with me," said Chaeyoung. And the phrase meant three things at once.

Jihyo nodded. Turned toward the corridor.

"Bring the healer. And someone pick up the guards in the hall, check them out, there unconscious, not dead."

Momo appeared behind Jihyo like a silent shadow. She had a shallow cut on her cheekbone and her hair was a mess, but she moved with the same fluidity Chaeyoung had seen twenty minutes ago, no, a lifetime ago, in the east wing corridor. The Momo who fought. She was still there, just beneath the surface, like an animal that hasn't finished returning to its cage.

Momo looked at the room. Looked at Mina. Looked at Chaeyoung.

"Oh" she said.

One syllable. It contained everything Momo didn't know how to put into words and everything she didn't need to put into words because her face said it for her: the worry, the confusion, the instinctive understanding that something enormous had happened here, something no sword could have caused and no sword could have stopped.

Jihyo glanced at her. "Help with the prisoners. I want them in separate cells before anyone finds out who they are."

Momo got moving. Efficient now. She lifted the first infiltrator with an ease that contradicted her build and hoisted her over her shoulder like a sack of grain. The woman groaned. Momo said "sorry" with the same intonation she used when she bumped into a door, and went out into the corridor.

Jihyo stayed a moment longer. Looking at Chaeyoung. Looking at the shoulder.

"The shoulder reopened," she said. Not a question.

"Probably."

"I'll tell the healer to prioritize you."

"See Mina first."

"Chaeyoung—"

"See Mina first."

Jihyo's jaw tightened. But she didn't argue. She nodded once and left. Her footsteps receded down the corridor with the same cadence as always, without hesitation, without pause, and Chaeyoung heard her give three more orders before distance swallowed the words.

Silence returned. Lighter now. No longer the silence of something that has just exploded but of something beginning to settle.

---

Tzuyu arrived with her leather bag and that face Chaeyoung had learned to read better than any map: the expression of someone who's been pulled from her shift to tend to the wounded and is cataloguing severity before crossing the threshold.

She didn't come alone. An apprentice followed, a young boy carrying bandages and vials who stopped in the doorway with wide eyes staring at the crack in the wall as if it were the most terrifying thing he'd seen in his life. Tzuyu didn't stop. She entered, knelt beside Mina, and her hands began moving before her mouth formed the first question.

"Your Highness. Can you hear me?"

Mina lifted her head. Slowly, as if it cost her. Eyes red but dry. Composure rebuilding itself piece by piece beneath the gaze of someone who wasn't Chaeyoung, the reflex of an entire life of being watched.

"I'm fine."

"With all due respect, Your Highness, that's for me to decide."

Tzuyu checked her pulse, her pupils, her hands. She paused at the hands. Mina's palms were red, not burned, not irritated, red as if the blood were closer to the surface than normal. Tzuyu held them a second longer than necessary. Her face didn't change. But Chaeyoung saw how her eyes narrowed a millimeter, how her fingers turned Mina's wrist searching for something she wasn't going to find with normal tools.

Filed. Like Jihyo. But where Jihyo filed data, Tzuyu filed symptoms.

"No external injuries," said Tzuyu, turning back to Mina. "But she's in shock. She needs rest, warmth, and for no one to ask her questions tonight."

The last four words were said looking at Chaeyoung. And at the corridor. And at the entire castle, just in case.

Then she turned to Chaeyoung.

"You."

"I'm—"

"If you say 'fine' I'll suture your lips shut."

Chaeyoung closed her mouth.

Tzuyu wiped the blood from her nose with a damp cloth. The gesture was clinical, but the pressure was gentler than Chaeyoung expected. Tzuyu tilted her head, checked her eyes, one eye, the other, and frowned.

"The nose isn't broken. No impact. No wound." Pause. Looking at her with that intensity that didn't ask permission. "Where is the blood coming from, Chaeyoung?"

Chaeyoung didn't have an answer. Not one she could say out loud, not one that would make sense outside her own head, where the truth was something like: _I walked into the room and the pressure was so strong my nose started bleeding without anyone touching me._

"I don't know."

Tzuyu looked at her three seconds more. Chaeyoung held the gaze. And she saw the moment Tzuyu decided not to press, not because she believed her, but because she understood that conversation couldn't happen here, with Mina two steps away and the castle still smoking.

"The shoulder," said Tzuyu, changing fronts with the surgical efficiency of someone who knows how to pick her battles. She removed the pauldron. Made a sound that wasn't exactly professional. "You tore three of my stitches. Three. Out of ten."

"The circumstances—"

"The circumstances are that you don't listen to me. You never listen to me. Nobody in this castle listens to me." She applied the tincture. Chaeyoung hissed through her teeth. "I'm going to restitch you. Hold still for once in your life."

She held still. Not because Tzuyu asked but because Mina was watching, sitting on the bed now, wrapped in a blanket someone had brought, and her eyes followed every movement of Tzuyu's needle with an expression Chaeyoung couldn't quite read. Concern, maybe. Guilt, maybe. Something else that had no name, like everything between them.

Tzuyu finished. Bandaged. Stood up.

"Three days without using that arm. And this time I mean it."

"You meant it last time."

"And last time you ripped the stitches in less than a week. Let's see if this time we make it to two."

Tzuyu gathered her things. She stopped at the door. Looked at the room one last time, the crack, the books, the wax, the furniture that wasn't where furniture should be. Looked at Mina. Looked at Chaeyoung. And something in her face softened for an instant before returning to professional neutrality.

"Rest. Both of you."

She left. The apprentice followed, still staring at the crack.

---

Nayeon arrived the way a storm arrives when it's decided to contain itself: fast, silent, with a pressure you feel before you see.

She wasn't wearing a crown. She wasn't wearing the formal dress or the cloak or any of the accessories that turned her into the heir of Twiceland before the court. She wore a dark robe over what looked like sleepwear, her hair loose, her feet in slippers that made no sound on the stone. Chaeyoung had seen her arrive at the scene of the Chapter 3 attack dressed, composed, perfect — because Nayeon always dressed before appearing, always projected before reacting, always was heir first and person second.

This time she hadn't dressed. And that told Chaeyoung more about the gravity of the moment than any report.

Nayeon entered the room. Looked at the crack. Looked at the furniture. Looked at the marks on the floor where the infiltrators had fallen. Looked at Chaeyoung, a quick, evaluative glance that confirmed the guard was alive and functional, and then looked at Mina.

And the control cracked.

It didn't break. Nayeon didn't break, she didn't know how, or didn't allow herself to know, or had forgotten at some point between the death of her parents and the weight of a throne she didn't yet occupy but already carried. What it did was crack. Like the wall. A thin line, almost invisible, climbing from somewhere deep up to the eyes.

"Mina."

The voice was the sister's. Not the heir's, not the first princess's, not the woman who said things like _Chaeyoung did her job_ and _end this, Jihyo_ with the coldness of someone moving pieces on a board. This was the voice of someone who had just crossed half the castle in slippers because her little sister was in a room with a crack in the wall and she hadn't gotten there in time.

She sat on the bed. Next to Mina. Not across from her — next to her. And what she did next was so small and so enormous that Chaeyoung felt she should look away, because some gestures are too private to have witnesses.

She took her hand.

She didn't say _are you okay?_ or _what happened?_ or _who did this to you?_ She took Mina's hand — the left one, the one still red, the one that burned with something that wasn't fever — and held it between hers with the gentleness of someone who knows that what she holds is fragile and dangerous and precious, all at the same time.

Mina didn't move. But her fingers closed around Nayeon's, and it was a different grip from the one she'd had with Chaeyoung — not the grip of someone holding on to the world but of someone holding on to childhood, to the memory of an older sister who came to her room during storms.

And in Nayeon's eyes, Chaeyoung saw something that chilled her blood.

It wasn't surprise.

It was recognition.

The recognition of someone who looks at a room destroyed by something that isn't physical and doesn't ask herself _how is this possible_ but _it finally happened_. Nayeon's eyes on the crack, on Mina's red palms, on the books on the floor — those eyes knew something. They had known something before tonight, before this chapter, perhaps before Mina knew anything about herself.

Nayeon knew.

_How much?_ thought Chaeyoung. _How much do you know? Since when?_

The questions stayed where they were — in Chaeyoung's head, filed alongside all the other things she couldn't say aloud. Because Nayeon was holding her sister's hand and the crack in her control was real, and this wasn't the moment for questions that sounded like accusations.

"I'm here," said Nayeon. Soft. So soft Chaeyoung almost didn't hear it. "I'm here, Mina."

Mina closed her eyes. And for a moment, just a moment, she stopped being the princess of the nameless power and was just the younger sister who needed someone to tell her the world was still in its place.

---

Sana arrived last.

Not because she was the slowest or the least concerned, but because someone — a guard, a servant, perhaps Jihyo herself — had stopped her in the corridor and told her everything was under control and that Her Highness the third princess was fine, and Sana had heard _everything is under control_ and _she's fine_ and had decided that those two phrases together were the biggest lie she'd been told in her life, and had come anyway.

She entered the room with wide eyes. Barefoot, same as Nayeon — she'd gotten out of bed with the urgency of someone who hears fire bells and doesn't stop to look for shoes. Hair loose, face without the mask of light she wore during the day, without the smile or the glow or the charm that made people forget that behind the sun there was a person who felt with an intensity that frightened.

She looked at the room.

The crack. The furniture. The wax. The books.

She looked at Nayeon holding Mina's hand.

She looked at Chaeyoung with dried blood on her face and fresh bandages on her shoulder.

And for the first time since Chaeyoung had known her — through all the dinners, all the breakfasts, all the brilliant remarks and the laughter that filled rooms — Sana had no words.

She said nothing. Asked nothing. She crossed the room, sat on the other side of Mina, and hugged her.

It was a hug without form or protocol. Arms around Mina's shoulders, head against her head, her whole body turning toward her like a sunflower finding the sun except this time it was reversed — it was Sana, who was always the sun, turning toward someone who needed the warmth more than the light.

Mina went rigid for a second. Like someone who's forgotten how to receive a hug, or who wasn't expecting one, or who has so much inside that a kind gesture threatens to break the last wall keeping her whole.

Then she sank into Sana.

She didn't cry. Chaeyoung suspected Mina wasn't going to cry tonight — that the tears were behind too many layers of shock and exhaustion and terror and would arrive later, maybe tomorrow, maybe in three days, maybe in some quiet moment when Mina was alone with her hands and remembered what they'd done. But she sank into Sana's embrace with the surrender of someone releasing something she didn't know she was carrying.

Nayeon didn't let go of Mina's hand. Sana didn't let go of Mina's shoulders. The three sisters, on a bed in a broken room, held together by nothing more than the gravity of shared blood.

Chaeyoung rose from the floor. Slowly, because the shoulder reminded her with a lash of pain that the three torn stitches weren't an abstraction. She stepped back one pace. Two. To the antechamber door. Not because she wanted to leave but because that moment didn't belong to her. The sisters needed the sisters, and the guard needed to be exactly that: the person who watches the door so no one interrupts what's happening inside.

She leaned against the frame. From there she could see the entire room — Mina between her sisters, the crack in the wall climbing like a question with no answer, the corridor torches casting long shadows on the stone. And from there she could feel — still, faintly, like the echo of a bell that stopped ringing a while ago — the beat of something in her chest that pointed at Mina with the insistence of something that refused to be ignored.

She stayed there. Watching. Bleeding inside in ways that had nothing to do with the shoulder.

_Cricket._

The question left Chaeyoung's mouth in a moment of stillness, when Nayeon had gone to speak with Jihyo and Sana had stayed with Mina and a replacement guard passed through the corridor.

"The stables. Is there a report?"

The guard stopped. "The fire was contained, Lieutenant. The stable master got the animals out before it spread. No casualties."

The stable master. Jeongyeon.

Chaeyoung closed her eyes. One second. Two. A relief so physical it loosened her shoulders, and the injured shoulder punished her for it immediately, but it didn't matter. Cricket was alive. Jeongyeon had gotten him out. Of course she'd gotten him out — Jeongyeon treated those horses as if they were her children, with the same quiet fierceness with which Chaeyoung treated her sword, and if the world ended tomorrow, the last thing Jeongyeon would save from the flames would be a stable full of animals who didn't know they loved her.

Cricket was fine.

Her father's horse was fine.

It was a small thing amid everything that had happened tonight. But small things were what kept the weight of the big ones from crushing you, and Chaeyoung collected them like coins from the ground — one by one, storing them where they could serve as counterweight.

She opened her eyes. Breathed. Returned to her post in the doorframe.

The night continued. The castle breathed slowly, like someone recovering from a blow. And somewhere in the corridors, Chaeyoung knew Jihyo was moving pieces, and Momo was hauling prisoners, and Dahyun was being woken to process evidence, and the world kept turning with the brutal indifference of things that don't stop for a broken room or a princess who had just discovered she could break far more than rooms.

## Scene 9 — "The Silk"

Jihyo didn't stop until there was nothing left to do.

Five prisoners in separate cells. New guards on every door. North wing perimeter secured. Reliefs organized for the night. Preliminary report dictated to a scribe who was more afraid of her than of the prisoners. The stables inspected — damage contained, structure stable, Jeongyeon confirming the report with the brevity of someone who's saved sixteen horses from a fire and doesn't want to talk about it. Callen in the cell at the far end, the coldest, the farthest from the rest, because Jihyo knew that traitors talk more when they think no one can hear them and sometimes stone walls carry sound better than wooden ones.

Everything done. Everything in place. The castle in order like a clock that's been wound again.

And only when there was nothing left to do did Jihyo stop.

She was in the corridor connecting the east wing to the inner gardens. A stone hallway with arches open to the night sky, where the torches fought the darkness and lost. She leaned against the wall. She didn't sit — Jihyo didn't sit in places where someone might see her vulnerable, and a corridor was a place where someone might see her — but she leaned, and for Jihyo that was the same as collapsing.

The armor weighed twice what it normally did. Or her body weighed half. She had dust on her face, soot in her hair, a bruise on her left side that would bloom tomorrow in colors that matched nothing, and a cut on her right palm that had stopped actively bleeding but continued dripping with the stubbornness of wounds you ignore until the body decides it's been ignored long enough.

"You're getting blood on the floor, Captain."

She hadn't noticed it. She didn't know when she'd gotten it — the fight? A corner while running? The edge of a sword she didn't remember touching? The hours after combat were always like this: the body saved its complaints for later, like a patient creditor who waits until the debtor stops before presenting the bill.

Blood dripped over her knuckles. From her knuckles to the floor. Dark drops on dark stone. Almost invisible. Almost.

Jihyo raised her head.

Sana was at the end of the corridor. Not walking toward her — stopped, as if she'd gotten that far and had needed a moment before deciding whether to approach. She wore the same robe Jihyo had seen on her in Mina's room, her feet still bare on the cold stone, her hair falling over her shoulders with the disorder of someone who hasn't looked in a mirror in hours and doesn't care. But the eyes were different. Not the eyes of the princess who flirted in the gardens or the sister who hugged without words. They were the eyes of someone who has cried at some point in the last hour and isn't going to mention it.

Jihyo straightened. The reflex was automatic — back straight, shoulders squared, the captain's posture that activates like a switch when someone from the royal family is present. But the movement pulled a wince from the bruised side, something that didn't quite become a grimace but wasn't invisible either, and Sana's eyes registered it with the precision of someone who pays more attention than she lets on.

"Your Highness. Do you need something?"

"I was going to ask you the same thing. You are the one bleeding."

Sana approached. Slowly. Not with the studied grace of a princess in public or the urgency of someone in crisis. She walked toward Jihyo the way you approach something you don't want to startle — a wounded animal, a dying fire, something that needs presence more than intervention.

She stopped one step away. Looked Jihyo up and down. The soot, the dust, the bruise peeking above the armor's neckline. The hand.

She pulled something from between the folds of her robe. A handkerchief. Light silk, fine embroidery at the edges — the kind of object that exists in the world of princesses and has no place in Jihyo's world, where fabrics serve as bandages and embroidery is a waste of thread.

She extended it toward Jihyo. Not toward her face. Toward her hand.

"This?"

"Even the strongest ones need some help. I think that qualifies as needing something."

There was no game in the voice. No flirtation or double meaning or the layer of shine Sana used as social armor. It was a clean voice. Simple. The voice of someone who has spent the last few hours watching her sister tremble and has decided that the next person she finds bleeding isn't going to keep doing it if she can help it.

Jihyo looked down at her own hand. The blood on her knuckles. The drops on the floor. She was right, she hadn't noticed. The cut wasn't deep, but it was long, crossing the palm diagonally like a river on a map.

She took the handkerchief.

The silk was absurdly soft against her skin. Jihyo's fingers, calloused, rough, the fingers of someone who wields swords and never applies cream or balm because that would be time she could spend on something useful; closed around the fabric with the clumsiness of someone handling a material that doesn't belong to her. She wrapped her hand. Badly. The knot came out crooked, the silk staining red on first contact, and the result was something Tzuyu would have described as an insult to medicine.

But the blood stopped dripping.

Sana looked at her. One moment. Two. And in the third moment she did something Jihyo didn't expect: she sighed. Not a dramatic sigh, not the exasperated sigh of someone dealing with something frustrating. A tired sigh. Real. The sigh of someone letting go of something they've been holding.

"Thank you, Your Highness."

"Sana."

"Pardon?"

"My name. It's Sana." A pause. The briefest one. "We've had three conversations and you've called me 'Your Highness' in all of them. I think we can move past that."

The nearest torch crackled. A night breeze came through the corridor arches, carrying the smell of distant smoke and wet garden. Jihyo held Sana's gaze — not the captain's evaluative gaze or the strategist's tactical one, but the gaze of someone who's run out of layers for today and doesn't have the energy to build new ones.

She didn't say "Sana." She didn't say "I can't." She didn't say anything.

But something moved on her face — something that wasn't a smile or a nod or any gesture Jihyo knew how to name, something that lived in the space between what she wanted to say and what protocol allowed, between the woman leaning against a wall bleeding and the captain standing before a princess.

Sana didn't press. The smile that appeared on her face wasn't the big one, the kind that lit up halls and made people forget their problems. It was the small one. The one at the edges. The one Jihyo had seen for the first time in the rose garden when Sana asked her if she ever rested, and that now, in a dark corridor smelling of smoke, felt like something that shouldn't be hers but was there anyway.

"Good night, Captain."

Soft. Without game. And yet, with everything.

Sana turned. Walked down the corridor, bare feet on stone, her hair moving with the night breeze. She didn't look back. She didn't need to. Some things are said with the certainty that the other person will carry them, and Sana had just placed her name in Jihyo's hands with the trust of someone who knows that what she gives won't be lost.

Jihyo stayed in the corridor.

Hand wrapped in silk. Blood stopped. The name floating in the air like something with its own weight, something that occupied space, something Jihyo could feel in her throat like a word she hasn't said but already knows how it tastes.

_Sana._

She didn't say it out loud. But she looked down at the handkerchief and didn't take it off.

She didn't take it off that night. Or the next. And when the silk stained red and dried in the shape of her knuckles, Jihyo folded it and kept it in the inner pocket of her uniform, where no one could see it but where she could feel it every time her hand brushed the seam.

Some things don't need to be said to be carried.

## Scene 10 — "What Goes Unsaid"

Later.

_Later_ was a word that meant different things depending on the night. Some nights, _later_ meant tea on the terrace, the poetry Mina read in a low voice, the comfortable silence of two people who have learned to be together without filling the air. Other nights it meant the antechamber cot, the contained hiss of Chaeyoung adjusting the shoulder, the sounds of the castle falling asleep like an enormous animal.

Tonight, _later_ meant the after of everything. After the prisoners and the cells and the orders and the blood and the reports and the sisters and the healer. After the castle had digested what happened and had decided, with the indifference of structures that survive centuries, to stay standing.

Mina was sitting on the bed. The blanket someone had draped over her shoulders was still there, but she no longer needed it — she'd stopped shaking a while ago, and composure had returned the way the tide returns: slowly, inevitably, covering what lay beneath with the efficiency of a lifetime of practice. But her hands were in her lap, and every now and then she turned them over, looking at her palms with the concentration of someone searching for a mark that isn't visible.

The books had been picked up. Chaeyoung had done it while Tzuyu tended to Mina — without asking what they were, without looking at the titles, without commenting that one of them had a symbol on the cover that didn't resemble anything she'd seen in the royal library. She'd stacked them on the nightstand, aligned by size, because Chaeyoung did things with her hands and her hands did things in order even when everything else was chaos.

The room bore marks. The crack in the wall that no one was going to explain with a credible excuse. The dents in the floor where the furniture had been pushed by something that wasn't physical. The pool of wax beside the candle, too large, the kind that only forms when a flame burns with an intensity the wax can't contain. Details that told a story neither of them was ready to read aloud.

Chaeyoung was on the floor.

Not on the antechamber cot — on the floor of Mina's room, beside the door connecting the two spaces. Legs stretched out, back against the stone, sword in her lap. Not in guard position. In Chaeyoung position: the version that sits where she can and rests where she fits and has never needed more than a point of support to feel she has a place in the world.

The shoulder throbbed. Tzuyu's new stitches pulled with every breath, a constant reminder that the body has limits even if the will doesn't acknowledge them. But pain was something familiar, something Chaeyoung knew how to inhabit the way you inhabit a house with leaks — moving around the broken parts, finding the corners that still work.

They didn't speak.

Not for a long while. The silence wasn't uncomfortable — it never was between them, not since the silences had begun to mean something during the nights on the terrace, the mornings on the way to the library, the spaces between conversations where what went unsaid weighed more than what was said. But this silence was different. This silence had the shape of something they both knew and neither mentioned, like a third person in the room waiting to be acknowledged.

Chaeyoung had called her Mina.

Without title. Without distance. Without the careful architecture of _Your Highness_ that Chaeyoung used as a wall between what she should feel and what she felt. In the moment of the awakening, with the pressure crushing her and the blood in her nose and the world shrinking to the space between the door and the person she needed to reach, she had said _Mina_ with the ease of something that had always been there, waiting for urgency to strip the formality away the way you strip a bandage.

They both knew it. It floated in the air with the weight of things that change everything without making a sound.

Mina broke the silence.

"Chaeyoung?"

"Yeah?"

A pause. Mina looked at her hands one more time. Closed them. Opened them. Raised her head.

"Do you still owe me a story?"

The question was absurd. It was absurd in context — a room with a crack in the wall, a princess who had lifted two people with her hands, a guard with dried blood under her nose and fresh stitches in her shoulder. It was absurd and it was perfect, because what the question said wasn't _tell me a story_ but _tell me we're still us_. _Tell me the version that whispered between shelves and stole books at midnight and walked to the dining hall sharing a secret that was only ours — tell me that version still exists beneath what happened tonight._

Chaeyoung felt something on her face. The almost-smile. The one that wasn't a full smile because Chaeyoung didn't give full smiles — she gave them in pieces, in fragments, like someone distributing something valuable with care. The almost-smile that Mina had learned to look for in the angles of her face the same way Chaeyoung had learned to look for the moments Mina let the composure drop.

"Another day, Your Highness."

But this time the words didn't sound like the other times. Not like the library's polite evasion, the _not yet_ that kept the distance intact. This time they sounded warm. Like a _yes_ that takes its time. Like a promise that's in no hurry because it knows it will be kept.

_Another day. Another day I'll tell you why I became a knight. Another day I'll tell you about Hwaren and the fire and my father and the sword and all the things I carry. Another day, because tonight what we need isn't stories but this — the silence, the presence, the certainty that you're there and I'm here and that's enough._

Mina nodded. Slowly. And something in her face relaxed — not a smile, not quite, but the absence of the tension she'd been carrying in her jaw and her shoulders and her eyes since the world had changed. A small surrender. The decision to stop holding everything for a moment and trust that gravity wasn't going to destroy her.

The silence returned. But it was a different silence now — not the one shaped by what was left unsaid but the one shaped by what doesn't need to be said. Mina on the bed, the books stacked beside her like paper sentinels. Chaeyoung on the floor, sword in her lap, shoulder bandaged, back against the stone. The antechamber door open between them, as it had been every night since the library, like a threshold neither fully crossed but neither closed.

And between them, unnamed, the _Mina_ that Chaeyoung had spoken floated like something that could no longer be undone. They wouldn't mention it. Not tonight, maybe not tomorrow, maybe not for many nights. But it was there — like the open door, like the silk handkerchief, like the crack in the wall, like all the things that change between two people without anyone naming them and that for that very reason change everything.

Mina closed her eyes. Not to sleep — to rest. To stop looking at her hands and trust that the hands would still be hers tomorrow, with everything they contained.

Chaeyoung didn't close her eyes. She kept them open, on the room, on the door, on Mina. Not because it was her duty — duty was a word that was too small for what she felt and that she used anyway because she didn't have another.

She watched. Because that's what Chaeyoung did.

She stayed. Because that was the only thing she knew how to do when someone mattered to her in a way that had no name and that grew every night, like a crack in a wall — slowly and unstoppably.

## Scene 11 — "The Board"

The private audience hall was cold.

Not the temperature — the atmosphere. Nayeon seated in the chair that wasn't a throne but functioned as one, the robe swapped for a dark dress and her hair pinned in a hurry, the heir rebuilt over the bones of the sister who had held Mina's hand less than an hour ago. Jihyo standing before her, because Jihyo didn't sit in front of Nayeon — not out of protocol but out of instinct, because conversations with the heir were operational and operations were done on your feet.

"Five prisoners," said Jihyo. "Callen in an isolated cell. The four women separated. All holding minor knight rank — legitimate castle access, verified insignias. They'd been inside a long time."

"How long?"

"Months. At least since before the first attack. Possibly since before Chaeyoung arrived."

Nayeon processed this with the face she used for the council: motionless, attentive, no visible reaction. But Jihyo knew her well enough to notice how her fingers tightened on the arm of the chair.

"Have they talked?"

"No. None of them. They shut down before we even sat them down. Callen is requesting a noble advocate. The other four haven't opened their mouths."

"Fanatics."

"True believers. They think they're doing the right thing." Jihyo paused. "Which makes them worse than mercenaries. A mercenary's silence can be bought. A fanatic's can't."

Nayeon nodded. The gesture was minimal, but it contained a decision.

"Connection to the order."

It wasn't a question. Jihyo answered anyway.

"The anti-prophecy order. They believe Mina is a threat to the kingdom. They wanted to extract her from the castle — kidnapping, not assassination. Same pattern as the first attack." Pause. "But these five are local. Recruited, most likely. Pushed forward as expendable front-line assets. The real organization wouldn't send minor-ranked knights on an operation like this. They're too easy to catch, too easy to identify."

"Pawns."

"Pawns. The hand moving the pieces is still somewhere we can't see."

The silence that followed belonged to two people who understand the same thing without needing to say it: that the internal threat was neutralized but the real threat — the order, the network, whoever was coordinating from the shadows — remained intact. They'd cleaned the surface. What lay beneath was still rotting.

"Operational outcome," said Nayeon, shifting tone. More formal. The heir in briefing mode. "The deadline."

"Met. Four and a half days out of five."

Something crossed Nayeon's face that might have been satisfaction if Nayeon were someone who showed satisfaction.

"Good." A word that weighed more than it should. It wasn't praise — Nayeon didn't give praise — but it was acknowledgment, and coming from her, it was the closest thing to a _thank you_ that Jihyo was going to get.

Jihyo nodded. Accepted the weight of that word and stored it where she kept the things that mattered: somewhere silent behind duty.

"Pending decisions," said Jihyo.

"Mina returns to the royal wing." Nayeon didn't frame it as a proposal. "The original chambers have been restored for two days. The antechamber stays — Chaeyoung remains with her."

Jihyo noted the decision: the proximity was no longer just security. Nayeon had seen what Chaeyoung did for Mina — or what Mina did when Chaeyoung was near — and had decided it worked. She wasn't interested in why it worked. She was interested in it working.

"The movement restrictions," Nayeon continued. "Are lifted. The castle is secure. Or secure enough." The pause was deliberate. "There's a diplomatic visit next week. And Sana's birthday. I can't have the castle under siege conditions."

"Understood."

Nayeon reclined one centimeter in the chair. The gesture of someone who has finished with operational matters and is now arriving at the part she doesn't want to touch but can't avoid.

"Jihyo."

"Yes?"

"What happened in my sister's room?"

The question fell into the hall like a stone into still water. Not with violence — with weight. The weight of something they'd both been circling the entire conversation and that Nayeon was finally placing on the table with the candor of someone who has no patience for detours at this hour of the night.

Jihyo chose her words with the same precision she used to choose combat formations.

"The two infiltrators entered the princess's room. They overpowered the guards. They tried to take her." Pause. "Princess Mina stopped them."

"How?"

"I don't have a complete answer for that."

"But you have an incomplete one."

Jihyo held Nayeon's gaze. In that gaze there were many things — the years-long relationship between an heir and her captain, the trust built on crises and impossible decisions, the silent understanding of two people who carry the same castle on different shoulders. And beneath all of it, the certainty that both of them were standing before something that didn't belong to the world of swords and strategy they'd inhabited until now.

"The room has a crack in the wall that wasn't there before. The furniture was displaced without physical contact. Both infiltrators have fractured ribs with no strike marks. And Chaeyoung arrived at the scene with blood coming from her nose without any wound to explain it."

Each datum landed with surgical precision. Jihyo presented them as what they were — evidence without interpretation, facts without conclusion — because Jihyo knew that Nayeon didn't need to be told what they meant. Nayeon already knew what they meant. She'd known before tonight.

And there it was again. In Nayeon's eyes. Not surprise. Not confusion. Not the vertigo of someone discovering something impossible.

Recognition.

The look of someone who has been waiting for a confirmation she dreaded and has just received it.

"My sister needs answers," said Nayeon. Voice controlled. Too controlled. "Answers that neither you nor I can give her."

Jihyo waited. She knew there was more.

"I'm going to write to my great-grandmother."

The sentence landed without noise. But Jihyo felt the weight — not of the words but of what they meant. The princesses' great-grandmother wasn't a name invoked lightly. She was the matriarch of a lineage that spanned twenty generations, the woman who had known about the prophecy before any of them were born, the person who had the answers Nayeon had spent years avoiding seeking.

If Nayeon was writing to her, it was because she could no longer avoid it. Because a crack in a wall and two women floating had turned the prophecy from a political abstraction into something that breathed and trembled and had red hands and was called Mina.

"When?" Jihyo asked.

"Tomorrow. The letter goes out with the first rider."

Jihyo nodded. She didn't ask more. Some decisions are made and obeyed, and Nayeon's expression said this was one of those.

"One more thing," said Nayeon. Jihyo waited. "What happened in that room. Whatever my sister did or was done to her or that woke inside her." Nayeon's eyes found Jihyo's with an intensity that admitted no ambiguity. "It doesn't leave the people who already know. It doesn't reach the council, doesn't reach the nobles, doesn't reach anyone outside this hall and the people who were in that room tonight."

"Understood."

"It's not a secret, Jihyo. It's a protection."

"I know."

Nayeon looked at her a moment longer. And in that moment, beneath the heir and the strategist and the woman who moved pieces and made impossible decisions, Jihyo saw the older sister. The one who had held Mina's hand on a bed. The one who had spent years knowing something about her little sister that terrified her and that compelled her to protect her with a ferocity that Mina mistook for control.

Nayeon loved Mina. She did it badly — with secrets and distance and overprotection and the broken tools of someone who learned to govern before she learned to love. But she did it with everything she had.

"Good night, Captain."

"Good night, Your Highness."

Jihyo gave the protocol bow. She turned toward the door. And before leaving, in the doorway, she said:

"Nayeon."

The heir looked up. The use of the name — without title, without rank — was something Jihyo allowed herself only in the moments where truth mattered more than form.

"Princess Mina is stronger than you think."

Nayeon didn't respond. But something moved on her face — something that could have been relief, or pain, or the mix of both that people feel when they discover that what they feared most is also what they hoped for most.

Jihyo left.

The door closed behind her. In the empty hall, Nayeon was left alone with the weight of a letter she hadn't yet written and a crack in the north wing wall that reminded her, with the insistence of things that don't lie, that the world was about to change.

That it had already changed.

That her little sister — the quiet one, the one who read poetry and drank tea and watched the stars before bed, was something that no strategy book and no council of nobles had prepared her to handle.

And that the letter to her great-grandmother wasn't a decision.

It was a surrender.
