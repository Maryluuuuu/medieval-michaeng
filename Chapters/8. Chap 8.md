## Scene 1 — "Before the Party"

The training yard was empty at this hour.

That was the point.

Chaeyoung had been awake since four. Not the slow, reluctant kind of wakefulness that came from bad sleep — the sharp kind, the kind where the body opens its eyes and already knows it isn't going back. She'd lain in the dark for twenty minutes listening to the sounds of the royal wing: the distant change of the watch, a cart in the lower courtyard, the particular quality of silence that Mina's room produced when Mina was still asleep. Then she'd gotten up, pulled on her training clothes, and left.

The shoulder was better. Two weeks out from Callen's fist and Tzuyu's new stitches and the fire and everything else, and the range of motion was almost what it had been. _Almost._ The last ten degrees of a full extension still cost her something — a warning rather than a punishment, a tightness that said _carefully_ in the voice Miyoung used when she handed over jars of salve. Twice a day. No excuses.

She was running her third form when she heard footsteps.

Not the footsteps of the morning patrol — those came in pairs, heavy, with the rhythm of two guards who had been walking the same route together for long enough that their steps had synchronized without either of them noticing. These were single footsteps. Lighter, faster, with the economy of someone who moved like they were always about to need to move differently.

Jihyo appeared at the entrance to the yard with two wooden practice swords under one arm and the expression of someone who had also been awake since four and had made better use of the time.

"Captain."

"You're up early."

"So are you."

Jihyo tossed her one of the practice swords without ceremony. Chaeyoung caught it left-handed — a reflex she'd developed sometime in the last month that she hadn't entirely decided to develop. Jihyo noted it. Said nothing.

They took position.

"How's the shoulder?"

"Fine."

"Show me."

Chaeyoung raised the practice sword to guard position. Full extension, right arm. The last ten degrees arrived and the shoulder said _carefully_ and she held it there for three seconds anyway, because Jihyo was watching and because Jihyo's definition of _fine_ required evidence.

"Better than last week," Jihyo said. "Still not full rotation."

"Getting there."

"Good enough for today." Jihyo shifted her weight. "Come on."

They sparred. Not the kind of sparring that produced bruises and reports — the kind that was conversation in another language. Jihyo pushed her left side repeatedly, methodically, not to exploit the weakness but to map it. Chaeyoung adjusted each time, compensating, and Jihyo watched the compensations the way Dahyun watched metal: looking for what the pattern said beneath the pattern.

After the third exchange Jihyo stepped back and lowered her sword.

"You're protecting the left by leading with the right more than you used to," she said. "Not badly. But it's become a habit. If someone's paying attention—"

"They'll go for the right to open the left."

"Callen went for the left directly."

"Callen knew the shoulder."

"Callen had been watching you for months." Jihyo said it without inflection. The fact itself was the point. "The next one who has access like that, who's paying that kind of attention — they'll see the compensation before they see the wound."

Chaeyoung absorbed this. "So stop compensating."

"So be aware you're doing it. There's a difference." Jihyo resumed her stance. "Again."

They went again. Chaeyoung worked the left side deliberately, letting it cost what it cost, and Jihyo pushed harder when she did as if rewarding the honesty of it.

After the next exchange Jihyo stepped back again. Less critical this time. More like the pause between paragraphs.

"The prisoners," she said.

Chaeyoung lowered the practice sword. "Someone talked?"

"Not yet. But the math is changing." Jihyo kept her voice level. "The two allied knights from the corridor — they've been separate since the arrest. No contact, no way to coordinate their silence. Two weeks of that and people start calculating differently." A pause. "One of them asked for water yesterday. Asked the guard by name."

"That's not talking."

"No. But it's the first time either of them has treated the guards as people rather than furniture." Jihyo's tone didn't change, but something in it sharpened slightly. "People who treat guards as people are people who are thinking about what comes next."

Chaeyoung understood. "And Callen's allies. The ones we took in the corridor. Still nothing?"

"Still nothing. Though _nothing_ looks different now than it did a month ago." Jihyo took a drink from her flask. "In the beginning it was professional silence — controlled, deliberate, the silence of someone following a protocol. Now it's something else. More like someone weighing what they know against what happens if they say it."

"They're scared of whoever's above Callen."

"More scared of them than of us, yes." Jihyo handed her the flask. "We're working on changing that."

Chaeyoung thought about the mailbox stone. The four allied traitors who arrived with the autumn rotation. The network that had been building since before she came to the castle — maybe longer.

"The prisoner from the infiltration," she said. "Chapter Three. Is he still—"

"Alive. Still not talking." Jihyo picked up her water flask from the ground, took a drink. Offered it. Chaeyoung took it. "Though _not talking_ has started to look different. Less professional silence, more someone who's weighing the cost of what they know against what happens if they say it."

"They're scared of whoever's above Callen."

"More scared of them than of us, yes." Jihyo took the flask back. "We're working on changing the math."

The sun was coming up properly now. The yard had that quality of early morning light that made the stone look carved rather than built, everything sharp-edged and temporary. In an hour the castle would be awake and the yard would fill with the day's training rotations and none of this conversation would have happened.

"The new recruits," Chaeyoung said. "When do they arrive?"

"End of the month. Twelve of them." Jihyo's voice shifted slightly — not warmer exactly, but more present, the way it got when she was thinking about something that mattered to her rather than something that required managing. "Younger than the last rotation. The kingdom's producing knights faster than it's producing experienced ones, which means the training burden falls—"

"On us."

"On you, specifically." Jihyo looked at her with the expression she wore when she was assigning something she knew would cost something. "I need someone who can run the physical assessments and the early forms work. Someone the recruits will take seriously without needing rank to make them."

Chaeyoung understood the shape of what was being offered. Not a promotion. Not a formal reassignment. Something more like a trust placed in a particular direction. "And Her Highness?"

"The morning sessions. Before she's up." Jihyo gestured at the empty yard. "Like this. The recruits come in at six. You'd be done before seven." A pause. "It won't affect your duties. But I need to know you can do both."

"I can do both."

Jihyo nodded. The way she nodded when she'd already known the answer and had asked anyway because the asking was part of the record. "One more thing."

"Captain."

"Nayeon asked me last week how it was going." Jihyo said it without preamble. "Her Highness Princess Mina. The assignment." She looked at Chaeyoung with the directness that was her default in private and her deliberate choice in public. "I gave her a report. I'm giving you the same one."

Chaeyoung waited.

"The work is good. Better than good." Jihyo's voice had the particular quality of a compliment from someone who didn't give them for comfort. "You've recalibrated your instincts for a different kind of threat — internal, proximate, slower-building. The infiltration, Callen, the night of the fire. Every decision you made was the right one." A pause. "Including the ones that cost you."

The shoulder. The six steps against the pressure in the room. The blood on the floor from the nose she hadn't mentioned in any report.

"What you're doing now," Jihyo continued, "isn't just guarding a princess. You know that."

"I know that."

"Good." Jihyo picked up her practice sword again. Signaled for one more round with the tilt of her head. "Then act like it. And fix the compensation on the left side before the recruits get here, because the first thing they're going to do is look for the crack."

Chaeyoung raised her sword.

"Yes, Captain."

They ran the last form as the castle woke up around them — the distant sounds assembling themselves into a day, cart wheels and voices and the bells of the chapel marking the hour. The sun finished arriving. The yard filled with light.

Somewhere in the royal wing, Sana's birthday was about to begin.
## Scene 2 — "The Morning Of"

The antechamber door was open when Chaeyoung returned.

Not ajar — open. The full width of a person who had passed through it without bothering to pull it shut behind them, which meant Mina was already up, which meant either something was wrong or Mina had woken before seven for the first time since Chaeyoung had known her.

She stepped inside. The antechamber was empty. Her sword was on the hook where she'd left it; the water pitcher was untouched. Through the connecting door she could hear the specific quality of silence that meant occupied rather than absent — small sounds, the soft drag of something across a surface, the particular stillness of someone in a room being careful not to make noise.

Chaeyoung knocked on the frame. "Your Highness."

"Come in."

Mina was at the desk. Not dressed yet — still in the sleeping robe, dark blue, with her hair loose down her back in the way Chaeyoung had only ever seen on the terrace before dawn or after the last candle. She had a book open in front of her, but she wasn't reading it. She was looking at her own hands, fingers arranged in a sign Chaeyoung recognized after a beat: the third letter. The one with the difficult pinky angle.

She was practicing.

Chaeyoung stood in the doorway and said nothing for a moment. Filed the image in the place with no name, beside everything else.

"You're up early," she said.

Mina looked up. Took in Chaeyoung's state — training clothes, hair pulled back with the slightly collapsed braid of someone who'd been moving for an hour, a sheen of sweat at her temple that the morning air hadn't dried yet — with the calm, complete attention she gave to everything.

"So are you," Mina said.

"I'm always up early."

"I know. I hear you leave." She said it without inflection, a simple fact, and returned her attention to her hand. The pinky adjusted. Almost right. "How's the shoulder?"

"Fine."

Mina looked at her again. The look that meant _that's not an answer._

"Better," Chaeyoung said. "Jihyo made me run the left side for twenty minutes. It held."

"Did it hurt?"

"It always hurts a little."

"That's also not an answer."

Chaeyoung leaned against the doorframe. "Seven out of ten. Down from last week's eight."

Mina nodded, apparently satisfied with the number. She looked back at her hand. The third letter. The pinky still not quite there.

"You're bending the knuckle too much," Chaeyoung said.

Mina stopped. Looked at her hand. "I thought that was the correct position."

"It's close. The bend is here—" Chaeyoung crossed the room without deciding to cross it, which was a thing that happened with increasing frequency in Mina's spaces, and took her hand. Three seconds, the same three seconds as every night on the terrace, adjusting the pinky, rotating the wrist a fraction. "There."

Mina held the position after Chaeyoung let go. Studied it. "That does look different."

"It is different. Half a centimeter, but sign language reads the half centimeter."

"You've been practicing."

"You've been teaching."

Mina almost smiled. The almost one, the real one, the one Chaeyoung had spent weeks learning to produce and still hadn't entirely figured out how. She turned back to the book she wasn't reading, and Chaeyoung became aware that she was still standing next to the desk, which was not the doorframe, which was not her post.

She took a step back. Stopped when Mina spoke.

"Sana's birthday is today."

"I know."

"Nayeon is going to make it complicated."

"Nayeon makes everything complicated."

"She means well."

"She always means well. It doesn't make it less complicated."

Mina looked up at her with an expression that was trying not to be amused and failing in the specific way she failed at things — quietly, almost invisibly, only detectable if you'd been paying close attention for two months. "There will be dancing."

"Nayeon mentioned it."

"Jihyo mentioned it to Nayeon."

Chaeyoung went still. "Jihyo mentioned—"

"That you know how." Mina turned a page of the book. Not reading it. "She said it in passing. I don't think she intended for me to use the information."

"She definitely intended for you to use the information."

"Probably." The page turned again. "I'm not going to make you dance."

"I know."

"I'm simply making note of the fact that the option exists."

"Your Highness."

"Chaeyoung."

The name without the title. Said in the same voice as last night on the terrace, the voice that didn't have layers, the one that came out when Mina wasn't performing composure but simply being herself, which on the best mornings was the only version of her that existed in this room.

Chaeyoung looked at her. Mina looked at the book.

"Go get ready," Mina said. "You smell like training."

"That's — yes. I'll—" She took another step back toward the antechamber. "The celebration starts at noon."

"Nayeon said eleven."

"Then eleven."

"And the dancing."

"I'm not dancing."

"Of course not," Mina said. She turned one more page. The almost-smile still there at the corner of her mouth, not trying to hide and not trying to show, simply present the way things are present when they've stopped needing permission to exist.

Chaeyoung returned to the antechamber. Closed the connecting door — not all the way — and stood for a moment with her hand still on the wood.

In the place with no name, something settled. Not a new thing. Just an old thing finding its proper weight.

She went to wash up.
