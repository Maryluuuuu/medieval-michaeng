The days found their shape.

Not all at once; routine didn't build itself like a wall, brick upon brick, but like a river finding its course. Slowly, along the path of least resistance, until one day Chaeyoung realized she no longer had to think about where to go or what came next. Her body knew.

Dawn. Training with Jihyo in the yard; the forms turned into light sparring as her side healed, and the light sparring turned into real sparring as Jihyo stopped showing mercy. Bathing. Putting on the uniform. Walking to the royal wing, where the ceilings rose and the floors changed, and everything still smelled of things she couldn't name. Taking her place beside Mina's door. Waiting.

Mina always came out at the same time. Always with at least one book under her arm. Always with that expression of non-surprise at finding her there that had become something like a greeting.

"Good morning, Knight Chaeyoung."

"Good morning, Your Highness."

And they walked.

Breakfasts. Nayeon at the head with her scrolls, Sana arriving late with excuses no one believed, Mina eating in silence while her sisters filled the room with a dynamic Chaeyoung had begun to understand without anyone needing to explain it. Nayeon was the rudder. Sana was the wind. Mina was the anchor no one saw but without which the ship would drift.

The library. Hours of silence that Chaeyoung learned to inhabit instead of fight. She stopped counting tiles. Stopped counting books. Instead, she learned to notice the small things: what kind of book Mina chose depending on her mood, how the rhythm of the pages quickened when something excited her, how sometimes she would pause on a line and reread it two, three times, her lips moving in that silent whisper that Chaeyoung had stopped pretending she wasn't watching.

She never fell asleep again. She found her own rhythm in the silence, a state halfway between alertness and calm that she didn't know existed before this job.

The garden. The walks grew slower, longer. Mina began pointing things out: the name of a flower, the history of a tree, the biggest fish in the pond that she had named Captain because it swam like the others reported to it. Chaeyoung listened. Sometimes she nodded. Sometimes she asked a question. Sometimes, without realizing it, she smiled, and Mina filed it away every time without saying a word.

And the nights. The nights were the terrace.

The terrace where they had met, the one with the tea and the open sky and the candles that flickered when the wind decided to remind them it existed. Mina read poetry there, sometimes in silence, sometimes aloud, the verses floating in the night air like things that weighed and flew at the same time. Chaeyoung stood at first. Then in the chair. Mina never told her to sit; there simply stopped being one empty cup on the table and started being two full ones, and Chaeyoung stopped declining an invitation no one had extended.

They didn't talk much on the terrace. They didn't need to. The silence there was different from the library's, softer, more personal. Like a conversation in a language that didn't need words.

The changes were small. So small that Chaeyoung wouldn't have noticed them if she weren't trained to notice things.

Mina stopped calling her Knight Chaeyoung all the time. Sometimes it was just Chaeyoung, without title, without formality. Always when they were alone, never in front of anyone else. Like a secret they shared without having agreed to share it.

Chaeyoung stopped answering in monosyllables. Her responses gained a word, then two, then whole sentences that sometimes included opinions, real opinions, not the diplomatic version she gave to royalty. Mina never commented on the change. She just listened with that complete attention of hers, as if every word Chaeyoung said were a line of poetry that deserved to be read twice.

Mina stopped asking personal questions directly. Instead, she offered things of her own, details about her childhood, about her favorite books, about the time Sana tried to teach her to dance and they both ended up on the floor, and she left space for Chaeyoung to respond with something of her own if she wanted. Sometimes she wanted. Sometimes she didn't. Mina accepted both options with the same grace.

And Chaeyoung began to anticipate. She knew what book Mina would choose before she took it. She knew when she'd want to go to the garden by the way she looked at the window. She knew that when Mina turned the page slower than usual, she was about to read a verse aloud. She knew that when she tucked the strand of hair behind her ear with more force than usual, something was bothering her and she wasn't going to say it.

She knew all of this.

What she didn't know, what she wouldn't allow herself to know, what she pushed to the back of her mind every time it surfaced, was why she cared.

_It's your job,_ she told herself every night, walking back to the east wing after the terrace, the taste of Mina's tea still on her lips. _Knowing her patterns. Her movements. It's your job._

Her father's voice said nothing. Which, coming from him, was his way of saying he didn't believe her.

That night, the terrace was prettier than usual.

Mina couldn't have said why. It was the same terrace as always, the same stone columns, the same table with the same candles, the same sky stretching over the kingdom like a dark blanket embroidered with stars. But there was something in the air that night, a stillness that felt stiller than normal, a softness in the breeze that made the candle flames barely move. As if the world were taking a deliberate pause.

Before what, she didn't know yet.

Mina was in her usual spot, book open in her lap, cup of tea between her hands. She wasn't reading. She'd been on the same page for several minutes, eyes passing over the lines without registering a single word, because her attention was somewhere else.

Chaeyoung was sitting across from her.

She had stopped resisting the chair several days ago; the process had been gradual and silent, like everything that happened between them. The first night, standing. The second, leaning against the column. The third, in the chair but not touching the tea. The fourth, with the cup in her hands but not drinking. And at some point between the fifth and sixth nights, Chaeyoung simply sat down when they reached the terrace, took her cup, and the world kept turning.

Tonight, the knight's gaze was lost in the distant mountains, her profile lit by candlelight. There was something different about her when they were here, something that loosened, like a knot easing half a turn. The jaw less clenched. The shoulders a centimeter lower. The eyes less busy sweeping the perimeter and more willing to simply look.

Mina took a sip of tea. Turned the page of the book she wasn't reading. And decided that tonight was as good as any other to pull the thorn she'd had lodged since the first day.

"Chaeyoung."

"Yes, Your Highness?"

"I wanted to apologize. For my sister."

Chaeyoung turned her head. Confusion crossed her face like a quick shadow. "Apologize?"

"Sana. In the garden. The first day." Mina took another sip of tea, searching for the right way to say this without saying what she was really saying. "She's… like that. With everyone. She has no filter. She says the first thing that comes to mind, regardless of whether it's appropriate or not."

"Oh…"

"And what she said about…" Mina made a vague gesture with her hand meant to encompass the entirety of the comment without having to repeat it. "…that. It wasn't… I mean, she didn't mean to imply…"

She stopped. She was being as eloquent as a rock.

Chaeyoung was looking at her with an expression Mina couldn't entirely decipher. It wasn't discomfort. It wasn't amusement either. It was something in between, something that lived in that territory the knight inhabited frequently, the space between what she felt and what she showed.

"It's not important, Your Highness," Chaeyoung said. Simple. Clean. No embellishment.

"Really?"

"Your sister was kind. A little… direct, maybe. But kind."

"Sana would be delighted to hear you consider her kind. Most people use other words."

Something crossed Chaeyoung's mouth, that gesture Mina already knew, the smile that didn't quite become a smile, the halfway point that had become her favorite expression to catalog.

"Then you have nothing to apologize for, Your Highness. Truly."

Mina left it there. There was nothing more to say without entering territory she wasn't ready to explore, the territory of why exactly Sana's comment bothered her so much, and why exactly she had spent a week thinking about it, and why exactly she needed to know whether Chaeyoung had heard it or not.

_Because it's embarrassing,_ she told herself. _That's all. It's embarrassing that your sister implies things in front of your guard._

It was a perfectly reasonable explanation. Mina accepted it with the same conviction with which she accepted Chaeyoung's rounded truths.

The silence that returned to the terrace was comfortable. The kind of silence they had built without meaning to over the course of the week, made of shared nights, of pages read aloud, of cups of tea growing cold while the sky changed color above them.

Mina returned to the book. This time she actually read. A poem about the sea, something she had never seen but imagined with the stubborn certainty of someone who has read enough about something to feel they know it.

"May I ask you something, Your Highness?"

Mina looked up. Chaeyoung wasn't looking at her; she was looking at her teacup, turning it between her fingers with a slow, almost meditative movement.

"Always."

"Why the terrace?"

"What do you mean?"

"You could read in your room. Or the library. Or the garden. But every night you come here." Chaeyoung raised her eyes from the cup. Her gaze met Mina's, and for once there was nothing professional in the look. Just curiosity.

Simple, honest. "Why?"

Mina considered the answer. The real one. Not the rounded version.

"Because it's the only place in the castle that doesn't have a ceiling," she said. "In my room there's a ceiling. In the library there's a ceiling. In the hallways, the dining hall, everywhere. There's a ceiling." She looked up, at the open sky with its stars. "Not here. Here I can see that there's something beyond the walls. Even if I can't go."

Chaeyoung didn't respond immediately. Mina watched her process the words with that seriousness she brought to everything, as if each thing Mina said deserved to be weighed before answering.

"That makes sense," she said at last. Quiet. Without judgment.

"It does?"

"Yes." A pause. "I have something similar. But with the training yards."

"The training yards…?"

"They don't have a… ceiling either." Genuine honesty across Chaeyoung face, “It just feel like I can be more myself, feels like… home I guess”

They look at eachother, and something happened, something that wasn't a smile but lived in the same house, used the same rooms, sat at the same table. An understanding. Small, fragile, the kind of thing that breaks if you name it. Neither of them named it.

Mina went back to the book. Chaeyoung went back to the tea. The candles kept flickering. The sky kept being there, open, infinite, reminding them both that a world existed larger than this castle and smaller than this terrace. A while later, it could have been half an hour or an hour, time on the terrace didn't work as it did in other places, Mina closed the book gently.

"It's late," she said, though it wasn't particularly late. It was simply time to go. She knew it the same way she knew when a page needed to be turned.

Chaeyoung stood before she did. She always did, a reflex Mina suspected was more instinct than protocol.

They walked back along the torch-lit path. The royal wing hallways were empty at this hour, just shadows, statues, and the echo of their steps on polished floor.

They stopped in front of Mina's door. The carved vines on the frame received them as always, still and patient.

"Good night, Chaeyoung."

"Good night, Your Highness."

No title. Just Chaeyoung. The bare name, without the armor of protocol. Mina had been saying it this way every night for three days, and every night Chaeyoung replied with the usual Your Highness, and neither of them commented on the asymmetry.

Mina entered her chambers. Closed the door. Leaned against it for a moment, book pressed to her chest, listening to Chaeyoung's footsteps retreat down the hallway, the sound of boots on polished floor that was already, without her having realized it, one of the things that marked the end of her day.

She changed. Washed her face. Got into bed. The sheets were cold at first and then took her temperature, as always. She closed her eyes.

The castle slept. The guards made their rounds. The candles burned down. The silence thickened.

Mina fell asleep thinking about roofless terraces and training yards.

Outside, in the hallway, Chaeyoung took her post.

The royal wing hallway was a different animal at night.

During the day, there was movement, servants, rotating guards, the distant echo of the kingdom's affairs resonating through the stone corridors. At night, all of that disappeared. What remained were the torches, spaced every ten meters, casting pools of orange light between sections of darkness. What remained were the statues of dead kings and queens, staring from their pedestals with marble eyes that didn't blink. And what remained was Chaeyoung. Standing. Back to the wall. Three steps from the door with the carved vines.

She'd been doing this for a week and already had the map of nocturnal silence engraved in her mind. She knew that at the first hour the courtyard guards changed shifts; she could hear the metallic echo of their steps on the distant staircase. She knew that the second hour was the quietest, no wind, no movement, just the slow breathing of the sleeping castle. She knew that the third hour brought the mice, scurrying between the walls with an urgency that always struck her as somewhat comical for such small animals.

She knew which sounds belonged to the night and which didn't.

That was the advantage of standing night guard with the same insomnia she'd carried since she was fifteen; you learned to read silence the way others read books.

Speaking of books.

Chaeyoung let her head rest against the stone. Behind the door, Mina was sleeping. She knew because exactly forty minutes ago the princess had blown out the last candle; the thread of light under the door had faded with that particular slowness of a flame someone blows from the bed, too comfortable to get up.

_Because it's the only place in the castle that doesn't have a ceiling._

Mina's words were still there, circling somewhere in her head that should have been occupied with more useful things. Like watching. Like staying alert. Like doing the job she was being paid for.

_Here I can see that there's something beyond the walls. Even if I can't go._

Chaeyoung frowned.

It wasn't her problem. The princess had her terrace, her books, her poetry. Chaeyoung had her post, her sword, and a vine-carved door to protect. That was the line. Clear, simple, professional.

_The training yards?_

_They don't have a ceiling either. Feels like home, I guess…_

She had answered without thinking. She'd given her something real, not a rounded response, not a "Yes, Your Highness," but something that came from a place she didn't usually open for anyone. And Mina had received it with that gaze of hers, the one that made Chaeyoung feel she was being read with the same attention the princess gave her damned books.

_Enough._

She readjusted against the wall. Settled her hand on her sword's hilt. Breathed.

_Dad, your daughter is standing guard outside a princess's door thinking about ceilings. You'd be proud._

A blurred image. Her father's smile, crooked, with that quiet humor Chaeyoung had inherited and buried under layers of discipline. _I'm proud of everything you do, little one._ The voice was getting more indistinct with each passing month. Losing a nuance, a tone, a texture she wasn't going to get back.

Chaeyoung clenched her jaw. Pushed the thought away.

She listened.

Silence. The right kind of silence, the second-hour kind, dense and uniform. The torches crackled with their usual rhythm. The air moved with the royal wing's habitual current, that barely perceptible flow that came from the corridor's high windows.

Everything in order.

Except it wasn't.

She couldn't identify what came first, whether she heard it or felt it. Because they were almost simultaneous: a sound and a sensation, arriving at the same time from different directions.

The sound was small. Almost nothing. A change in the air current, not the hallway's current but another one, one that shouldn't exist. As if somewhere nearby something that had been closed was now open. A window. An entrance. A gap where the outside was seeping inward, carrying with it a breeze with a different smell, damp stone, open night, something that didn't belong inside the sealed interior of the royal wing.

The sensation was worse.

It started in her chest. Not as pain, as pressure. As if something inside her ribs had tightened half a turn, an invisible nut torquing against her sternum. An urgency without a name, without an origin, without logic. The kind of thing she couldn't point to or explain with words, but that screamed at her with the clarity of an alarm bell.

_Something is wrong._

Her fingers closed around the hilt. It wasn't a decision; it was reflex, the same one that made her raise a shield before seeing the blow, the same one that had kept her alive in every battle she shouldn't have survived.

Her father had that too. Moments where his body moved before his mind, where something in the blood reacted to a danger the eyes couldn't yet see. As a child, she had watched him stop in the middle of a forest trail, hand raised, eyes fixed on nothing, and three seconds later a boar would burst from the bushes exactly where he was looking. _It's just instinct,_ she had told herself every time it happened. _Experience. Training. Nothing more._

But tonight, the pressure in her chest didn't feel like trained instinct. It felt like something older, deeper, something that existed in her before she learned to hold a sword.

The air current shifted again. Subtle. Someone who hadn't spent a week cataloging every sound in this hallway wouldn't have noticed.

Chaeyoung noticed.

It came from the left. From the corridor leading to the inner garden, where the windows were lower and the walls bordered the castle's exterior. The least guarded route in the royal wing, something anyone with access to the castle blueprints could know, but that only someone who had studied the guard patterns would know when to exploit.

The pressure in her chest intensified. It wasn't pain. It was direction. Like a compass pointing toward a north she couldn't see but felt with every fiber of her body.

And that north pointed to the door behind her.

To Mina.

Chaeyoung drew in silence. The steel made no sound leaving the scabbard; it never did when she wanted it not to.

She peeled away from the wall. Three steps to the door. Her boots made no sound on the polished tiles, another thing she'd perfected over years of night marches and silent ambushes.

She stopped in front of the door. Put her left hand on the carved wood, fingers among the sculpted vines. Listened. Nothing.

No, not nothing. Something worse than nothing. The silence inside the room was different from a moment ago. Before, it was the silence of someone sleeping, soft, rhythmic, alive. Now it was the silence of someone holding their breath.

Or of someone who shouldn't be there trying not to make noise.

The pressure in her chest became certainty.

Chaeyoung didn't knock. Didn't call out. Didn't ask permission.

She opened the door.

The darkness inside the room was nearly total.

The curtains were drawn; Mina closed them every night, Chaeyoung knew because she had watched her do it from the doorway for a week. Only a thread of moonlight entered from the lower edge, a silver streak cutting across the stone floor like a thin scar. Enough to distinguish shapes. Not enough to see faces.

Chaeyoung didn't need to see faces.

She needed to see movement. And she saw it.

Two figures. One by the window, the source of the air current, the glass opened with the precision of someone who knew exactly which lock to cut. The other closer to the bed, advancing with short, measured steps, the kind of footfalls that make no noise from training, not luck.

_Professionals._

The thought crossed her mind and left. There was no time to analyze it. There was no time for anything except what her body was already doing.

She moved.

The one closest to the bed saw her first. Chaeyoung knew by the way his silhouette tensed, a minimal shift in posture, shoulders squaring, weight redistributing to the balls of his feet. But by the time he finished registering that someone else was in the room, Chaeyoung was already on him.

She clamped her free hand over his mouth. Drove her elbow into his solar plexus with enough force to empty him of air; she felt the blow connect, the body double over, the choked attempt to scream muffled against her palm. She hooked his foot and took him to the ground, controlling the fall so the impact was muffled against the carpet instead of bone against stone. Once down, she struck a sharp blow with the sword's pommel to his temple. The body went slack.

Silence. She had to maintain silence. If Mina woke now and screamed, the second one would use the chaos. If she made too much noise, the infiltrators would change strategy, and Chaeyoung didn't know what the backup plan was for people who scaled castle walls in the dark.

One second. Maybe two. That was all she had before the second one moved.

This one didn't charge head-on. He slid to the side, a lateral, controlled movement, seeking the angle where Chaeyoung couldn't use the length of her sword. Smart. Mina's room was spacious for a bedroom and small for a battlefield; shelves of books to the left, vanity to the right, the bed at the back. A full arc with the blade would graze the walls, knock things over, break the silence. Which meant fighting with half a sword, point and short movements, sacrificing the reach advantage.

The infiltrator knew this. That's why he carried a short knife, wide blade. The weapon of someone who knew how to fight between walls.

He attacked low. A diagonal cut toward the legs, looking for mobility, wanting to limit her. Chaeyoung pulled her leg back and deflected with the flat of the blade, redirecting the edge outward. The clash of metal was a contained sound that died in the heavy curtains. The man turned his wrist; the knife changed angle before the deflection finished, a fluid correction that said training, repetition, thousands of hours with that blade. Chaeyoung blocked with her forearm and pushed, trying to create space.

Then something changed. The same thing as in the hallway, but stronger. The pressure in her chest expanded and the world adjusted; the shadows gained texture, the edges of darkness sharpened, and she could see with a clarity that had no explanation. The contours of the man in front of her came into focus: the stance, his weight distribution, the pulse beating in the vein of his neck.

_His right shoulder drops a millimeter before he attacks with his left._

She didn't know how she knew that. She didn't care.

She used the information. Read the attack before it existed, got ahead of the movement, drove a short blow with the pommel into his ribs. The man absorbed the impact with a choked grunt, but didn't retreat. He caught her sword wrist. And before Chaeyoung could free herself, he turned.

It was a simple movement. Efficient. He twisted her wrist inward, forcing the blade down, and in the same turn closed the distance between them until they were chest to chest, too close for steel to be of any use.

The knife came up.

Chaeyoung saw it. Saw it with that strange clarity that showed her everything in slow motion and too fast at the same time. The blade ascending in a short arc toward her left shoulder, the space between collarbone and neck where the uniform had no protection.

She let go of the sword.

The decision was instant. The sword was useless at this distance; what she needed were both hands. She caught the knife wrist with both, stopping the edge centimeters from her body.

And then it was force against force.

The man pushed down. Chaeyoung pushed out. Both their arms trembled with the effort; she could feel every tendon taut, every muscle fighting for half a centimeter, the vibration of the knife between them like a harp string about to snap.

He was stronger than her. Not by much, but enough. Enough for the blade to advance. Millimeter by millimeter, with the horrible slowness of something inevitable, the edge closed the distance between the air and her skin.

Chaeyoung gritted her teeth. Adjusted her grip. Pushed with everything she had, legs planted on the floor, shoulders tilted forward, core burning. The knife stopped. One second. Two.

And then her foot slipped.

Half a centimeter. Nothing. But it was enough to break the balance, for the man to gain the angle he needed, and the blade found flesh.

It went in slowly.

Not like in battles; in battles, wounds arrive fast, explosive, an instant of nothing and then pain. This was different. This was feeling the steel open the skin, part the muscle, sink centimeter by centimeter into the shoulder while every fiber of her body screamed to pull it out, to move away, to do something.

She didn't scream.

A sound came out, something between a grunt and a forced exhale, something born at the bottom of her throat and dying clenched between her teeth. But she didn't scream. Because Mina was asleep three meters away and if she screamed she'd wake her and if she woke…

The pain flooded her shoulder like boiling water poured inside the bone. Her left hand lost strength. The fingers loosened on the man's wrist, yielding, and Chaeyoung knew she had exactly one second before he withdrew the blade and stabbed again.

She used that second.

She stopped resisting. The man expected opposition; when it disappeared, his own momentum threw him off balance. Chaeyoung turned her body, used the man's weight against him, and hurled him aside with what was left of her strength in her right arm and legs.

The infiltrator flew.

He crashed into the bookshelf with a thunderous crack that shattered the night like glass. Wood splintering. Books falling. The sound reverberating through the room, through the hallway, through the entire wing.

In the bed, Mina sat up with a start.

"What—?"

_No. No, no, no._

Chaeyoung was already moving. The knife was still in her shoulder; she felt it with every step, a hot weight pulsing to the rhythm of her heart. She ignored it. Ignored the arm hanging limp, the blood beginning to soak the fabric, the dark edge eating at the periphery of her vision. She reached the bed in three strides.

"Your Highness, come with me. Now."

Her voice came out steadier than it had any right to be. A command, not a request. The voice of someone who'd been in enough battles to separate what she felt from what she did.

Mina was frozen. Eyes enormous, bright with a fear Chaeyoung had never seen on her, the kind of fear that's born not from imagination or books but from a violent awakening to the reality that someone wants to hurt you.

"Chaeyoung, you're—"

"Now, Your Highness. Please."

Something in the tone, or perhaps something in Chaeyoung's eyes, something Mina read with that ability of hers to see what others hide, made her move. She got out of bed. Chaeyoung took her arm with her right hand and put her behind her, between her body and the wall. Human shield. Instinctive position. She didn't think it; she simply executed it, because a part of her knew that was the only place Mina should be.

The infiltrator from the shelf was getting up.

Books at his feet. Splinters of wood in his hair. But he stood, with that empty determination of professionals, no visible pain, no hesitation, like a machine resetting itself. He found the sword that had fallen to the floor when Chaeyoung tried to resist the stab. He picked it up.

And charged.

Not at Chaeyoung. At them. At the space they occupied against the wall, where Mina was pressed to her back and Chaeyoung could feel the princess's trembling transmitting to her own body.

Chaeyoung pulled the knife from her shoulder.

She did it with her right hand, in one pull, without thinking; thinking would have meant not doing it. The pain was white. Total. A lightning bolt that crossed her entire body, filled her mouth with a metallic taste, and put black spots in her vision. Her knees wanted to give. She forced them to stay where they were.

The infiltrator was four meters away. Three. Two.

Chaeyoung threw.

It wasn't a textbook throw. It wasn't technique, it wasn't form, it wasn't anything they'd taught her in the training yards. It was her body moving from somewhere deeper than muscle, something ancient, something that lived in the blood and responded to a single directive: _protect her._

The knife turned once in the air.

It embedded in the infiltrator's forehead.

The man stopped. On his feet. Eyes open, emptying. The body took a second to learn it was already dead; it staggered forward, a mechanical step going nowhere, and then fell. Face-first. The sound of body against floor was final.

Chaeyoung exhaled.

Her hand was shaking. All of her was shaking: the arm, the shoulder, the legs, the jaw. The clear vision had switched off, leaving her with normal darkness and an exhaustion that weighed in her bones like liquid lead. The shoulder throbbed with its own heartbeat, exposed to the air, bleeding freely now that nothing was plugging the wound. Behind her, Mina breathed in short, quick gasps. The sound of someone processing terror who doesn't have enough air to do it.

Chaeyoung turned to face her. Put her body between Mina and the corpse. Blocked the view, an instinctive gesture she didn't think about, simply executed, because a part of her knew that image was something that couldn't be unseen.

"It´s alright, Your Highness. It´s alright. It's over."

A lie. It wasn't over. Because while she said those words, her eyes searched for the first infiltrator, the one she'd neutralized at the start, the one who should have been unconscious on the floor by the door.

He wasn't.

Chaeyoung saw him at the same instant she lost him, a shadow at the edge of the window, a silhouette launching itself outward with a fluid, rehearsed movement. For a fraction of a second their eyes met in the darkness, the infiltrator's and hers, and Chaeyoung saw something that chilled her blood more than the knife.

Calm. The man was perfectly calm. No rush, no panic, no desperation of someone fleeing. He was leaving because the job had failed, not because he was afraid. It was a tactical retreat. Clean. Professional.

And then he disappeared. The sound of a body landing on something soft, a rooftop, a vine, an escape route planned in advance, and then nothing. Just the night.

He was gone.

She wanted to follow him. Everything in her wanted to follow, the muscles, the instinct, the fury of knowing that one of the men who had entered this room to take Mina was free, running, vanishing into the darkness with information about the royal wing's layout, the blind spots, about her.

But Mina was trembling against the wall behind her. And Chaeyoung knew, with the same certainty with which she knew how to grip a sword, that she was not going to leave her alone in this room.

She turned. Faced Mina. Blocked her view of the corpse, the blood, everything that should never have been in the bedroom of a princess who an hour ago was reading poetry about the sea.

"Your Highness. Look at me."

Mina looked at her. Eyes bright, face pale, hands pressed against her chest. Trembling with the kind of tremor that doesn't come from cold. But she looked. With those eyes that saw everything, that read everything, that in this moment were registering the blood on Chaeyoung's uniform, the arm hanging limp, the knife missing from the shoulder and the open wound that replaced it.

"I'm fine," Chaeyoung repeated. The voice a little less steady.

Mina said nothing. She just looked. And Chaeyoung had the uncomfortable feeling that, of the two of them, Mina was the one seeing more clearly.

Chaeyoung stepped into the hallway without letting go of the door. Breathed once. Twice.

And shouted.
