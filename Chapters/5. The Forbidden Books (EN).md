The bed was too short.

Chaeyoung had discovered this the first night, when her feet hung over the edge and the cold of the stone bit at her ankles. She’d solved it by folding the spare blanket into a rectangle that served as an improvised extension. It wasn’t comfortable.

The antechamber was small. More storage room than bedroom, with a low ceiling that turned the space into a stone box designed to hold dresses, not people. They’d removed the clothing trunks to make room. A military bed, a water jug, a candelabra without candles because Chaeyoung slept in the dark, and her sword leaning against the wall less than an arm’s length away. That was all.

And the door.

The interior door connecting to Mina’s chambers. Oak, uncarved, without the decorative vines of the original door in the royal wing. Functional. Honest. With a latch that the princess hadn’t locked either of the two nights.

Fifteen steps. Chaeyoung had counted them the first morning, from her bed to Mina’s bed, through the door, across the small vestibule, to the edge of the mattress where the princess slept. Fifteen steps that had previously been an entire corridor, an oak door carved with vines, and the professional distance that comes with sleeping in one place and standing guard in another.

Now they were fifteen steps and an unlatched door.

_Don’t think about that._

Chaeyoung opened her eyes.

The darkness of the antechamber was absolute. Without windows, the only temporal reference was sound. And sound, at this hour, said: pre-dawn. She could hear the changing of the guard in the exterior hallway, the muffled footsteps of the north wing sentinels exchanging positions with the silent efficiency that Jihyo had drilled into every soldier under her command. First shift ending. That meant one hour until sunrise.

She sat up on the bed. The left shoulder protested immediately. Not with the sharp pain of the first night but with something more subtle, more insidious. A deep pull that reminded her that Tzuyu’s ten stitches were doing their job and that the job hurt. The moonblossom tincture helped with the inflammation but not with the stiffness, and after hours of immobility the muscle felt like wet rope someone had wrung out.

Chaeyoung flexed her arm. Slowly. Centimeter by centimeter, measuring the range of motion the way one measures the edge of a sword, finding the exact point where functional ends and dangerous begins.

_More than yesterday. Good._

She stood. Washed her face with the jug water. The cold cleared her head, as always, that first contact of icy water against skin that was the closest thing to a cup of tea she could afford at this hour. She dressed with the efficiency of someone who has practiced the sequence thousands of times: undershirt, service shirt, belt, sword. The clean uniform pinched at the shoulder because of the bandage. She’d have to ask Tzuyu to adjust the wrappings before midday.

She tied her hair back. A movement that normally took three seconds and now took eight, because raising the left arm above her head was a negotiation with pain that required patience and internal vocabulary not appropriate for a lieutenant of the crown.

_You did what you had to do that night._

Mina’s voice. Not yesterday’s, the morning before’s. In the hallway, with dark eyes fixed on the bandaged shoulder and that expression Chaeyoung hadn’t been able to fully classify. It wasn’t Nayeon’s look, which saw the wound as evidence that the system had worked. It wasn’t Jihyo’s, which saw the wound as an acceptable cost. It was something different. Something that looked at the wound and saw the pain, not the result.

_I wish you wouldn’t. Get hurt, I mean._

Chaeyoung finished tying her hair. Adjusted the sword on her belt. Looked at her hands for a second, the same hands Mina had taken in the darkness, the left one, the broken one, not the one being offered, and closed them into fists. Opened. Closed. The fingers responded well. The grip was firm.

_It works. That’s what matters._

She stepped out of the antechamber into the hallway.

The north wing wasn’t the royal wing. The ceilings were lower, the walls unpolished stone, the torches more widely spaced. There was a functional honesty to this place that Chaeyoung appreciated. It was more like the east wing, like the barracks, spaces that didn’t pretend to be more than what they were. But it was still crown territory, and the two guards posted on either side of the main door confirmed it.

Chaeyoung relieved them with a nod. Took her position: back against the wall, three paces from the door, line of sight down the corridor in both directions. The same spot as yesterday. The same spot as always.

But it wasn’t the same.

Before, when she stood guard from the royal wing hallway, Chaeyoung had distance as an ally. A closed door. An entire corridor. The sound of her boots walking away was the last thing Mina heard before sleep, and the first thing she needed to do each morning was walk to that door and open it to find her guard on the other side.

Not now. Now Chaeyoung slept fifteen steps from Mina. She could hear when the princess shifted in bed, the whisper of sheets, so faint that only the absolute silence of the north wing made it audible. She could hear when she blew out the last candle, that soft puff followed by total darkness seeping under the door. She could hear, if the castle was quiet enough, the breathing.

She didn’t listen on purpose. She didn’t stay awake waiting for the sounds. They simply arrived. The way the sounds of the sea arrive when you sleep near the coast. You don’t seek them, but they’re there, and at some point you stop noticing them and at another point you realize you’ve been listening for hours without knowing you were listening.

_That’s not professional._

It wasn’t. But Chaeyoung didn’t know how to stop hearing something that the silence delivered without asking permission.

Dawn entered through the north wing windows with less ceremony than in the royal wing. Without the golden light of stained glass, without the calculated angles designed to illuminate portraits of ancestors. Here the light was direct: a white rectangle advancing across the stone floor like a horizontal clock hand.

Chaeyoung waited.

At some point, the sounds on the other side of the door changed. The rhythm of breathing shifted, shallower, faster. The sound of sheets moving. Feet on the floor. Water. The small rituals of a person waking, so intimate in their banality that Chaeyoung felt like an intruder each time she registered them.

_Stop listening._

She couldn’t. The north wing walls were thinner than the royal wing’s. Another functional detail no one had considered when they decided to move a princess here.

The door opened.

Mina appeared with the reconstructed composure of someone who has spent a lifetime practicing the art of being ready before the world sees her. Hair gathered with that elegant simplicity Chaeyoung had learned required more effort than it appeared. A dress in blue-gray tones, more sober than the ones she usually wore. Because of the north wing? Because of her mood? Chaeyoung couldn’t read dresses, but she could read changes, and this was one.

Mina’s eyes found her. And there it was: the scan. Quick, top to bottom, so brief that someone who wasn’t watching would have missed it. But Chaeyoung was watching, because watching was literally her job, and she saw how Mina’s gaze paused for a millisecond on the left shoulder before completing its sweep and returning to her face.

She didn’t say anything about the shoulder. Didn’t ask how she’d slept. Didn’t mention the night, or the morning before, or the words they’d exchanged in the hallway about getting hurt and wishing she wouldn’t.

“Good morning, Chaeyoung.”

The bare name. No title, no armor of protocol. She’d been doing it since before the attack, three, four days, and each time it landed in a different place in Chaeyoung’s chest. Today it landed soft. Like something beginning to become familiar.

“Good morning, Your Highness.”

The usual asymmetry. Mina without title, Chaeyoung with it. Both aware of the imbalance and neither moving to correct it, like two people standing on opposite sides of a bridge they both know exists but pretend doesn’t.

Mina closed the door behind her. The gesture had something final about it, like closing a book at the end of a chapter. Everything that had happened on the other side of that door, the sounds, the silences, the fifteen steps of distance that felt like five, was sealed. What existed now was the hallway, the morning light, and the princess walking toward breakfast with her guard half a step behind.

But something was different.

Not in Mina, or yes, but in ways Chaeyoung was still learning to read. There were new layers to her composure. Last week’s Mina was serene. Today’s Mina was measured. Serenity is natural; measured is constructed. And Chaeyoung, who made her living distinguishing between what people are and what people show, could feel the difference the way you feel a change in the wind before the banner moves.

Mina was processing something. Something that had started the night of the attack, when she took Chaeyoung’s wounded hand in the darkness, and that had kept growing in silence, like those roots that work underground without anyone seeing them until one day they lift the stone.

Chaeyoung didn’t know what it was. It wasn’t her place to know. Her job was to be there, half a step behind, with the sword on her belt and her eyes on the corridor.

But she felt it. The same way she felt the changes in the nighttime silence, the same way she’d felt the infiltrators before she saw them, with that instinct that had no name and lived in a place older than logic.

Something in Mina was changing.

And something in Chaeyoung, something that also lacked a name, something that stored things in a place that was filling up too fast, was paying attention.

They walked in silence toward the royal dining hall. The north wing corridors gave way to the central wing’s, and the unpolished stone became waxed floor, and the torches became candelabras, and the castle dressed itself as they approached the heart where the crown lived.

Mina didn’t speak. Chaeyoung didn’t speak. The silence between them was the usual one, but the usual one was no longer the same.

It had changed shape.

---

The royal dining hall smelled of freshly baked bread and forced normalcy.

Mina knew it the moment she crossed the door. The table was set as always, but there was something in the arrangement that betrayed the effort. The flowers in the center were new, more elaborate than usual. The teapot was in its exact place, as if someone had measured the distance with a ruler. Everything was where it should be, and precisely that made it feel wrong. Like a room someone has cleaned too well after an accident.

Nayeon was already seated. Not with a scroll this time. Today she had her hands around her tea cup and her eyes on the door. Waiting. The scan was immediate. Mina felt it before she saw it: her sister’s gaze sweeping over her with the efficiency of an inventory. _Did she sleep? Is she whole?_

“Good morning, Mina. How did you sleep?”

The question was new. Nayeon didn’t ask how she slept. Nayeon assumed things worked until someone proved otherwise. The fact that she was asking meant the night of the attack was still crawling beneath the surface of every interaction.

“Very well, thank you.” Mina said. It was a lie, but it was the kind of lie that functions as social currency: both knew it was false, both accepted the exchange, and the conversation could continue.

She sat in her place. Poured tea with the slow, deliberate movements of always, not because she cared about the precision of the pour but because the alternative was looking back, toward where Chaeyoung had positioned herself behind her chair.

Mina could feel her.

It wasn’t something new. She’d been feeling her for days. But before it was easier to categorize. _She’s my guard. It’s natural to be aware of where she is. Anyone would._

Now it was different.

Now she knew what Chaeyoung sounded like when she slept. She’d discovered it accidentally, the first night in the north wing, when the silence was so complete that the sounds from the antechamber seeped through the door like water under a dam. The slow, deep breathing that indicated sleep. The abrupt shift that indicated she’d woken. The creak of the bed. And then, a low, contained hiss, the sound of someone moving in pain and refusing to give it voice.

The shoulder.

Mina had clenched her teeth in the darkness when she heard it. Had closed her eyes and gripped the sheets and felt something that had no name but resembled rage. Not at Chaeyoung. At the situation. At the fact that the person on the other side of that wall had taken a knife for her and the sound she made was _that_, tight and minimal, as if pain were something that could be denied entry to the world if you contained it enough.

She had meant it when she asked her not to. Every syllable. And Chaeyoung’s answer, _I’ll try,_ was worse than any lie, because it meant they both knew the attempt had an expiration date. That the next time something threatened Mina, Chaeyoung would step in front with the same naturalness with which she breathed.

And that was what Mina couldn’t stop thinking about.

Not the attack. Not the infiltrators. That was terrible, but it was something that had happened to _her_, something she could process with the tools she had: logic, composure, internal boxes with orderly labels.

What she couldn’t package was Chaeyoung. The fact that there existed someone who, faced with the choice of crying out in pain or protecting Mina’s sleep, had chosen to swallow the sound. That this wasn’t a calculated heroic act but a reflex, something so integrated into her way of being that she probably hadn’t even thought about it.

Mina had read about this. In poems, in novels, in those texts on courtly love she’d devoured as a teenager with the avidity of someone studying a foreign country she knows she’ll never visit. The knights of the ballads did exactly this: suffer in silence, protect without asking anything, bleed with elegance.

But the knights of the ballads didn’t exist. They were literary constructions, archetypes designed to make the hearts of readers like her beat faster. They weren’t real people with stitched shoulders and hisses at three in the morning.

Chaeyoung was real. And that was infinitely more complicated.

“Mina?”

The teapot was in her hand, tilted, and she realized she’d been pouring tea for several seconds into a cup that was already full. The liquid threatened to overflow.

“Sorry.” She straightened the teapot with a fluid movement, as if the excess had been intentional. “I was thinking.”

“About what?”

_About the sound my guard makes when her shoulder hurts at three in the morning._ “Nothing important.”

Nayeon didn’t press. But her gaze moved for an instant toward Chaeyoung, behind Mina, and then returned. She said nothing. She didn’t need to. Nayeon communicated entire volumes with the movement of her eyes, and what that movement said was: _I’m watching you, sister._

Mina drank her tea. Too hot. She burned her tongue and didn’t react.

The dining hall doors opened.

“Good morning, family!”

Sana. Peach-colored dress, hair down, high-voltage smile. But today the smile was one degree brighter than necessary. One degree Mina recognized immediately. Sana was doing what she did best: being the sun so no one had to look at the clouds.

She dropped into her chair, found the pastries, and attacked an apple one with the concentration of someone on a sacred mission. Nayeon scolded her for being late. Sana responded that the world was running ahead of schedule. The exchange was the usual one, the same music of every morning. And yet Mina could feel the seams. The way Nayeon held her cup with both hands instead of one, as if she needed the extra anchor. The way Sana looked at Mina one second longer than usual before launching each joke, checking, measuring, making sure laughter was possible before trying to provoke it.

They were taking care of her. Both of them, in their own way. Nayeon with control and Sana with light. And Mina loved them for it with a silent ferocity she would never know how to express.

But it also suffocated her. Just a little. Enough to notice.

“How’s your new room?” Sana asked between bites. “I heard the north wing is very… rustic.”

“It’s functional.” Mina said.

“Functional. Nayeon’s favorite word for _ugly but necessary_.”

“The north wing is perfectly adequate.” Nayeon interjected.

“Have you seen the curtains? Because I went by once and the curtains looked like they were made of sadness.”

“Sana.”

“I’m just saying that if my sister has to sleep outside her room for reasons I refuse to consider on an empty stomach, she could at least have curtains that don’t depress her.”

There was something beneath the words. _I refuse to consider_ meant _I can’t think about what happened without something inside me breaking, so I’m going to talk about curtains._ Sana didn’t process fear with silence like Mina or with control like Nayeon. She processed it by turning it into something else, anything else, something she could hold without cutting herself.

“The curtains are fine, Sana,” Mina said softly. “Really.”

Sana looked at her. For one second, just one, the mask slipped. What was underneath wasn’t the fun princess or the sister who lights up the room. **It was a twenty-seven-year-old gir**l who had learned that someone tried to take her little sister in the middle of the night and who didn’t know how to carry that except by making jokes about curtains.

“Good,” Sana said. And she put the mask back on with the speed of someone who has practiced all her life. “But if you change your mind, I know a seamstress who works wonders.”

Breakfast continued with the inertia of the everyday. Safe topics, shallow, the kind of conversation that fills space without demanding anything from anyone.

And behind her, Chaeyoung remained standing. Still. Present.

Mina didn’t look at her. Couldn’t, not with Sana there, not after Nayeon’s glance. But she felt her. She knew when she shifted weight from one foot to the other by the almost imperceptible sound of the belt leather adjusting. She knew when her eyes swept the room in the surveillance scan she did every thirty seconds. And she knew, without needing to turn, that the left shoulder was bothering her, because the interval between weight shifts was shorter on the left side. Three seconds less. Mina had counted them.

_That’s not normal._

Counting the movement intervals of your guard wasn’t normal. She knew that. The same way she knew that memorizing the rhythm of someone’s breathing through a wall wasn’t normal, and that the emptiness she felt in her left palm since the night of the attack, where Chaeyoung’s fingers had been and no longer were, wasn’t normal either.

She had read about this. The involuntary recognition of patterns in another person. Selective attention. The poems called it many things: fixation, sleeplessness, the sweet sickness. The natural philosophy texts described it as an imbalance of humors. The chivalric novels covered it in metaphors about arrows and invisible wounds.

Mina didn’t trust any of those sources. Poets exaggerate. Philosophers simplify. And the chivalric novels had been written by people who clearly had never had to eat breakfast half a meter from the person causing their imbalance of humors.

What she had was a hypothesis. Incomplete, unconfirmed, based on insufficient data. The hypothesis was: something in her attention had changed direction, like a weathervane turned by the wind, and it now pointed consistently toward one specific person. That was observable. That was a fact.

What was not a fact was the meaning. It could be gratitude. It could be the logical result of sharing a traumatic event. It could be the forced proximity, the antechamber, the fifteen steps, the unlatched door. It could be many things that didn’t require a word Mina wasn’t prepared to use.

_It’s gratitude. It’s logical to pay attention to the person who bled for you. Anyone would._

The explanation was clean. Orderly. Perfectly rational.

And she had prepared it too quickly. Like a rehearsed answer for an exam she hadn’t been given yet. Which, if she was honest with herself, was stronger evidence than any of the others.

“Mina.”

Sana. Looking at her with that expression that meant _I just said something and you didn’t hear it._

“Sorry. What were you saying?”

“Whether you’re going to the library today.”

“Yes. After breakfast.”

“Perfect. I want to come see you afterward.” Sana bit another pastry. “I need your opinion on something.”

“On what?”

“On _something_.” The smile had layers. “Sister things.”

Nayeon looked up. “Should I be worried?”

“Nayeon, darling, if you had to worry every time I wanted to talk to Mina, you’d never sleep.” Sana paused. “Well. Less than you already don’t sleep.”

The comment landed with more weight than she probably intended. Nayeon’s eyes hardened half a degree. Sana saw it, registered it, and changed the subject with the fluidity of someone accustomed to reading the temperature of a room.

“Anyway. Mina, I’ll see you in the library. And tell your guard she can sit down, please. It makes me nervous when someone stands for that long.”

Mina didn’t turn. But she felt Chaeyoung tense a millimeter behind her.

“It’s her job, Sana.”

“I know. But jobs should include chairs.”

Nayeon sighed. Mina hid something behind her tea cup. Sana chewed with the satisfaction of someone who has completed her morning mission.

Nayeon left first. Sana after, leaving the promise of appearing at the library.

Mina stayed a moment. The cup empty, the echo of the conversation settling.

Not alone. Chaeyoung was still there.

“Your Highness?”

Mina stood. Smoothed her dress with an automatic gesture, hands seeking something to do so they wouldn’t seek Chaeyoung with her gaze.

“The library,” she said. And walked toward the door.

Behind her, half a step to the left, Chaeyoung followed.

---

The library was the only place in the castle where Mina didn’t perform.

It wasn’t a conscious decision. The shoulders dropped. The jaw loosened. The steps became slower, more her own, without the measured cadence she used in the hallways or the perfect posture of the dining hall. In the library, Mina walked like someone who knows exactly where everything is and doesn’t need to impress any of it.

Today was no different. Or shouldn’t have been different.

She went to her table. The extra cushion was still on the chair, the empty cup waiting for her as always, and the mid-morning light entered through the high windows at angles that turned the dust into tiny constellations. All familiar. All in its place.

She pulled three books from her bag. Not the ones she’d brought from her hiding place — those were concealed at the bottom of the temporary wardrobe, between layers of clothing no one would check. These were the cover books: poetry from the classical period, an essay on medicinal botany, a volume of ancient cartography. Innocuous. Decorative. Exactly the type of reading the silent princess would choose.

But between the pages of the cartography were notes. Small, written in the cramped handwriting she used when she didn’t want anyone else to read them. Cross-references from the manuscript notebook with the titles she remembered seeing in the restricted section catalog. Three confirmed matches. Two probable. One that made her pulse quicken each time she reread it: _On the Nature of the Unborn Power_, author unknown, estimated location third floor, north shelf, top row.

_Unborn._ Like something that exists before it takes form. Like something that waits.

Mina opened the cartography book. Placed the notes between the pages of the chapter on southern trade routes, where no one would look for them, and began to read. Or to pretend to read. What she was actually doing was thinking, and what she was thinking about was a different map from the one in front of her: the map of the library’s third floor, the location of the restricted section door, the type of lock, the librarian’s schedule.

She’d been planning it for weeks. Long before the attack. What she hadn’t planned was needing someone else to do it.

Chaeyoung was in her usual position. Against the wall, between two shelves, with a line of sight to the main door, the stairs to the second floor, and the windows. Mina didn’t need to look at her to know it. She knew the position by memory — the exact distance, the angle. But today she allowed herself to look.

Not directly. That would have been too obvious, and Mina was many things but obvious wasn’t one of them. She did it through the reflection in the window. The morning light turned the glass into an imperfect mirror, and if she tilted her head at the right angle over the book, she could see Chaeyoung’s silhouette cut against the shelves in the background.

She was still. Arms at her sides, her right hand resting on the sword pommel with the naturalness of someone for whom the weapon is an extension of the body. The posture was straight but not rigid. Professional. What Jihyo would call standard guard position.

But Mina saw more than that. She saw that the weight was shifted onto the right leg, more than usual. That the left shoulder sat half a centimeter lower than the right — an asymmetry that hadn’t existed last week. That the fingers of the left hand, the hand of the injured arm, opened and closed at regular intervals, as if Chaeyoung were testing her grip without realizing she was doing it.

And she saw something else. The eyes.

Chaeyoung’s eyes made their sweep every thirty seconds — door, stairs, windows — but between sweeps there were moments when they stayed fixed. Not on a surveillance point. On something closer. On Mina’s table. On the book. On the way Mina turned the pages.

It lasted a second. Maybe two. And then they returned to the sweep, as if nothing had happened.

Mina wasn’t sure Chaeyoung knew she was doing it.

She turned her gaze back to the book. The southern trade routes blurred as her mind processed what she’d just observed. _She looks too._ It wasn’t a conclusion; it was a data point. And Mina was careful about the difference between data and conclusions, because hasty conclusions were the most common mistake of impatient academics.

A data point meant nothing on its own. Chaeyoung could be looking at the table because it was the most interesting point in an empty room. She could be looking at the book out of curiosity. She could be looking at the pages because the repetitive movement was the only thing breaking the monotony of hours of silent guard duty.

It could be many things. And Mina was going to treat them as many things until the evidence said otherwise.

The problem was that the evidence was accumulating faster than she could file it.

An hour passed. Maybe more. Time in the library moved by its own rules — thick and slow like honey — and Mina had genuinely lost herself in the notebook’s notes when she heard something that pulled her out of her concentration.

Silence.

Not the library’s normal silence — the active silence that contains the creak of leather, the brush of fabric, the small movements of someone who is awake and alert. This was a different silence. Still. Dense.

Mina lifted her eyes from the book.

Chaeyoung was losing the battle against sleep.

She recognized it immediately — the clenched jaw, the half-closed eyes, the rigidity of someone using every muscle in her body to stay vertical. The shoulder wasn’t helping. The constant pain drained energy that Chaeyoung needed for other things, like for instance not falling asleep standing up in a princess’s library.

Mina watched her for a moment. Saw how her head tilted half a centimeter forward and corrected with a sharp jerk. Saw the slow blink — too slow, the kind of blink that precedes sleep by seconds. Saw the fingers tighten on the sword pommel, seeking the pain of the grip as an anchor.

The first time this happened — the very first day — Mina had let her fight it alone. She’d hidden behind the book and waited for Chaeyoung to find her own balance with the drowsiness, because intervening would have meant acknowledging that she’d been watching her, and that would have crossed a line neither of them was ready to cross.

Today, Mina closed the book.

“Chaeyoung.”

The eyes snapped open. Instant alertness — hand closing on the sword, body tensing into response position. It took half a second to process that there was no threat, that the princess was sitting at her table looking at her, and that what had woken her wasn’t danger but her name.

“Your Highness?”

“Could you help me with something on the second floor?”

Chaeyoung blinked. The transition from nearly-asleep to functional was impressive in its speed. “Of course.”

Mina stood. Took the cartography book — not because she needed it upstairs but because she needed something in her hands. She walked toward the back staircase with Chaeyoung half a step behind, and as they climbed she noticed the change. Chaeyoung’s shoulders relaxed. Her steps grew livelier. Movement woke her the same way stillness put her to sleep. _She needs to move to be at ease,_ Mina thought. _She’s like a war horse locked in a stable that’s too small._

The comparison drew something like a smile that she hid by turning her face toward the shelves.

The second floor was narrower than the first. The shelves rose to the ceiling, creating narrow aisles where two people barely fit side by side. The light was different here — filtered by the distance from the windows, dimmer, with that amber tone sunlight acquires after passing through layers of dust and time.

“I need a book from the top shelf,” Mina said, pointing to a section of ancient history. “Third from the left. The red spine.”

She didn’t need it. She’d read it two years ago and it contained nothing useful to her now. But it was on the highest shelf, and Chaeyoung was the closest person, and giving her a task was better than letting her fight her own eyelids for another three hours.

Chaeyoung looked at the shelf. Assessed the height. And reached with her right arm.

Mina saw her calculate. Saw the exact moment Chaeyoung considered using the left arm — which gave a better angle — and discarded it. The right one reached, but barely. She stretched, fingers brushing the book’s spine, and Mina found herself closer than she’d intended — because she’d moved to point out which one and now they were both in the narrow aisle, between the shelves, at a distance that belonged to whispers and not to instructions.

Chaeyoung pulled the book out. Offered it with her right hand, arm still extended, and for a second they were like that: Chaeyoung holding the book up high, Mina with her hand raised to receive it, and between them a space of thirty centimeters that held dust floating in amber light and something else that Mina was not going to name.

“Thank you,” Mina said. She took the book. Their fingers didn’t brush.

She was aware of the absence of contact with absurd clarity.

“Any others?” Chaeyoung asked. Her voice sounded different up here — closer, contained by the narrowness of the shelves. Normal. Completely normal.

Mina pointed to two more. Chaeyoung retrieved them with the efficiency of someone grateful to have something useful to do. And while she did, Mina allowed herself to observe her up close for the first time without the filter of a window’s reflection.

She was smaller than most knights. She knew that. Sana had said it the first day with the subtlety of a sledgehammer. But height wasn’t what Mina saw when she looked at Chaeyoung up close. What she saw were the hands. Strong, with calluses on the fingers and knuckles that had hit things harder than skin, but surprisingly precise in their movements. The way she took books from the shelf was the same way she gripped her sword: sure, exact, without waste.

And the neck. Where the service shirt collar opened half a centimeter, Mina could see the edge of the bandage descending toward the shoulder. White against tanned skin. The reminder.

_I wish you wouldn’t._

Mina looked away. Focused on the spine of the book in her hands as if it were the most fascinating text on the continent.

“Shall we go down?” she asked. Her voice came out controlled. Perfect. The intact princess.

Chaeyoung nodded. Stepped aside to let her pass first — because the stairway was narrow and protocol said the princess goes ahead — and during the second it took Mina to pass beside her, the space between them shrank to centimeters, and Mina could smell the leather of the belt and something else beneath it, something that wasn’t perfume or soap but simply Chaeyoung — the base scent of someone who rises before dawn and washes with cold water and lives in her own body without pretending it’s something else.

They went down. Mina returned to her table. Chaeyoung returned to her wall.

But the interval between surveillance sweeps shortened. They were no longer thirty seconds. They were twenty. And the moments where Chaeyoung’s eyes lingered on Mina’s table between sweeps grew longer.

Mina didn’t mention it. She opened the ancient history book she didn’t need and pretended to read.

But she was smiling. Just a little. Enough to hurt.

## Transition — Sana's Arrival at the Library → Walk to the Garden

Time passed slowly after that.

Mina fell back into her reading — genuinely this time, the notes between the cartography pages pulling her under with the steady gravity of an unsolved problem. The library did what it always did: closed around her like water, muffling everything outside the radius of the page.

Chaeyoung heard the footsteps before she saw them.

Distant at first. Soft-soled, unhurried, with a rhythm that didn't belong to a guard or a servant. Guards walked with purpose. Servants walked with efficiency. These footsteps walked like they had all the time in the world and fully intended to use it.

Her hand found the sword pommel. Her weight shifted forward, feet ready to close the distance to Mina in two strides if needed. Her eyes fixed on the door.

Mina didn't look up. Her gaze held the page with the concentration of someone who had trained herself to filter out everything that wasn't text — a skill that was admirable in a scholar and deeply inconvenient in someone with enemies.

The heavy wooden door groaned open, its echo rolling through the library like a slow wave.

Chaeyoung was already moving — not toward the door, but half a step closer to Mina, hand firm on the hilt, body angled to shield — when a peach-colored dress appeared in the doorway, revealing, inch by inch, the bright and unmistakable smile of Princess Sana.

Chaeyoung's hand stayed on the sword for one beat longer than it should have.

Sana's eyes swept the room the way they always did — quickly, completely, missing nothing. They found Mina first. Then Chaeyoung. Then Chaeyoung's hand, still resting on the sword with the particular tension of someone who had been ready to draw it.

Sana's smile didn't waver, but something behind it sharpened. Filed. Stored.

Chaeyoung gave a short nod. A greeting contained to a gesture, not wanting to break the library's silence with words it hadn't asked for.

Sana moved through the space with careful steps, though she was humming — softly, a melody that filled the room the way a breeze fills the air, present without insisting. It was impossible to be annoyed by it. It was also impossible to ignore. That was Sana's particular talent: existing at a volume that was always exactly one degree above what the room expected.

"Knight Chaeyoung," she whispered as she passed.

"Your Highness," Chaeyoung whispered back.

Sana approached Mina from behind. She touched her shoulder lightly — a warning, not a surprise. The kind of gesture that said _I'm here_ before saying anything else.

Mina's head lifted from the book. And for one instant — brief, unguarded, visible only to someone standing exactly where Chaeyoung was standing — her eyes went to Chaeyoung's position by the wall before they went to the hand on her shoulder.

"It's me, it's me..." Sana said, bubbly. A small laugh rippled through the room.

"Sana, you came..." Mina turned to her sister, and Chaeyoung couldn't help but notice a slight dip in her voice. Not disappointment, exactly. More like the sound of someone who had been pulled from one frequency and was adjusting to another.

"I did. I hope I wasn't interrupting anything important." Sana glanced between Mina and Chaeyoung. Back and forth. Twice. With the precision of someone measuring a distance she found very interesting.

"Not at all. What is it?"

"Would this bookworm mind a walk in the garden?" Sana said, running her fingers gently through Mina's hair. The gesture was easy, practiced, the kind of thing that only comes from years of sisterhood — hands that know exactly where the other person's hair parts, how much pressure means affection and how much means _I'm worried about you and this is the only way I know how to say it right now._

Mina agreed. She closed the books scattered across the table and stacked them in a neat pile — cartography on the bottom, poetry on top, the hidden notes safely buried between southern trade routes where they would wait for her return.

Shortly after, both princesses were walking through the garden. The same one Mina and Chaeyoung had walked on their first day. But the scene was different now. The sisters walked arm in arm, intertwined with the effortless ease of two people who had been doing this since before they could remember starting. It was the kind of closeness that didn't need to be performed or explained — bodies that had grown up in the same hallways, leaned against the same walls, shared the same silences.

Chaeyoung watched them from behind and thought of Tzuyu.

The image arrived without permission: Tzuyu's hand on her arm in the infirmary, the way she stood beside her during inspections, the particular quality of silence they shared that didn't require filling. The thought carried warmth and an ache she didn't have time for, so she pushed it to the side and focused on the present.

She walked slowly behind both princesses, leaving a wider distance than protocol required. Neither of them asked for the extra space, but Chaeyoung felt it was right. Some conversations needed room to breathe, and whatever Sana had come to the library for, it wasn't cartography.

They walked through the garden for a while before stopping at a marble bench, settling into the warmth of the afternoon sun and the gentle breeze that carried the scent of jasmine and old stone. The peace of the moment was broken by Mina's voice.

## Scene — "The Garden" (Sana & Mina)

_[Picks up from the marble bench in the garden]_

"Everything alright, sister?" Mina asked gently, not looking at her but at the tree above them, its branches swaying gracefully from side to side with the wind.

"I think the one who should be asking that is me." Sana turned slightly, angling her body toward Mina. "Is everything alright, Mina?"

"Is this about what happened?"

The question hung between them. The breeze moved through the garden, carrying the scent of jasmine and something older — stone, earth, the memory of rain.

Sana didn't answer immediately. She pulled a loose thread from her sleeve, wound it around her finger once, twice, and then let it go.

"I wasn't there," she said. Not lightly. Not the way Sana usually said things. "When it happened. I was in my room, asleep, and someone was trying to—" She stopped. Rewound. "I found out the next morning. From Nayeon. Who told me in her _Nayeon_ voice, the one she uses when she's already decided how everyone should feel about something." She paused. "I wanted to come to you immediately. She said you were sleeping. That you needed rest. That everything was under control."

"It was."

"I know. But _under control_ and _fine_ are not the same thing, Mina." Sana looked at her. The playfulness was gone. Underneath was something raw, almost angry, but not at Mina. At the situation. At the distance between knowing your sister was attacked and being allowed to do something about it. "You don't have to be fine. Not with me."

Mina was quiet for a moment. The tree above them cast shifting patterns of light and shadow across the bench, and she watched them move as if they were words in a language she was still learning to read.

"I'm not fine," she said. Quietly. Like testing the weight of honesty. "But I'm not broken either. It happened. It was frightening. And Chaeyoung was there. She handled it."

"Handled it." Sana repeated the words carefully, the way she did when she wanted more without asking for it.

"She took a knife for me, Sana." Mina said it simply. Like a fact. Like the date of a treaty or the name of a river. But her hands, folded in her lap, tightened half a degree. "One of them got through. Past the door, past everything. And Chaeyoung stepped between us and the blade went into her shoulder instead of—" She stopped. Not because the words weren't there, but because the next word was _me_ and saying it out loud meant hearing it, and hearing it meant feeling the full weight of what that exchange had been. "She didn't make a sound."

The last sentence came out differently. Softer. Almost private, as if Mina had said it to herself first and it had slipped past the gate.

Sana was very still.

"Not when it happened," Mina continued, and now she wasn't looking at the tree or the bench or anything in the garden. She was looking at a point somewhere in the middle distance, where the memory lived. "Not after. I didn't even know she'd been hurt until I saw the blood. She just... kept going. As if it didn't matter. As if _she_ didn't matter."

The silence that followed had texture. It was the silence of someone recalibrating — Sana hearing not just the words but the way they were said, the specific quality of attention it took to notice that someone _hadn't_ made a sound in the middle of an attack. You didn't notice silence unless you were listening for that particular person.

Sana filed it. Carefully. Completely.

"That must have been terrifying," she said. And she meant it — but which part she meant, she left open.

"It was." Mina's hands loosened. The composed surface returned, layer by layer, like water settling after a stone. "And now I have a guard who sleeps in the next room and a sister who asks about curtains because she can't ask about the thing she actually wants to ask about."

Sana's mouth opened. Closed. Opened again.

"The curtains _are_ terrible, Mina."

"I know."

"I wasn't _only_ talking about the curtains."

"I know that too."

They looked at each other. And then something cracked — not dramatically, not with tears or trembling voices, but with the quiet shift of two people who had been holding the same weight from opposite sides of a wall and finally found the door.

Sana leaned her head against Mina's shoulder. Mina let her.

"I'm here," Sana said. "For whatever you need. Even if what you need is for me to talk about curtains for the rest of my life."

"That's a significant commitment."

"I'm a significant person."

Mina almost smiled. Almost. The kind of almost that Sana counted as a victory.

They sat like that for a while. The garden didn't demand anything from them. It was one of the few places in the castle that didn't — no protocol, no posture, no performance. Just two sisters on a bench with their arms linked and the afternoon light turning everything soft.

It was Sana who broke the silence, because Sana always broke silences the way she broke everything: with style and questionable timing.

"So." Sana sat up. The shift was instant — grief folded away, something brighter pulled to the surface. Not fake. Just _next_. Sana moved through emotions the way other people moved through rooms: quickly, completely, without looking back at the door. "My birthday."

"Your birthday is in three weeks."

"Two and a half. And I need to start planning because Nayeon is already planning, which means if I don't intervene, I'll end up with a formal banquet for two hundred people where everyone wears grey and discusses grain taxes."

"Nayeon's events are perfectly—"

"Perfectly _adequate_." Sana's eyes were bright now, lit with the particular energy she reserved for schemes, events, and anything that gave her an excuse to make decisions other people would have to live with. "Adequate is Nayeon's love language. I want candles. I want music. I want it small enough that I can actually talk to people instead of waving at them from across a hall the size of a battlefield."

"Have you told her that?"

"I've _suggested_ it. In the way that I suggest things to Nayeon, which involves saying what I want, Nayeon saying she'll think about it, and then me doing it anyway while she pretends not to have noticed." She turned fully toward Mina. "Which is where you come in."

"Me."

"You." Sana took both of Mina's hands in hers, the way she did when she was about to ask for something she'd already decided was happening. "I need you to help me plan it."

"Sana, I don't plan events. I attend them reluctantly and leave early."

"Exactly. You have an outsider's perspective. You'll see things I miss because I'm too excited, and things Nayeon misses because she's too Nayeon." She squeezed Mina's fingers. "I'm serious. I don't want the court planner. I don't want the steward's list of _appropriate entertainment options_. I want my sister to help me make something that actually feels like me."

There was something underneath the request. Mina heard it the way she heard most things Sana said — in the space between the words, in the slight tightening of her grip. _I want something that's mine. In a castle where everything belongs to the crown, I want one night that belongs to me._

"What did you have in mind?" Mina asked.

Sana's face broke into the kind of smile that historically preceded decisions Nayeon would need to be managed about.

"The east terrace. Not the grand hall — the terrace. Open air, under the stars. Lanterns, not chandeliers. Small tables instead of one long one so people can move and actually _choose_ who they sit with. Food that you eat with your hands, not with seven forks arranged by size."

"Nayeon will have opinions about the forks."

"Nayeon can have a fork. One fork. A personal fork. She can bring it from home." Sana waved a hand. "And I want dancing. Real dancing. Not the stiff, step-count-step nonsense they do at court. I want the kind where you actually move."

"You'll scandalize the older nobles."

"Good. They could use the exercise."

Mina considered. "The east terrace gets wind from the valley after sundown. You'd need screens. Or you could use the stone planters to create a natural barrier."

Sana stared at her. "See? _This_ is why I need you. I would have had people shivering in their formal wear and blamed the wine for not being warm enough."

"And the music — if you want less formal dancing, you need musicians who can play by feel. Not the court ensemble."

"You know musicians who play by feel?"

"No. But the kitchen staff do. There's a cellist who plays for the servants' hall on feast nights. I've heard her from the corridor."

"Mina, eavesdropping on servant parties?"

"I was walking to the library. The corridor passes by the servants' hall. It wasn't intentional."

"It was a little intentional."

"It was geographically inevitable."

"I also want you there," Sana added, pointing a finger at Mina's chest. "Present. Not hiding in a corner with a book."

"I don't hide."

"Mina, darling, you once brought an entire volume of botanical illustrations to a state dinner and read it behind the centerpiece."

"The centerpiece was large enough to provide cover. It would have been wasteful not to use it."

Sana laughed. Not the bright, performative laugh she used in public. The real one — short, surprised, the kind that escaped before she could shape it.

Behind them, at her careful distance, Chaeyoung's head turned slightly at the sound.

Sana noticed. Of course she noticed. Sana noticed everything that involved other people's attention, because reading rooms was what she did the way Mina read books — compulsively, thoroughly, and with an eye for subtext.

She didn't say anything. Not yet. She filed it away with the precision of someone who had been watching her little sister for twenty-one years and knew exactly what a new variable looked like.

"You should dance too," Sana continued, as if nothing had happened.

"I don't dance."

"You don't dance _yet_." Sana's eyes drifted — casually, almost lazily, the way a cat's gaze drifts toward a bird it has no intention of chasing right now — toward the figure standing at a respectful distance behind them. Chaeyoung. Still. Watchful. Hand resting on her sword with the ease of long habit.

"Your guard is very attentive," Sana said. Lightly. The opening move.

"That's her job."

"Mmhm." Sana twirled the blade of grass. "She's also very... dedicated. I've noticed she doesn't take breaks."

"She takes breaks."

"When?"

Mina opened her mouth. Closed it. The honest answer was _I don't know because I don't monitor her schedule,_ which was a lie so transparent that even attempting it would be an insult to Sana's intelligence.

"She has a rotation," Mina said instead. "Jihyo manages it."

"Jihyo." Sana repeated the name like she was tasting it. "The captain?"

"Yes."

"I've seen her around. Stern. Very... upright." Sana pulled a blade of grass from beside the bench and twirled it between her fingers. "Does she ever smile, or is that against regulations?"

"I wouldn't know. We don't interact much."

"Hmm." Sana twirled the grass once more, then seemed to lose interest in the topic — or seemed to decide she'd gotten what she needed from it, which with Sana was often the same thing. "And is it also part of her _rotation_ that she looks at you every twenty seconds?"

The number was wrong. It was thirty. Sometimes twenty. Mina knew this because she had counted, which was exactly the kind of information she was not going to share with Sana under any circumstances.

"She's scanning the environment. It's standard guard protocol."

"Is it."

"Yes."

"And the environment happens to include your face."

"Sana."

"I'm just making an observation." The blade of grass spun between her fingers. "The way she stood when I touched your shoulder in the library — did you see that? Her hand went to her sword. For _me_. Her princess's sister. Touching a shoulder."

"She didn't know it was you."

"She saw me walk in."

"Reflexes don't always—"

"Mina." Sana turned to face her fully. The teasing was still there — it lived in the curve of her mouth, the tilt of her head — but beneath it was something sharper. Not accusation. Recognition. "I have spent my entire life watching people look at other people. It's the only skill I have that Nayeon doesn't. I know what polite attention looks like. I know what professional attention looks like." She paused. "That's not what that is."

She let the words settle. Then, quieter:

"You said she didn't make a sound, Mina. That's what stayed with you. Not the knife. Not the blood. The fact that she was _quiet_." Sana's voice was gentle, but her eyes didn't move. "You don't notice someone's silence unless you know what their sound is supposed to be."

The garden was very quiet. The tree swayed. Somewhere, a bird was making a sound that should have been beautiful but was currently extremely inconvenient because it filled the silence that Mina needed in order to construct a response.

"She's good at her job," Mina said. Each word placed like a stone in a path she was building as she walked it.

"She is." Sana agreed. Generously. Like someone handing back a chess piece they didn't need. "She's also the first person you looked at when I sat down next to you in the library. Not me. Not the door. Her."

Mina's jaw tightened a fraction. The barely perceptible tension of someone who has just been handed evidence she was hoping no one had collected.

"I was checking whether she'd identified you as a threat."

Sana stared at her. One second. Two.

Then she burst out laughing. Full, real, the kind that made her shoulders shake and her eyes squeeze shut. The kind that rang through the garden like a bell and made two birds leave a nearby branch in startled formation.

"A _threat_," Sana wheezed. "Me. In my peach dress. Touching your shoulder. A _threat_."

"Mm?"

"Twenty seconds, Mina," she whispered. "I counted too."

Behind her, half a step to the left, Chaeyoung waited.

"Your Highness? Shall we return to the library?"

Mina didn't turn. If she turned, Chaeyoung would see her face, and her face was currently doing something she hadn't authorized.

"Yes," she said. "The library."

And she walked, and Chaeyoung followed, and the distance between them was exactly what it always was — half a step, professional, correct.

But Mina's ears were still pink. And she was almost certain Chaeyoung could see them.

The castle forge was in the basement of the east wing, where the heat wouldn’t bother anyone who mattered and the sound of metal wouldn’t interrupt the nobility’s sleep. Jihyo had always found it an unintentional metaphor: the weapons that protected the kingdom were made beneath it, in a place the kingdom preferred not to look at.

She descended the stairs with the knife wrapped in a cloth. She carried it in her left hand, away from her body, with the caution of someone transporting evidence and not a weapon. The difference was in the intention, not the object.

Two days. She’d used two of the five Nayeon had given her, and what she had so far fit in a single sentence: a poisoned corpse, a dead prisoner, a faceless traitor, and a castle that suddenly felt larger and fuller of corners than Jihyo remembered.

The heat hit her as she crossed the forge door. Dry and dense, the kind of heat that reminds you fire doesn’t need permission to occupy space. The main furnace’s embers burned a deep orange, and the air smelled of coal, hot metal, and the oil Dahyun used to temper blades.

Dahyun was bent over her workbench, examining something with a magnifying glass that looked older than the castle. She wore the soot-stained leather apron, her hair gathered in an improvised bun that defied several laws of physics, and on her face the expression of absolute concentration that only appeared when she was with her metals.

“Captain.” Dahyun looked up at the sound of footsteps. Her eyes lit up with the kind of enthusiasm most people reserve for parties or gifts. “Are you here to visit me or here to ask me something? Because if you’re visiting I get excited, and if you’re asking me something I get more excited.”

“I’m here to ask you something.”

“Excellent!” Dahyun cleared what she’d been examining and wiped the work surface with a rag that only redistributed the soot in a different pattern. “What do you have for me?”

Jihyo placed the cloth on the table and unwrapped it. The knife lay exposed under the furnace light: short blade, double edge, handle wrapped in unmarked black leather. The type of weapon designed not to be remembered.

Dahyun leaned over it without touching it. A magnifying glass appeared in her hand as if by instinct.

“It’s from one of the infiltrators” Jihyo said. “The one that we captured. He was carrying two. We took one when we captured him. The other was in his boot.”

“Generic manufacture,” Dahyun murmured, moving the glass along the blade. “Standard double edge, centered balance… the kind of knife you can buy at any weapons market on the continent. Whoever chose them wanted exactly that.”

“I already know that. What I need is something that doesn’t fit.”

“Patience, Captain. Metal speaks, but it doesn’t shout.” Dahyun turned the knife with a delicate movement, exposing it to the furnace light from different angles. Her eyes narrowed. “The blade is common steel, yes. But the tempering is good. Too good for a generic market knife. Whoever forged this knew what they were doing.”

“Can you identify the smith?”

“By the tempering, no. But…” Dahyun lifted the knife and brought it closer to the furnace, letting the more intense light fall on the base of the blade, where the metal met the handle. “Here. Do you see it?”

Jihyo leaned in. She saw nothing.

“Closer. Where the guard begins.”

Then she saw it. A tiny mark, engraved in the metal at a depth that made it nearly invisible to the naked eye. It wasn’t a decorative symbol or a smith’s signature. It was a number. Three vertical lines and a dot.

“It’s a lot mark,” Dahyun said, and her voice had changed. She was no longer her usual enthusiastic self. She was the Dahyun who studied — the one who knew metals and history and ancient texts, the one who lived beneath the bright surface. “Not a smith’s mark. A commission mark. Someone ordered a specific batch of these knives. The number identifies the lot.”

“Can you trace the manufacturer?”

“By the mark alone, no. But I know this style of marking.” Dahyun set the knife on the table with the care of someone handling a museum piece. “It’s used in military commissions. When an army or garrison orders weapons in bulk, the smith marks each lot for inventory control. It’s an old system, hardly anyone uses it anymore. But there’s a registry.”

“Where?”

“In the armory. If the commission went through any official garrison, there should be a purchase record with the lot number, the smith’s name, and the quantity. If it didn’t go through official channels…” Dahyun let the sentence float. “Then we have something more interesting.”

Jihyo stared at the mark. Three lines and a dot. Tiny, nearly invisible, the kind of detail a professional would leave because the system required it but would expect no one to find on a generic knife wrapped in unmarked black leather.

“If you can cross-reference that mark with the armory records…”

“I can do it today.” Dahyun was already moving toward a shelf packed with books and folders that looked more like a miniature library than part of a forge. “I have the records from the last three years. If the commission was earlier than that, I’d have to go down to the general archive, but to start…”

A crash interrupted the conversation.

Jihyo spun with her hand on her sword. Dahyun jumped enough to bang her knee on the table.

Momo was in the forge doorway. She had dropped her sword on the nearest workbench with the delicacy of someone unloading firewood. The metal hit the surface with a sound that reverberated through the entire chamber.

“Came to sharpen,” Momo announced, apparently oblivious to the two pairs of eyes regarding her with very different intensities.

“YAH!” Dahyun crossed the forge in three strides and lifted Momo’s sword with both hands as if it were an injured baby. She examined the table beneath it with an expression of horror. “This is a precision surface! Do you know how long it took me to level it? Do you have any idea what you do to the calibration when you drop four kilos of steel from that height?”

Momo blinked. “I just put it on the table.”

“You _threw_ it on the table! There’s a difference. One is a civilized gesture and the other is an act of war against my workshop.” Dahyun inspected the surface with her fingers, searching for dents with the devotion of a surgeon. “Always the same with you. Always.”

“It wasn’t that hard,” Momo said, scratching the back of her neck.

“Tell that to my tools. Tell them it wasn’t that hard.”

Jihyo observed the exchange with the patience of someone who had seen this scene before, in minor variations, every time Momo and Dahyun occupied the same space. The dynamic between them was predictable in its chaos: Momo did something without malice that offended Dahyun’s artistic sensibility, Dahyun reacted as if her firstborn had been insulted, Momo didn’t understand the problem, and eventually Dahyun forgave her because she was physically incapable of holding a grudge for more than four minutes.

“Momo,” Jihyo said, and her voice cut through the argument with the efficiency of a well-sharpened knife. Appropriate.

Momo straightened up. Not entirely, because Momo never fully straightened up unless a superior other than Jihyo was present, but with something resembling attention.

“I need you to do something.”

“Kill someone?”

“No.”

“Fight someone?”

“No.”

“Eat something?”

“Momo.”

“Yes, Captain.”

Jihyo measured her words. What she was about to ask required something most people didn’t associate with Momo: sustained attention. But Jihyo knew her people better than her people believed, and she knew that beneath the clumsiness and the food obsession there was something that couldn’t be trained, that came factory-installed. Momo read spaces. Not the way Jihyo did, by analyzing them, but instinctively, like an animal. She knew when something was out of place the same way a dog knows when a storm is coming.

“I’m assigning you additional shifts. Rotations in the zones the infiltrators used to enter: the south wall corridor, the east wing accesses, the passage between the armory and the cells.”

Momo nodded. Didn’t ask why. Didn’t ask how long. She said yes with the same ease with which she accepted a second helping at dinner.

But Jihyo needed more than presence.

“I’m not looking for external threats.” She paused. Let the words land. “I’m looking for internal patterns. Who goes where, when, how often. If someone uses a route that doesn’t correspond to their post or their shift. If someone is where they shouldn’t be.”

Something changed on Momo’s face. Subtle, nearly imperceptible if you didn’t know her. The gaze sharpened. Not by much, just enough to confirm what Jihyo suspected: that Momo understood more than she let on.

“We’re looking for someone on the inside,” Momo said. It wasn’t a question.

“Yes.”

A silence. Dahyun had stopped inspecting her table and was looking at Jihyo with wide eyes. The smith was brilliant, but discretion wasn’t her strong suit, and Jihyo could see the questions piling up behind her expression like water behind a dam.

“Dahyun.”

“Yes, Captain?”

“This doesn’t leave this forge.”

“What thing? I didn’t hear anything. I was focused on my table, which by the way has a new dent thanks to—”

“Dahyun.”

“Understood. Absolute silence. Sealed tomb. Echoless desert.”

Jihyo held her gaze one second longer than necessary. Dahyun swallowed and nodded, this time without embellishment.

“The lot registry,” Jihyo returned to the subject. “Today.”

“Today,” Dahyun confirmed.

Jihyo looked at Momo. “You start tonight. Midnight to six shift. I want you in the south wall corridor first.”

“Understood.” Momo paused. “Can I bring food?”

“You can bring food.”

“Then perfect.”

Jihyo climbed the forge stairs with the same measured step she’d used going down, but something had changed. She didn’t have answers yet. What she had was direction. A lot number, a registry to cross-reference, and a new pair of eyes in the corridors that the traitor believed they controlled.

Three lines and a dot. Someone had made a mistake. And Jihyo was going to find where that mistake ended and where a name began.

---
The royal wing had an interior garden that Jihyo had crossed hundreds of times without looking at it.

It was part of the route between the guard office and Nayeon’s audience chamber: a square courtyard with a covered walkway around it, stone columns, a pair of trees someone insisted on keeping alive despite receiving three hours of sunlight a day, and marble benches that existed primarily for nobles to sit on while waiting for the heir to grant them five minutes.

Jihyo never sat on those benches. Jihyo rarely sat anywhere that wasn’t her desk, and that only because reports couldn’t be written standing up. Sitting in a garden was something people who had time did, and Jihyo didn’t have time. Jihyo had three days, a lot number, and a traitor.

The servant had asked her to wait. Princess Nayeon was finishing a meeting with the council and would see her shortly. _Shortly_ could mean five minutes or thirty, depending on how much the councilors were arguing about taxes or borders or whatever the crisis of the day was that didn’t involve dead infiltrators in cells.

Jihyo stood by a column. Did not sit. Crossed her arms and mentally reviewed what she was going to tell Nayeon: the lot mark, the armory registry, Momo in the corridors. Facts. No speculation. Nayeon hated speculation as much as Jihyo hated wasting time.

“You’re smaller than I imagined.”

The voice came from the left. Jihyo turned her head.

Princess Sana was sitting on one of the marble benches, half hidden by the shadow of a column, legs crossed and a closed fan in her hand that she used to point at Jihyo as if she were a dot on a map.

Jihyo recognized her immediately. It was impossible not to. The second princess of Twiceland was a presence the entire castle knew by name, by anecdote, and by the trail of perfume she left in every room she passed through. Jihyo had seen her at court events, formal banquets, ceremonies. Always at a distance. Always on the other side of the invisible line that separated the crown from the sword.

They had never spoken.

“Your Highness.” Jihyo bowed. Correct, measured, at the exact angle protocol dictated for a captain of the guard before a member of the royal family. “Forgive me, I didn’t see you.”

“Nobody sees me when I sit here. It’s the only corner of the castle where I can be alone without someone coming to ask if I need something.” Sana tilted her head. “Well. Until now.”

“I can wait elsewhere, Your Highness.”

“No, no. Stay.” Sana unfurled the fan with a movement that seemed rehearsed by generations of women who had turned an accessory into a language. “My sister talks about you.”

“Princess Nayeon is generous in her comments.”

“I didn’t say she talks well.” Sana’s smile was something that should come with a warning. “I’m joking. She talks very well. _Captain Jihyo is the most competent officer I’ve had. Captain Jihyo solved such and such in such and such time._ She holds you in high esteem.”

“It’s an honor, Your Highness.”

“Do you always talk like that?”

Jihyo blinked. “Like what?”

“Like you’re reading from a manual.” Sana fanned herself once, slowly. “Everything correct, everything in its place. Not a word too many or too few.”

“It’s the appropriate way to address a member of the royal family, Your Highness.”

“Appropriate. Yes. I suppose it is.” Sana looked at her with an attention that didn’t match the lightness of her tone. There was something behind the eyes, something that measured and cataloged with the same precision Mina used when reading books, but disguised as casual curiosity. “How long have you been captain?”

“Four years, Your Highness.”

“And before?”

“Lieutenant. Six years before that.”

“You started young?”

“At sixteen.”

“And don’t you get tired?”

The question landed in a place Jihyo didn’t expect. It wasn’t a question about her career or her record or her rank. It was a question about _her_. About the person inside the uniform. And it came wrapped in that soft voice Sana used the way others use perfume: naturally and in just enough quantity that you notice it without being able to ignore it.

“Service isn’t measured by exhaustion, Your Highness.”

“That’s a manual answer again.”

“It’s the answer I have.”

A brief silence settled between them. Not uncomfortable — exploratory. The kind of silence that happens when two people are still deciding what shape the conversation is going to take.

Sana's gaze drifted away from Jihyo, past the columns, toward the far side of the courtyard. Her expression shifted — something softer crept in, something almost territorial.

"Your guard," she said, "is standing in front of my roses."

Jihyo turned to look. The guard she'd posted at the garden entrance — standard protocol when a captain waited near the royal wing — stood exactly where he'd been assigned. Straight-backed, still, doing his job with the unremarkable competence of someone who'd been told where to stand and hadn't been told to think about it.

He was also, Jihyo now noticed, planted directly between the courtyard's only substantial patch of sunlight and a climbing rose bush that was doing its best to reach it.

"Is there a problem with him, Your Highness?"

"My roses won't get enough light if your guard stands there all day." Sana announced it with the gravity of a diplomatic incident. Her eyes lifted toward the glass panels in the garden's ceiling, where the thin afternoon sun filtered through in pale, angular beams. For a second — less than a second, the kind of interval that shouldn't register and shouldn't matter — Jihyo noticed the way the light caught the princess's hair. Not the elaborate arrangement of it, not the pins or the style. The color. A warm chestnut that turned almost golden where the sun touched it, glowing against the shadow of the column behind her.

_Focus._

"I'll see what I can do to assist your roses, Your Highness." The answer came automatically. Without much thought, because her thoughts were occupied with the faint flush on the princess's cheeks under the sunlight — the kind of color that could be warmth from the afternoon or could be something else entirely, and Jihyo had no business cataloging the difference.

She pulled her gaze back to center. Roses. Guard. Sunlight. Tactical problem. Solvable.

_Not the other thing. The other thing is not a problem to be solved. The other thing doesn't exist._

Sana looked at her one second more. And then she smiled, not the bright smile of the dining hall, not the mask smile Mina would have recognized as absent. A smaller smile. More real. As if Jihyo had just done something interesting without knowing what.

“I like you, Captain.” Sana closed the fan with a snap. “I don’t usually say that after thirty seconds of conversation, so take it as a compliment.”

Before Jihyo could formulate a response that was simultaneously appropriate, professional, and not completely useless, a servant appeared in the garden archway.

“Captain Jihyo, Princess Nayeon will see you now.”

Jihyo bowed again toward Sana. Correct. Measured.

“It’s been a pleasure, Your Highness.”

“The pleasure is mine.” Sana reopened the fan. “And Captain.”

“Yes?”

“You should sit down sometime. The benches are surprisingly comfortable.”

Jihyo didn’t respond. Not because she didn’t have one, but because the three that occurred to her were all inappropriate for addressing a princess: one was too dry, another too honest, and the third wasn’t an answer at all but rather the involuntary observation that Sana had a beauty mark under her left eye that Jihyo hadn’t registered at any of the formal events where she’d seen her from a distance.

She gave a nod and left.

She walked down the covered corridor toward the audience chamber with her usual firm step. Straight back, firm jaw, her mind already reorganizing toward the report she had to give: lot mark, armory registry, additional shifts. Facts. No speculation.

But in some corner of her head, in a place that didn’t appear in any report or obey any protocol, something had shifted. Barely. Like a chess piece someone nudges half a centimeter without the other player noticing.

_You should sit down sometime._

Nobody told her that. Nobody suggested she rest, that she stop, that she occupy a space without tactical purpose. Jihyo existed in motion, in function, in service. The marble benches of the gardens were for people who had the right to pause.

Jihyo didn’t know if she had that right.

She dismissed it. There was work. There was always work.

But the chess piece didn’t return to its place.

---

The private audience chamber was smaller than the council’s. A table, two chairs, a window overlooking the interior courtyard. No maps on the walls, no banners, none of the scenography of power Nayeon employed when she needed to impress someone. This was the space where Nayeon actually worked, and it showed: there were scrolls piled in one corner of the table, a half-finished cup of tea that had probably been there for hours, and the kind of controlled disorder only produced by someone who has too many things in their head and not enough hours in the day.

Nayeon was standing by the window when Jihyo entered. She didn’t sit. Didn’t invite her to sit. That meant the meeting would be short, and that what Nayeon wanted was facts, not conversation.

“Report,” Nayeon said without turning.

“The infiltrator’s knife has a lot mark at the base of the blade. It’s not generic equipment. It’s a specific commission, military production, with a serial number that can be traced through the armory records.”

Nayeon turned. The light from the window illuminated half her face, leaving the other in shadow, and Jihyo thought, not for the first time, that Nayeon had exactly the right face for ruling: the half the world saw was serene, composed, flawless. The half it didn’t see was the one that made decisions at three in the morning with a glass of wine she never drank.

“How long to cross-reference the registry?”

“Dahyun’s on it. I’ll have results today.”

“What else?”

“I’ve assigned Momo to additional shifts in the access zones the infiltrators used. Not as security reinforcement. As internal observation.”

The silence that followed was brief but dense. Nayeon processed quickly. She always did.

“Do you trust her for that?”

“Momo is one of my best Knight's. She sees things others don’t. She doesn’t think like me or like you. She thinks like someone who has spent her whole life reading spaces by instinct. If someone is where they shouldn’t be, she’ll notice.”

Nayeon looked at her a moment longer. Evaluating. Not the information, Jihyo. Measuring whether the captain before her was taking firm steps or grasping at what she could.

“Two days, Jihyo. You’ve used two of five.”

“Yes, Your Highness.”

“Are you closer?”

Jihyo chose the truth. Not because it was noble, but because lying to Nayeon was a waste of time neither of them could afford.

“I’m closer than I was yesterday. Not close enough.”

Nayeon nodded. Once. The acceptance of someone who understands the difference between progress and results, and who knows that pushing won’t accelerate either one.

“I need something concrete before the fifth day. I want this matter resolved as soon as possible, Captain.”

“You’ll have it.”

It wasn’t a promise. It was something heavier than that. It was Jihyo telling the heir to the kingdom that she was going to find the traitor walking her halls, and that if she didn’t find them in five days, the failure would be hers and no one else’s.

Nayeon dismissed her with a gesture. Jihyo left.

In the hallway, she allowed herself a second. Just one. She closed her eyes. Breathed.

Three days. A lot number. A new pair of eyes in the corridors. And the cold certainty, settled in her stomach like a stone, that the name existed. That it was there, in some registry, in some pattern, in some detail she hadn’t yet seen.

She just had to find it before the traitor found a way to disappear.

---

Night arrived with the punctuality of inevitable things.

Mina didn’t go to the terrace. Not because she didn’t want to, the terrace was still the only place without a ceiling, the only space where the sky was accessible and the castle stopped feeling like what it was. But going to the terrace meant crossing half the castle with two additional guards Nayeon had assigned to the night shift, and Mina didn’t have the energy to be escorted like a fragile package through corridors where every shadow was now potentially hostile.

So she stayed.

The temporary room in the north wing was exactly what Sana had said: functional. The curtains were thick and of an indefinite color, the type of fabric that exists to serve a purpose and not to be looked at. The bed was firmer than hers, the sheets rougher, and the space more cramped. There was no carved desk where she usually wrote, no shelf with her favorite books, no false panel behind which her secrets lived.

But she had the door.

The interior door leading to the antechamber. And on the other side of that door, the sounds Mina had learned to read in two nights the way one learns a new language: through immersion, without a dictionary, letting context provide the meaning.

Boots on stone. The belt being set down on something solid, probably the floor beside the bed. The hiss of the shoulder when Chaeyoung removed her service shirt, brief, contained, swallowed before it could become anything more audible. Water. The liquid sound of someone washing their face in a jug. And then the creak of the bed accepting a weight it wasn’t designed to sustain for more than a military campaign.

Mina was sitting on the bed with the three books open around her.

_Principles of Elemental Energy_ to her left, open to the chapter on involuntary manifestations. _Theory of Flow and Form_ to her right, in the section on the relationship between emotion and power. And the manuscript notebook in her lap, with that cramped, urgent handwriting that seemed written by someone who feared forgetting what they knew.

She’d spent the last hour cross-referencing information. The notebook mentioned five texts from the restricted section by name. Three were theoretical treatises Mina could look up in the general catalog to confirm their location. One was a compendium of documented cases that the author described as _the only empirical evidence I have found outside the royal archives._ And the fifth was the one that quickened her pulse: _On the Nature of the Unborn Power._

The notebook’s author mentioned it with a reverence used for no other text. _If an explanation exists for what I feel in my hands, it is in those pages. I have attempted access three times. The third-floor lock resists everything I have tried. I need another way._

Mina ran her fingers over the ink. _What I feel in my hands._ She lifted her gaze from the pages and looked at her palms.

There it was. The warmth. Faint, like an ember buried in ash, so subtle it could be imagination if it weren’t for the fact that she’d been feeling it for months. It came and went without apparent pattern, with no connection to ambient temperature or physical exertion. Sometimes it appeared when she was reading. Sometimes when she was thinking. Sometimes, and this was what unsettled her most, when she was still in the darkness and her mind stopped guarding the doors she kept closed during the day.

Like now.

She closed her hands. The warmth didn’t disappear, but it contained itself, like water in a bowl.

_It’s not imagination. It was never imagination._

But books didn’t lie. And the three she had open on the bed said the same thing in different words: what she felt was real, it had a name, and the name was _power._

The cups that moved. The pages that turned on their own. The breeze in closed rooms. All the things she’d dismissed as coincidences, accidents, tricks of perception. All the justifications she’d built with the same urgency with which she’d built this morning’s explanation of _it’s gratitude, anyone would._

Mina was good at lying. Especially to herself.

The problem was that all three books also said the same thing about limits: _without guidance, without structure, without understanding of the fundamental mechanisms, raw power is indistinguishable from danger._ She needed more. She needed the restricted section texts. She needed, specifically, _On the Nature of the Unborn Power,_ written by someone who had apparently felt the same thing she did and had found answers.

And for that she needed Chaeyoung.

The thought stopped her. Not the thought itself, which was logical and practical, but the way it arrived. Not as a cold conclusion but as something warmer, more complex. _She needed Chaeyoung_ wasn’t just _she needed someone who knew how to pick locks and knew the patrol routes._ It was _she needed the specific person on the other side of that door._ And the difference mattered more than Mina was willing to examine tonight.

She closed the books. Stacked them in the correct order and stored them at the bottom of the wardrobe, between the layers of clothing, in the darkness where no one would look for the secrets of a princess who was supposed to only read poetry.

She blew out every candle but one. The room shrank to a circle of yellow light around the bed and shadows in every other direction.

On the other side of the door, the silence had changed. Chaeyoung’s breathing had grown slower, deeper. Asleep. Or nearly asleep, on that edge where consciousness lets go but reflexes stay lit, ready to wake at the slightest sound that doesn’t belong to the nighttime vocabulary her body had memorized.

Mina stayed listening. Not on purpose. It was simply what happened when the world went quiet enough: Chaeyoung’s sounds filled the space the silence left, like water finding its level.

_The first sign is that the silences change shape._

She didn’t remember where she’d read it. In some text about bonds between people, perhaps. Or in a poem, those poems she’d devoured as a teenager with the avidity of someone studying maps of a place she’d never visit. The phrase had seemed pretty then. Abstract. The kind of thing poets say because it sounds nice regardless of whether it’s true.

Now it didn’t seem abstract. It seemed exact.

The silences between her and Chaeyoung had changed shape. The silence of the first day was empty: two strangers occupying the same space without knowing how to fill it. Last week’s silence was comfortable: routine creating a language of presences that didn’t need words. Tonight’s silence was something else. Dense. Aware. The silence of two people who know the other is there and that this knowing has a weight it didn’t have before.

Mina looked at the door. The closed door, without a latch, with the strip of darkness underneath indicating that Chaeyoung had blown out her candle.

_Tomorrow,_ she thought. _Tomorrow I ask her._

Not the favor. Not just the favor. What she was going to ask Chaeyoung tomorrow was more than access to a restricted section of the library. It was trust. It was telling Chaeyoung: _I have secrets, and I want you to be part of them._ It was opening a door that had no latch but that Mina had kept closed by her own choice.

It was terrifying.

It was necessary.

She blew out the last candle. Darkness covered her completely, and in the darkness the sounds sharpened: her own breathing, the pulse in her ears, and on the other side of the wall — fifteen steps away — the breathing of someone who slept with her sword within arm’s reach and who tomorrow, without knowing it, was going to become her accomplice.

Mina closed her eyes.

She didn’t sleep for a long while.

The problem with observation missions, Momo thought as she bit into an apple she’d procured from somewhere she preferred not to specify, was that no one warned you how boring they were.

She’d been in the corridors for six hours. Six hours of walking the same routes, seeing the same walls, passing the same guards who looked at her with the polite confusion of people who didn’t understand what Knight Momo was doing patrolling zones that weren’t hers. She’d told three of them she was covering an extra shift. She’d told two she was lost. Both had believed her, which said something about Momo’s reputation in matters of navigation.

But she was observing.

Jihyo had said _internal patterns. Who goes where, when, how often._ And Momo, who didn’t think in words but in shapes and rhythms, had converted the instruction into something simpler: _find what doesn’t fit._

The castle corridors had their own pulse. Momo had felt it since her first year as a knight, without being able to explain it, without trying to explain it, simply knowing that spaces had a rhythm and that when something broke it, it was like an off-key note in a melody you’d been hearing your entire life.

The south wall corridor had a predictable rhythm. Guards every forty-five minutes. Servants at dawn and dusk carrying firewood to the east wing fireplaces. A gray cat that appeared around midnight and vanished before dawn. Momo had named the cat. His name was General, because he walked as if the entire castle were his.

The passage between the armory and the cells was more irregular. More traffic during the day, almost none at night. The smiths came and went on schedules that depended on commissions. The cell guards rotated every six hours. The shift coordinator passed once in the morning and once in the afternoon.

All of that was normal. All of it fit.

What didn’t fit was Sir Callen.

Momo hadn’t been looking for him specifically. She didn’t have names in her head, didn’t have suspects, didn’t have the mental list someone like Jihyo would have built with data and deductions. What she had was a feeling. The same feeling that told her when an opponent was going to attack before they moved their arm, the same one that told her when food was done without needing to taste it. A feeling of _this isn’t where it should be._

Sir Callen was a knight from the west wing. His post was at the castle’s main gate, daytime rotation, six days out of seven. He had no reason to be in the east wing corridor. And yet Momo had seen him pass three times in two days.

The first time she thought nothing of it. People move through the castle. They have reasons. It wasn’t her problem.

The second time she registered it was the same man. Blond hair, square jaw, the build of someone who eats well and trains just enough. He walked with a purpose that didn’t correspond to a casual stroll but without the urgency of someone on an official mission. An intermediate pace. The pace of someone who doesn’t want to attract attention.

The third time, Momo stopped eating.

She went to the forge.

Dahyun was surrounded by folders. The workbench, the same table Momo had violated the day before with her sword, was covered with armory records: yellowed pages, inventories with columns of numbers, reception stamps with dates going back years. Dahyun had ink on her fingers, soot on her forehead, and the expression of someone hunting something with the determination of a bloodhound.

“Hey,” Momo said from the doorway.

Dahyun raised a finger without looking at her. “One moment. I’m onto something.”

Momo waited. Looked around the forge. Spotted an apple on a shelf near the furnace. Took it. Bit into it. The apple was warm from the fire’s proximity. It was oddly pleasant.

“That apple was my lunch,” Dahyun said without looking up.

“Want me to give it back?”

“You already took a bite.”

“I can give you the half I didn’t bite.”

“Momo.”

“Yes?”

Dahyun finally looked up. The irritation lasted exactly as long as Momo expected: two seconds. After which it was replaced by the affectionate resignation of someone who has accepted that certain forces of nature cannot be fought.

“What do you need?”

“I have a question.” Momo took another bite of the apple. “Is it normal for Sir Callen to pass through the east wing corridor three times in two days if his post is in the west wing?”

Dahyun went still. The hand holding the registry stopped mid-page-turn.

“Callen?”

“The blond one. Big. Was there for the basilisk thing.” Momo paused, searching for more descriptors. “I don’t like him.”

“Why don’t you like him?”

Momo considered the question with the seriousness of someone being asked something fundamental.

“Because when he saw the basilisk he ran away and left Chaeyoung to climb on it alone.”

The memory was clear. Momo hadn’t been on the basilisk mission, but she’d heard the story. Everyone had. And the detail that Sir Callen — who was bigger and more experienced than Chaeyoung — had fallen back while she jumped onto the creature’s back was the kind of thing Momo didn’t forget. Not out of spite. Momo didn’t operate on spite. What operated was something more basic: an instinctive classification of people into two categories. Those who stay and those who run. Chaeyoung stayed. Callen ran. That was all Momo needed to know.

“Three times in two days,” Dahyun repeated. Her voice had changed. The usual enthusiastic tone had been replaced by something sharper, more focused. The Dahyun who studied. “What times?”

“The first was in the morning, after the guard change. The second in the afternoon, near dinnertime. The third today at dawn.”

“Always the same corridor?”

“The one connecting the armory to the guard access.”

Dahyun looked at the records on her table. Then looked at Momo. And Momo saw the exact moment something connected behind the smith’s eyes, two pieces that until now had been on separate tables clicking together at once.

“Tell that to Jihyo,” Dahyun said. “Now.”

“Why?”

“Because I just found the lot registry.” Dahyun lifted one of the yellowed pages. Her hand trembled slightly, not from fear but from the excitement of someone who has been searching for something for hours and finally holds it in her fingers. “The knives were a commission from eight months ago. Twenty units. Paid in cash, no buyer name. But the smith who made them doesn’t work with just anyone. He has a select clientele, only castle officers with the rank of knight or above.”

The silence that followed was the kind Momo recognized. The silence before a fight. The moment when the air compresses and everything becomes clearer.

“Callen is a knight,” Momo said.

“Callen is a knight,” Dahyun confirmed.

Momo tossed the apple core into a bucket by the furnace. Wiped her hands on her trousers.

"I'm going to find Jihyo."

"Momo."

She stopped at the door. One hand already on the frame, body angled toward the corridor, mind already halfway to Jihyo's office. But Dahyun's voice had something in it that made her turn.

Dahyun was looking at her with an expression Momo hadn't seen before. It wasn't the outrage over the calibration table or the resigned despair over the apple. It was quieter than both. Heavier. The expression of someone who has just handed you a thread and understood, before you did, what it might be connected to.

"Be careful."

Two words. Dahyun, who could talk for forty minutes about the carbon content of a single blade, who narrated her own forging process to no one in particular, who had once given a twenty-minute eulogy for a cracked anvil, said two words and stopped.

Momo nodded. She understood what lived inside the silence that followed.

She turned to leave. Then paused.

Her hand went to the pocket of her trousers, deep, the one she used for things she didn't want to lose, and fished around for a moment. She pulled out an apple. Noticeably fresher than the one Dahyun had been calling lunch. Rounder, greener, without the bruise or the expression of defeat. She'd been carrying it since breakfast, because Momo always carried food, the way other people carried handkerchiefs or coins, and because at some point during the morning; she couldn't remember exactly when; she'd thought _Dahyun forgets to eat when she's working._

She set it on the workbench. Same spot where the old one had been. Said nothing about it.

Dahyun looked at the apple. Looked at Momo. Opened her mouth.

Momo was already gone.

She didn't get lost once on the way to Jihyo's office.

---

The south garden was, according to Sana, the best corner of the castle for thinking. It had roses that bloomed even in autumn, a fountain calibrated to the exact frequency between relaxing and hypnotic, and the tactical advantage of being far enough from the council chamber that no bored councilor would find her by accident.

Jihyo was crossing it for the second time in two days.

She had reasons. Momo had just given her the information about Callen. Dahyun had found the lot registry. The threads were touching but not yet tied, and thinking required a place where no one would interrupt her with reports or questions or problems that weren't the problem she had in hand.

The south garden was on the route. That was all.

_That was all._

She wasn't expecting to find her there.

Sana was by the roses. Not seated this time but standing, with pruning shears in one hand and a linen apron over her dress that made her look, improbably, like a gardener who happened to be royal rather than the other way around. She was cutting roses with the focus of someone who took the activity seriously — examining each stem before cutting, placing the flowers in a basket with an order that followed some logic Jihyo couldn't decipher.

Jihyo stopped. The north corridor would take her to the same destination with only three minutes' difference, and three minutes was a reasonable price for avoiding a conversation for which she had no protocol.

"Captain."

Too late.

Sana had seen her. Of course she had. The woman had the ability to detect presences as if the air itself sent her advance notice.

"Your Highness." The standard nod. "I didn't mean to interrupt."

"You're not interrupting. The roses and I were having a creative disagreement, and I could use a mediator." Sana held up a peach-colored rose to the sunlight. "This one or this one?" She held up another — nearly identical, a shade darker.

"I have no opinion on roses, Your Highness."

"Everyone has an opinion on roses. People who say they don't simply haven't stopped to think about it." She held them closer to Jihyo, as if distance were the problem. "The light one is more elegant. The dark one has more personality. Which do you prefer — elegance or personality?"

Jihyo looked at the roses. Then at Sana. Sana was looking at her with that smile that was simultaneously an invitation and a challenge — the smile of someone who knows she's asking about more than flowers and wants to see if the other person notices.

"Whichever lasts longer in the water," Jihyo said.

Sana laughed. Not the rehearsed laugh of the dining hall, not the bright one she used to illuminate rooms. A short, genuine thing, pulled out by surprise — the kind that escapes before you can decide whether you want to let it.

"Functional even with flowers. You're consistent, Captain. I'll give you that."

"I do what I can, Your Highness."

Sana cut the dark rose. Placed it in the basket with the others and pulled off a glove to examine her hand — a thorn had caught the pad of her thumb, leaving a thin line of red so small it barely qualified as a wound. She pressed her finger against it absently, the way someone touches a thought they're not ready to let go of.

Jihyo watched the motion. The hands were stained — not dirty, but _marked_. Petal residue along the fingers, a green smudge at the base of the palm from the stems, the faint impression of the shears' grip across the inside of her knuckles. These were not the hands of a princess who arranged flowers. They were the hands of someone who _worked_ with them, who knelt in dirt and pulled roots and understood the difference between cutting and pruning.

_Focus._

"I was hoping I'd see you, actually," Sana said, still examining the thorn mark.

The sentence landed in a place it shouldn't have. _Hoping_ was a word that implied waiting. Anticipation. The image of Sana cutting roses in a garden she didn't need to be in, glancing toward the corridor every few minutes, arranging a casual encounter that was anything but.

"How can I be of service, Your Highness?"

"The garden guard." Sana gestured toward the south entrance, where a new soldier stood at a position that left the rose bushes entirely unobstructed. "You moved him."

"You mentioned he was blocking the sunlight."

"I mentioned it once. In passing. During a conversation you didn't start and didn't seem to enjoy." Sana put the glove back on, slowly, finger by finger. "And the next morning, he was four meters to the left. No announcement. No explanation. No servant coming to tell me _the captain has accommodated your request._" She looked at Jihyo. "You just did it."

"It was a reasonable request, Your Highness."

"Do you know how rare that is? Someone hearing what I say and treating it as reasonable?" Sana's voice was light, but her eyes weren't. "Most people in this castle love me, Captain. But they love me the way you love weather — they enjoy it when it's pleasant and wait for it to pass when it's not. No one thinks the weather has a point."

The words sat in the air between them. Jihyo recognized what was underneath: not a complaint, not self-pity. A fact. Delivered with the same clarity Sana had used to say _I like you_ the day before. Here is what my life looks like. I'm not asking you to fix it. I'm telling you because you're the first person in a long time who made me think someone might actually be listening.

"And me?"

The question came out before Jihyo authorized it. Two words that weren't in any report and weren't governed by any protocol. Two words that came from the same place where the chess piece had shifted yesterday and still hadn't returned to its square.

Sana smiled. The small one. The real one.

“You moved my guard because it made sense to move him. Without condescension, without ceremony. As if my request were as valid as any other.” She paused. “That’s rare, Captain. And I pay attention to rare things. ”

_For now._ The words had a door in them. Jihyo could hear it — the hinge, the space on the other side, the implicit invitation to walk through it at some unspecified future point. _For now_ meant _there will be a later._ And Sana said it without pressure, without expectation, the way someone leaves a light on in a window without knowing if anyone is walking toward it.

The sun fell over the garden with the mid-afternoon generosity that makes everything look warmer than it is. The roses smelled. The fountain sounded. And Sana was standing among her flowers with petal-stained fingers and a thorn mark on her thumb and her eyes on Jihyo with an attention that was neither casual nor playful nor superficial.

It was honest. That was the problem. If it had been flirtation, Jihyo would have known what to do. She had defenses for flirtation — the same walls she used for nobles who confused respect with interest or officers who tried flattery as a shortcut. Flirtation was a game with rules, and Jihyo knew every rule.

This wasn't a game. Sana was telling her, simply and without hiding, that the way Jihyo treated her meant something. No mask. No layers. Just a woman in an apron with dirt on her hands and the particular courage of someone who has decided that honesty is less exhausting than performance.

Jihyo didn't know what to do with that. She had protocols for threats, procedures for crises, contingency plans for every scenario the castle could produce. She had nothing for a princess who cut roses and said things no one said to her.

"I should go, Your Highness. I have matters to attend to."

"Of course. The captain always has matters." Sana turned back to the roses, shears already finding the next stem. "Captain."

"Yes?"

"The rose that lasts longest in water is the dark one. Thicker stems." She cut it with a clean movement. "In case you ever need to know."

Jihyo left. Took the north corridor. The extra three minutes didn't matter.

In her head, the information about Callen and the knife lots competed for space with something that had no lot number and no armory registry and no place in any report she would ever write.

_I pay attention to rare things._

Jihyo was many things. Rare wasn't one of them. She was disciplined, efficient, the most competent officer Nayeon had ever had. Predictable in her excellence. Consistent in her rigidity. Absolutely, completely, functionally unremarkable in everything that wasn't her work.

_The rose that lasts longest is the dark one._

She shook her head. There was a traitor in the castle. A name at the end of a registry. Three days and a promise owed to Nayeon.

_There is no time for roses._

---

The afternoon had turned to night when Mina made the decision to stop deciding and act.

They’d dined in the private dining room — she and Nayeon and Sana — another dinner of safe topics and careful silences, and then they’d returned to the north wing with the escort of two guards and Chaeyoung half a step behind. The return routine already had its own choreography: the guards posted at the exterior door, Chaeyoung entered first to inspect the room, and Mina waited in the hallway for the forty-five seconds it took her guard to verify windows, curtains, and any corner where a threat might hide.

Forty-five seconds that had once seemed excessive and now seemed reasonable. Another thing the attack had changed.

“Clear, Your Highness.”

Mina entered. Chaeyoung moved toward the antechamber, as she did every night.

“Do you need anything else before I retire?”

It was the usual question. The same phrase, the same tone, the same professional offer Chaeyoung made every night before crossing the interior door and disappearing toward her too-short bed and her windowless darkness.

“No. Thank you, Chaeyoung.”

It was the usual answer.

Chaeyoung nodded. Turned toward the door.

“Wait.”

The word came before the usual answer. Before Mina could wrap it in courtesy or soften it with a _sorry_ or turn it into something less urgent than it sounded. A single word, bare, that stopped Chaeyoung mid-step.

She turned. Looked at Mina with those eyes that ran their automatic sweep, face, hands, posture, searching for the threat, because for Chaeyoung any break in pattern was potentially a threat until proven otherwise.

“Your Highness?”

Mina breathed. Once. It wasn’t enough, but it was what she had.

“I need to ask you something. And I need you to hear me out before you answer.”

Chaeyoung looked at her a second longer. Something changed in her posture, an imperceptible adjustment that meant she’d stopped searching for external threats and was paying real attention. The Chaeyoung who listened was different from the Chaeyoung who watched. Stiller. More present.

“I’m listening.”

Mina walked to the wardrobe. Opened the doors. Pulled the three books from between the layers of clothing where she’d hidden them and placed them on the bed, side by side, covers facing up.

Chaeyoung looked at them. Mina saw the moment she registered that these weren’t the library books, not the poetry and cartography and botany she carried under her arm during the day. These were different. Older, more worn, with the look of things that have been read in secret by candlelight that burns out quickly.

“I took them from a hiding place in my chambers before they sealed them,” Mina said. The truth, bare, without the wrapping of ambiguity she’d planned to use. She’d spent the previous night rehearsing elegant ways to say it, indirect ways, ways that maintained some distance between the princess and the secret. But now, with Chaeyoung looking at her with that attention that weighed more than any silence, elegance felt like cowardice. “They’re texts on ancient energies. On power. On things a princess is not supposed to be studying.”

Chaeyoung didn’t react. Not with surprise, not with judgment, not with the expression of _Your Highness, this is inappropriate_ that any other guard would have worn. She stayed still. Listening. As she’d been asked.

“I’ve been reading them for months,” Mina continued. “In secret. At night. They’re limited, fragmentary, and I need more. There are texts in the library’s restricted section that complete what these begin. I know which ones. I know where they are.” She paused. “I can’t reach them alone.”

The silence that followed wasn’t long, but it was dense. Chaeyoung looked at the books. Then at Mina. And Mina could see the process behind the eyes: the evaluation, the calculation, the layers of concern stacking one atop another.

“The restricted section is locked,” Chaeyoung said. Not as an objection, as a data point.

“I know.”

“If they catch you there…”

“They won’t catch me.” Mina held her gaze. “Because you know the patrol routes…”

Something crossed Chaeyoung’s face. Not a smile, but the recognition of the irony: the same routes the infiltrators had exploited, the same vulnerabilities Jihyo was trying to close, now being used by a princess to steal books. There was something almost absurd about it. Something that, in other circumstances, might have been funny.

“Your Highness, what you’re asking me…”

“I know. I know what I’m asking you.” Mina didn’t back down. Didn’t soften. Didn’t apologize or ask permission or give Chaeyoung the easy out of _don’t worry, it was a silly idea, let’s forget it._ “I’m asking you to break rules with me. To trust that I have a reason. And for that reason to be enough even though I can’t fully explain it to you tonight.”

Chaeyoung looked at her. And Mina saw something she didn’t expect: not the struggle of duty against request, not the conflict of the professional against the irregular order. She saw curiosity. Chaeyoung was looking at the books, looking at Mina, and she _wanted to know._ Not what was in the pages. What was in Mina that drove her to hide books and plan midnight heists and stand before her guard with a determination that didn’t belong to the princess who read poetry and drank tea.

“You did what you believed was right that night,” Mina said. Soft, but without yielding. “Without asking permission. Without consulting anyone. Because you knew it was necessary, and that waiting would have cost more than acting. This is the same thing.” She paused. “Only my weapons are different.”

The silence lasted three heartbeats. Mina counted them.

“When?” Chaeyoung asked.

Mina felt something loosen inside her. Not relief but something more complex. The feeling of having crossed a threshold and discovering that the other side wasn’t an abyss but solid ground.

“Tonight.”

Chaeyoung nodded. Once. And in that nod, Mina read everything she needed to read: the acceptance, the trust, the decision to cross a line that couldn’t be uncrossed. Not because Chaeyoung didn’t see the risk. Because she saw something more.

“Give me an hour,” Chaeyoung said. “I need to verify tonight’s shifts. The third-floor guard change is at one, there’s a twenty-minute window before the next sentry completes his first round.”

Mina blinked. “How do you know that?”

“It’s my job to know where every guard in the castle is at every hour of the day, Your Highness.” And there it was: not the almost-smile, but something closer. The shadow of something that on another face would have been amusement. “Turns out that’s useful for more things than security.”

Mina felt the smile before she could control it. Small, involuntary, born of relief and something else she wasn’t going to name tonight.

“One hour,” she said.

Chaeyoung nodded and crossed through the antechamber door.

Mina was left alone with the three books on the bed and the echo of a decision that had just changed something between them. She didn’t know exactly what. But she knew that the door she’d opened tonight wasn’t the one to the restricted section.

It was the other one. The one without a latch that she’d kept closed by her own choice.

That door was open now. And Mina didn’t want to close it.

---

At a quarter past one in the morning, Chaeyoung knocked on the interior door twice. Softly. A code they’d agreed on fifteen minutes earlier, when Chaeyoung returned from verifying the shifts with fresh information and the certainty that the window was real.

Mina opened. She’d changed out of the dress into something darker, more practical: a gray wool skirt and a night-colored long-sleeved blouse, her hair gathered in a tight braid that cleared her face. No jewelry, no ornaments. The princess had disappeared. What remained was the woman who was going to steal books.

Chaeyoung looked at her for a second. Registered the change, filed it, and said nothing. She simply gestured with her head toward the hallway.

They exited through the outer door of the chambers. The night-shift guards were at the far end of the corridor, twenty meters to the north, where the line of sight was broken by a curve in the wall. Chaeyoung had timed their rounds during the last hour: forty-five seconds out, thirty back, a one-minute pause at the turning point. They had ninety seconds to disappear.

It was enough.

They moved in silence. Chaeyoung ahead, Mina behind, pressed to the wall where the shadows were densest. The north wing corridors at night were a different animal than by day: the torches were extinguished to save oil, and the only light came from the moon entering through windows at irregular intervals, creating silver pools on the stone that had to be crossed or circumvented.

Chaeyoung circumvented them. Mina followed.

They didn’t speak. They didn’t need to. Chaeyoung raised a hand and Mina stopped. Chaeyoung lowered the hand and Mina advanced. A language of gestures they hadn’t rehearsed but that worked with the naturalness of something that had always existed, waiting to be used.

At the intersection of the north wing and the central wing, Chaeyoung halted. Raised her hand. Mina froze.

Footsteps. Distant, approaching, with the unmistakable rhythm of a patrol. Chaeyoung counted mentally — three, four, five — the steps nearing the intersection. The corridor they were in had no alcoves, no doors, nothing except stone and shadow and a high window that let in a rectangle of moonlight.

Chaeyoung retreated. Her hand found Mina’s arm in the darkness, fingers closing on the blouse fabric, guiding her backward. The wall received them. The space between them shrank to centimeters as Chaeyoung positioned her in the deepest shadow, between two decorative columns that formed a recess just wide enough for two people.

Mina felt the cold stone against her back and Chaeyoung’s warmth against her front. They weren’t pressed together, not quite, but the distance was that of a whisper. She could feel Chaeyoung’s breath against her forehead, controlled, measured, the breathing of someone trained to make silence of her own body.

The footsteps passed. A shadow crossed the pool of moonlight on the floor. It moved away.

Neither of them moved.

One second. Two. Long enough for Mina to register things she shouldn’t be registering: the heat radiating from Chaeyoung’s body despite the hallway’s cold, the smell of leather and cold water and something that was simply _her,_ the base scent of someone who rises before dawn and washes with cold water and lives in her own body without pretending it’s something else, the way Chaeyoung’s eyes gleamed in the darkness as they scanned the corridor above Mina’s head, attentive, professional, looking at everything except the person centimeters away.

And then Chaeyoung looked down.

It was an instant. Less than an instant. A fraction of a second where the eyes scanning the corridor dropped and found Mina’s, there, in the darkness, at a distance that belonged to secrets and not to guards.

Chaeyoung pulled away.

“Clear,” she murmured. Her voice sounded exactly as always. Firm, professional, without cracks. As if nothing had happened.

Mina peeled her back from the wall. Her hands were trembling, a tiny tremor she hid by clenching her fists against her thighs.

_It’s the adrenaline,_ she told herself. _It’s the risk of being discovered. It’s perfectly explainable._

And the explanation, like all the ones she’d built in the last two days, arrived too quickly.

They kept moving. The central wing gave way to the library corridor, wider, with wooden floors that creaked if you didn’t know where to step. Chaeyoung knew. She placed her steps along the edges, where the wood was firmer, and Mina imitated her, stepping exactly where she stepped, her feet finding the same safe boards with a confidence that came not from experience but from something simpler: she trusted Chaeyoung. In the darkness, in the silence, between the shadows of a castle that could discover them at any moment, she trusted her without reservation.

The library door was closed. Not locked, the librarian didn’t lock the main door, only the special sections. Chaeyoung opened it carefully, centimeter by centimeter, containing the creak of the hinges with her palm against the wood.

They entered.

---

The library at night looked like nothing Mina had ever known.

By day it was her refuge. Her territory. The place where the world shrank to pages and silence and the certainty that everything had its place. At night it was something else. Moonlight entered through the high windows and turned the shelves into geometries of silver and shadow, paper cathedrals where the titles on the spines glowed like ghost script. The smell was the same, old paper, leather, ink, but amplified by the darkness, denser, more intimate, as if the books breathed deeper when no one was watching.

The three floors rose above them like an inverted well. The dark wooden stairs spiraled upward, and from below Mina could see the second- and third-floor balconies silhouetted against the higher windows, where the moon was more direct and the light more blue.

Chaeyoung moved differently here. In the corridors she was tension and control. In the library she was something more fluid, even quieter than before, as if the space demanded a respect she delivered without thinking. Her boots made no sound on the wood. Her hands didn’t brush the shelves. She crossed the darkness as if she’d been born in it.

They climbed to the second floor. Then the third.

The restricted section was at the back of the third floor, behind an oak door reinforced with iron. The lock was old but solid, the kind of mechanism a competent smith would have installed thinking it should last generations, not that someone would try to force it at one-thirty in the morning.

Chaeyoung pulled something from her pocket. Mina needed a second to recognize it: a lockpick. Thin, dark metal, with a leather-wrapped handle that suggested frequent use.

“Where did you get that?”

“Dahyun gave it to me.” Chaeyoung knelt before the lock, inserting the pick with the delicacy of a surgeon. “Said it was a joke. I sharpened it.”

“You know how to pick locks?”

“It’s a long story, Your Highness.”

“We have twenty minutes.”

“Then it’s a story for another night.”

The mechanism yielded with a soft click. Chaeyoung pushed the door, which opened with a deep groan of hinges that hadn’t been oiled in years.

The restricted section was smaller than Mina had imagined. A rectangular space, perhaps four meters by six, with wall-to-wall shelves and a ceiling so low Chaeyoung could have touched the beams on tiptoe. The books were different from the rest of the library: older, some with chains on their spines tethering them to the shelves, others wrapped in cloth or leather with bronze clasps. The dust was thicker here. The air stiller. As if time moved more slowly behind that door.

Mina felt the transformation in her own body. Shoulders dropped. Breathing deepened. Hands stopped trembling and began to move with purpose, running along the spines, reading titles in the gloom with the fluency of someone who had memorized a catalog.

She wasn’t the measured princess of breakfast. She wasn’t the composed reader of the daytime library. She was something Chaeyoung hadn’t seen before, and Mina knew it because she could feel her guard’s gaze like a physical presence, like the heat of a nearby candle.

“First north shelf, top row,” Mina murmured, moving between the shelves with steps that didn’t hesitate. “There should be a volume bound in dark leather, no title on the spine.”

Chaeyoung positioned herself by the door, half guardian, half witness. From there she could watch the third-floor corridor and observe Mina at the same time. And she observed. Not with the thirty-second sweeps of the daytime library. With something continuous, uninterrupted, like someone seeing something for the first time and not wanting to miss anything.

Mina found the first book. She pulled it from the shelf with hands that trembled, not from fear but from anticipation, the tremor of someone who has been searching for something for months and finally holds it in her fingers. She opened it. The first page, in ancient calligraphy, read: _On the Nature of the Unborn Power. A compendium of observations and theories on the spontaneous manifestation of energy in individuals without formal training._

Her heart beat so hard she was certain Chaeyoung could hear it.

“Second shelf, third row,” she said, moving to the next. Her voice was a whisper, but inside the whisper something was alight. “A thick volume with blue ink edges.”

She found it. And a third, thinner, one that wasn’t on her list but whose title stopped her in her tracks: _Documented Cases of Late Manifestation in Dormant Lineages._ She took that one too.

Three books. Three pieces of a puzzle she’d spent months trying to assemble from fragments and guesses.

“Mina.”

She turned. Chaeyoung was watching her from the doorway with an expression that stopped her. It wasn’t the professional expression. It wasn’t the surveillance sweep or the trained neutrality. It was something open, almost vulnerable — the face of someone who has just seen something she didn’t expect and doesn’t know how to file it.

“Yes?” Mina asked.

Chaeyoung seemed to realize what her face was doing. The expression closed, not entirely, but enough, and what she said was:

“Nothing. Just… it’s the first time I’ve seen you like this.”

“Like what?”

Chaeyoung searched for the word. Mina watched her search for it, turn it over, weigh it, with the same seriousness she brought to everything Mina said.

“Lit up,” she said. Bright brown eyes looking at the knight.

The word landed in Mina’s chest like a stone in still water. Ripples. Silence after.

_Lit up._ Not beautiful, not elegant, not composed. _Lit up._ As if Chaeyoung had seen the fire behind all the composure and given it a name Mina would never have given herself.

“Books do that to me,” Mina said. Controlled. Perfect. An explanation that was truth and lie at the same time, because books did do that to her, but tonight the fire wasn’t only about the books.

Chaeyoung nodded. Accepted the explanation without pressing. But something in her eyes said she’d filed the image away, stored it in that nameless place they both shared without knowing it, where the important things lived that weren’t said aloud.

Mina moved toward the door with the three books against her chest. The space between her and Chaeyoung shrank to the width of the doorframe. The moonlight from a distant window illuminated them in halves, one side silver, the other shadow.

“Why did you become a knight?”

The question emerged with the softness of something that has been waiting for its moment. Not calculated. Simply ready.

Chaeyoung went still. Mina saw the change in her eyes, the gaze going to a distant place, to something with texture and weight and probably the smell of smoke. The answer was there, on the surface, wanting out. Mina could see it forming.

But Chaeyoung came back. And what arrived on her face wasn’t the usual almost-smile but something sadder, more honest, more real than any expression Mina had seen on her in two weeks.

“Maybe another night, Your Highness.”

It wasn’t a rejection. It was a promise wrapped in a refusal, a door that opens just enough for you to see the light on the other side before closing, so you know the light exists and that someday you’ll be let in.

Mina nodded. Didn’t press. Stored the promise in the same place where she stored the warmth in her palms and the sound of Chaeyoung’s breathing through the wall and the fraction of a second in the dark corridor where their eyes met at the distance of a secret.

“Another night,” she repeated. And she gave _another night_ the weight of something that would wait as long as necessary.

---

The way back was faster. Or it felt faster, because the weight of the way there, the tension of _what if they catch us,_ had been replaced by something different. Something lighter. Complicit.

Mina carried the three books wrapped in a cloth Chaeyoung had given her without being asked. Chaeyoung led the route with the same precision as before, but there was something different in her steps. Less tense. As if the successful mission had loosened a bolt she normally kept tight.

There were no patrols this time. The path was clean, and in fifteen minutes they were back in the north wing, at the door of the temporary chambers, with the night guards still at the far end of the corridor.

Chaeyoung checked the interior. Forty-five seconds.

“Clear.”

They entered. Mina placed the books on the bed with the care of someone setting down something fragile and valuable. She stood looking at them a moment, the three spines aligned, the promise of answers she’d been seeking for months.

Then she looked at Chaeyoung.

They were in the space between the bedroom and the antechamber, that threshold where they separated every night. Chaeyoung to her bed, Mina to her bed, the door between them like a border both respected.

But tonight the border felt different. Thinner. More permeable. Like a page read so many times that light passes through it.

“Thank you, Chaeyoung.”

“Don’t thank me, Your Highness. If we’d been caught, Jihyo would have killed me.”

“Worse than the stabbing?”

“Significantly worse. The stabbing was a knife. Jihyo would be a full execution with a speech included.”

The laugh caught her by surprise. Not the composed laugh she used in the dining hall or the measured smile of the library. A real laugh, short, pulled from a place she hadn’t visited in days. The sound filled the room for a second and then left, but it left something behind: a warmth in the air that had nothing to do with palms or books.

Chaeyoung was looking at her. And Mina saw on her face something that stopped her: the shadow of a smile. Not the almost-smile, not the shadow of the shadow. A real smile, small, that lasted one second before Chaeyoung stored it away with the quickness of someone unaccustomed to letting those things live on her face.

But Mina had seen it. And it was, she decided in that instant, one of the most beautiful things she’d ever seen. Which was an absurd and inconvenient thought utterly impossible to file under any rational category, so she stored it in the nameless place where she kept everything else, the place that was filling so fast it would soon need a name or it would overflow.

“Enjoy your reading, Your Highness”

“Thank you.” she murmur softly. “Good night, Chaeyoung.”

“Good night.”

Chaeyoung crossed through the antechamber door. Mina heard her moving on the other side: the belt, the boots, the creak of the bed. The sounds that were already part of her nighttime vocabulary.

Mina sat on the bed. Looked at the three books. Picked up the first, _On the Nature of the Unborn Power,_ and opened it.

The first page was an introduction. The second, a table of contents. The third was where the actual text began, and Mina read the opening lines with her heart in her throat:

_The unborn power is not the absence of power. It is power that waits. It sleeps in the blood like a seed in dry earth, and when it wakes, it does so in response not to will or instruction, but to emotion. Warmth in the palms. Pressure behind the eyes. The world changing shape for an instant before returning to its place, leaving the bearer with the certainty that something moved, though they cannot prove what._

Mina lowered the book. Looked at her palms. The warmth was there. Faint, steady, like an ember no one had taught her to light.

_Warmth in the palms._

Someone had felt this before. Someone had written it down, studied it, understood it enough to leave it in a book behind a locked door on the third floor of a library.

She wasn’t alone.

She kept reading. The candle burned. The hours passed. And on the other side of the door, which Mina hadn’t fully closed, a centimeter of light seeped into the antechamber, a golden thread falling across the stone floor and reaching the edge of the bed where Chaeyoung wasn’t sleeping.

Both awake. Both in silence. Both on either side of a door that was no longer a border.

---

Jihyo didn’t sleep that night.

She was in her office, door closed, with a candle that had been burning for hours. On the desk, three things: Dahyun’s report with the lot registry, Momo’s notes on Callen’s movements, and a map of the castle where she’d traced the routes the infiltrators had used to enter.

The three threads were touching. The knife lots, commissioned by someone with the rank of knight or above. Callen’s movements through zones that weren’t his. The infiltrators’ access route, which required knowledge of internal patrol patterns.

They still weren’t knotted. Something was missing. One more piece, one data point, one connection that would turn coincidence into certainty.

Momo was sitting across from her. Backward on the chair, arms crossed over the backrest and chin resting on her forearms. She was eating something, some kind of stuffed bread that had appeared from an improbable pocket of her uniform, and staring at the map with the silent concentration she only showed when something truly mattered to her.

“The corridor between the armory and the cells,” Jihyo said, pointing at the line on the map. “What time did you say he passed the third time?”

“At dawn. Right after the shift change.”

“The cell shift change is at six. If Callen passed right after, he could have seen who was going in and who was coming out. He could have known which guards were on and which weren’t.”

“Does that matter?”

“It matters if you want to know when the cells have the least surveillance.” Jihyo touched the map where the cells were. “The prisoner was poisoned between midnight and six. The lock was opened with a key. If Callen was mapping the shifts…”

Momo stopped chewing. “He could know when to go in.”

Jihyo looked at her. Momo looked at Jihyo. And in the silence of an office at two in the morning, with a dying candle and a map full of lines that were beginning to converge, they both saw the same thing.

It wasn’t proof. Not yet. But it was something that pulsed with the cadence of truth, something that fit with too much precision to be coincidence.

“I need one more piece,” Jihyo said. “Something that connects him directly to the infiltrators or to the prisoner’s death. Without that, all I have is suspicious patterns and a knife commission anyone with rank could have made.”

“What kind of piece?”

Jihyo thought. The candle crackled. Wax fell onto the table in a drop that solidified before reaching the map.

“Tomorrow I want you to follow him. Not closely. Not so he knows you’re watching. I want to know where he goes when he thinks no one’s looking.”

Momo nodded. Swallowed the last bite of bread. Rose from the chair, which spun on one leg from the momentum, and headed for the door.

“Momo.”

“Yes?”

“Good work.”

Momo smiled. The smile was wide, honest, without the layers that princess smiles had or the absence of them on Jihyo’s face. It was the smile of someone who has done something important and knows it and doesn’t need it explained.

“Thanks, Captain.”

She left.

Jihyo remained alone with the map and the candle and the shadow of a name she couldn’t yet speak but that already had a shape.

_Callen._

Three days. A lot mark. A pattern of movements. And tomorrow, if Momo did what Momo knew how to do, the missing piece.

She blew out the candle. Darkness covered the office, the map, the converging threads. But Jihyo kept seeing them. Because threads didn’t need light to exist. They only needed someone with the patience to follow them to the end.

And Jihyo had patience. It was the only thing she had in excess.
