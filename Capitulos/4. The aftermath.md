The shout brought the castle back to life.

Chaeyoung heard the responses before she saw them: boots on stone, the clinking of armor in motion, voices cutting through the royal wing's silence with the urgency of those who wake trained for the worst. The corridor guards arrived first. Two. Swords drawn, torches raised, eyes scanning the hallway as if expecting to find a battle in progress. What they found was Chaeyoung standing in the doorframe. Uniform soaked on the left side. Right hand gripping the frame. Behind her, the third princess's bedroom with a corpse on the floor and a pool of blood that wasn't only the dead man's.

"Infiltration," Chaeyoung said. Her voice was working. Her body, less so. "Two attackers. One dead inside. One escaped through the window, heading south, less than a minute ago. The princess is unharmed."

She said it all in one go. Protocol. Tactical report. The words came out in the right order because she'd trained them thousands of times: report first, feel later.

The guards exchanged a look. One entered the room with the torch. The light fell on the body on the floor, on the scattered books, on the knife embedded in the infiltrator's forehead, and the guard paused a second longer than was professionally acceptable.

"Notify Captain Jihyo," Chaeyoung said. "And Princess Nayeon."

"Lieutenant, your shoulder—"

"Notify Captain Jihyo. Now."

The guard left. The second took position at the door.

Chaeyoung turned toward the room. Mina was where she'd left her, against the wall, beside the bed, hands pressed to her chest. The torchlight had changed everything. The darkness that had been ally and enemy during the fight had retreated, and in its place was the naked truth of what had happened: the body, the blood, the broken books, the open window, the cold night air entering as if nothing had happened.

Mina wasn't looking at the dead man. She was looking at Chaeyoung.

At her shoulder. At the stain the torch now illuminated in full, dark, wet, spreading across the uniform's fabric with the patient slowness of something that's in no hurry because it knows it's going to reach everywhere.

"Your Highness." Chaeyoung walked toward her. Tried to make her steps steady. Didn't entirely succeed. "Are you hurt? Did they touch you?"

Mina shook her head. A small, almost automatic movement.

"Are you sure? I need you to tell me if—"

"They didn't touch me."

Mina's voice sounded different. It wasn't the soft whisper of the terrace or the polished composure of the library. It was something stripped, raw, the voice of someone using all their energy to hold the words together.

"Good. That's good." Chaeyoung nodded. She offered her right hand. "We're going to get you out of here. We'll move you to another room while they secure this one."

Mina looked at the hand. Looked at the shoulder. Back to the hand.

"You're bleeding."

"Yes."

"A lot." the princess insisted.

"It looks worse than it is. Shoulder wounds bleed a lot. It's the position of the—"

"Chaeyoung."

The name, like that, without title, without "lieutenant" or “knight” or without the protocol distance Chaeyoung had been using as a trench since she shouted in the hallway. Just the name. Two syllables that cut through the tactical speech more effectively than any command.

Mina was looking at her with an expression Chaeyoung hadn't seen in a week of teas and terraces and conversations about ceilings. It wasn't fear, or not only fear. It was something more complicated, something with layers that Chaeyoung, with blood running down her arm and the edge of her vision going gray, didn't have the capacity to disassemble.

"You need help," Mina said. Not as a question.

"I need you to be somewhere safe. Then I'll take care of this."

"After that you could faint."

"I'm not going to faint, Your Highness."

"Is that a promise or an estimate?"

Something escaped Chaeyoung. Not a smile; no smile was possible with a hole in her shoulder and a corpse three meters away. But something loosened in her expression for a second, something involuntary, provoked by the absurd fact that this woman who had just woken to a dead man in her bedroom could still form a sentence with that precision.

"An estimate with a high degree of confidence," she replied. And she didn't know why she said it. It wasn't professional. It wasn't protocol. It was something that came from the same place as the confession about the training yards.

Mina took her hand.

Not the one Chaeyoung had offered; that was the right, the functional one, extended in a practical gesture. Mina took the left. The one hanging limp. The one that belonged to the arm of the wounded shoulder, the hand that was losing strength and warmth.

She took it carefully. With the gentleness of someone who knows they're touching something broken. Mina's fingers were cold and trembling, but they closed around Chaeyoung's with a firmness that didn't match the tremor. Chaeyoung looked at her, directly in the eyes.

Something happened. Something that shouldn't have had space in this room, between the blood and the fear and the corpse and the guards beginning to gather in the hallway. Something that should have waited for a better moment, cleaner, more dignified. But it happened anyway, because the important things rarely have the courtesy to wait. "Let's go," Chaeyoung said. Softer than she'd intended.

They left together.

Mina didn't let go of her hand until they reached the hallway, where the torchlight was stronger and there were more guards, and the world went back to being a place with rules and protocols and appropriate distances between a guard and her princess.

Then she let go. Slowly. Finger by finger, as if she were closing a book she wasn't ready to stop reading.

Chaeyoung felt the cold where Mina's fingers had been. She cataloged it. Stored it somewhere that had no name. And went back to work.

Jihyo arrived the way she arrived at everything: fast, in control, and with the face of someone already solving the problem before having all the information.

Chaeyoung saw her at the end of the hallway, the unmistakable silhouette even in half-light, long strides, hand already on her sword's hilt. Four more knights followed behind her, but Jihyo walked ahead with the natural distance of someone who doesn't need to give the order to follow because people simply do.

She wasn't wearing armor. Sleeping shirt tucked into trousers, boots half-laced, hair pulled back in something that barely qualified as functional. She'd dressed in less than a minute. Chaeyoung knew because she knew that version of Jihyo, the one who slept light and activated in seconds, like a bow always at half-draw.

Jihyo swept the hallway with her gaze as she arrived. The posted guards. Mina's open bedroom door. The blood on the floor, drops, an intermittent trail running from the door to where Chaeyoung stood.

Her eyes landed on Chaeyoung. Dropped to the shoulder. Rose to the face.

Chaeyoung knew that look. The assessment. Quick, unsentimental, calibrating damage and functionality in the same second. _Are you standing? Can you talk? Do I need to replace you or can I get a report first?_

"Lieutenant," Jihyo said. Just that. The rank. The way she said it contained the question.

"Two infiltrators, Captain. They entered through the window of the princess's chambers. Professionals, trained, equipped, coordinated. I eliminated one. The other escaped through the same window, heading south, approximately four minutes ago. Princess Mina is unharmed."

"The dead one's inside?"

"Yes. Knife in the forehead. The one who escaped has a blow to the temple but was functional when he left." Jihyo nodded. Processed. Turned to the knights behind her.

"Kwan, Lee: search the south perimeter of the castle. Exterior wall, rooftops, gardens. Look for ropes, marks on the stone, any access point." Jihyo's voice wasn't loud. It didn't need to be. It had the kind of authority that works better at low volume. "Min, Hirai: reinforce the royal wing entrances. No one enters or leaves without authorization from the crown or me. No one. Notify the wall guard that active threat protocol is in effect."

The four moved. Without questions, without hesitation. Jihyo gave orders the way other people breathed: naturally and without thinking about it.

She turned to the guard posted at the door.

"Has anyone notified Princess Nayeon?"

"Yes, Captain. We sent a messenger—"

"Good." She cut him off without harshness, simply because she already had the information she needed. "No one touches anything inside the room until I've inspected it. The body stays where it is."

The guard nodded.

Jihyo walked toward Chaeyoung. Three steps. Stopped in front of her. At this distance, under the torchlight, Chaeyoung could see what Jihyo wasn't showing in her voice: the tension in the jaw, the line between the eyebrows that only appeared when something truly worried her, the micro-adjustment in posture that said she wanted to touch her shoulder and was restraining herself because guards were watching.

"How much blood have you lost?"

"I can serve"

"That's not what I asked."

"Captain—"

"Chaeyoung." Low. Just for her. Not Lieutenant. Chaeyoung. "How much blood?"

Chaeyoung assessed. Was honest, because with Jihyo there was no point in not being; the woman read lies the way others read smoke signals.

"More than I'd like. Less than worries me. The wound needs pressure and sutures, but I don’t think it's arterial. I have reduced mobility in the left arm and my vision blurred a while ago, but it's stabilized."

Jihyo looked at her a second longer. The silent assessment, that entire conversation they held without words, built over years of shared battles and trust earned one wound at a time.

_Are you really okay?_ Jihyo frowned.

_I will be._ Chaeyoung nodded slightly.

_Do I need to relieve you?_ A slight movement of the head to one side from the Captain.

_I'm not moving from here._ A strong but quick shake of the head.

Jihyo nodded. Accepted it. Not because she liked it, Chaeyoung could see she didn't, but because she knew her lieutenant well enough to know which battles she could win and which she couldn't.

"I'm sending Tzuyu. Until she gets here, pressure on the wound. With something clean, not your hand."

"Yes, Captain."

"And if you faint before she arrives, I'll kill you myself."

"Noted, Captain."

Jihyo held her gaze a second longer. There was something there, not concern, because concern was too soft a word for what Jihyo felt for the people under her command. It was something closer to the contained fury of someone who just learned one of her own was hurt and doesn't yet have anyone to blame.

Then she turned and entered Mina's room.

Chaeyoung watched her cross the threshold. From where she stood, she could see Jihyo stop over the corpse, examine the knife in the forehead, the infiltrator's clothing, the hands, looking for marks, insignias, tattoos, anything that would say who sent him. She saw her crouch, check the pockets with methodical fingers, examine the quality of the fabric, the edge of the knife she'd picked up from the floor.

_She's not going to find anything,_ Chaeyoung thought. And she thought it with a certainty that made her nauseous, because people who scale the royal wing of a castle at night and carry ropes to take a princess alive don't leave business cards.

_They carried ropes. They didn't come to kill her._

The realization hit now, with the delay adrenaline always imposed on important thoughts. She had registered it during the fight, the first infiltrator had a rope, but there hadn't been time to process it. Now there was.

They came to kidnap Mina. Not to assassinate her. They wanted her alive.

That was worse.

Because an assassin fulfills an objective and it's over. A kidnapping means plans. Means someone who wants something from her, something that requires her breathing, conscious, available for whatever they have in mind.

Chaeyoung clenched her jaw. The shoulder protested. She ignored it.

She turned to where she'd left Mina, in a hallway chair, three meters away, flanked by two guards maintaining a respectful distance and a posture that screamed "_we don't know what to do with a princess in a nightgown._"

Mina wasn't trembling. Not anymore. She was sitting with her hands together in her lap, back straight, composure rebuilt piece by piece like someone dressing after a shipwreck. Her gaze was fixed on a point on the floor, not vacant but focused. Processing.

_She notices everything,_ Chaeyoung had thought in the library, a lifetime ago. _She collects details about people the way others collect coins._

What details was she collecting now? The blood? The knife? The sound the body made when it fell? The way Chaeyoung had told her _It's alright_ with the decreasing conviction of a lie wearing thin with repetition?

Mina looked up. Her eyes found Chaeyoung's across the hallway. Three meters apart, two guards between them, and still the contact was direct, unobstructed, as if the space between them were a formality neither was taking seriously.

Chaeyoung made a gesture. Small. A nod that said _I'm here, I didn't leave, I'm not leaving._

Mina didn't return the gesture. Didn't nod or smile or do anything the guards could register. But something changed in her eyes, something settled, something that had been floating found a place to land.

And they stayed there. Three meters from each other. Waiting for Nayeon to arrive, for Tzuyu to arrive, for the castle to finish waking and the weight of what had happened to begin distributing among more people.

But for now, the weight belonged only to them.

Nayeon didn't run.

Chaeyoung noticed because it was the first thing she expected: quick steps, visible urgency, an older sister crossing the royal wing toward the room where someone had just tried to kidnap the youngest. But the steps she heard at the end of the corridor were measured. Firm. The rhythm of someone who had already made decisions while walking and didn't need to rush because panic wasn't going to serve her.

Princess Nayeon appeared under the torchlight and Chaeyoung understood something about her that she hadn't understood at dinner, or on the terrace, or in any of the week's brief encounters: this woman wasn't a princess playing at politics. She was a ruler. And the difference showed at three in the morning, when circumstances stripped away the courtesy like bandages from a wound.

She was dressed, not in a nightgown, not half-assembled. A dark, sober dress, hair up. She had taken the extra minutes not to appear as someone yanked from sleep. Which meant she had received the news, processed it, and decided that the image she projected in the next few minutes mattered as much as the orders she gave.

Two personal guards flanked her. She dismissed them with a gesture before reaching the group, like someone parting curtains.

Her eyes found Mina first. Swept her top to bottom, quick, clinical, searching for wounds. Mina was in the chair, back straight and hands folded, and when her eyes found her sister's, something moved in her expression, something that wanted to come undone, that wanted to be the scared little sister who had just woken to a dead man in her bedroom. Nayeon didn't give her room for that.

"Are you hurt?"

"No."

"Did they touch you?"

"No, Nayeon. I'm fine."

Nayeon nodded. Once. The relief existed; Chaeyoung saw it, a flash that crossed her eyes for half a second, but it was immediately consumed by what came after. The jaw closed. The shoulders squared. The sister disappeared and the heir replaced her with the fluidity of a changing of the guard.

She turned to the hallway. To the open door, the room, what was inside.

"Captain Jihyo."

Jihyo appeared in the doorway. She had something in her hand, a scrap of dark fabric from the dead infiltrator's clothing.

"Your Highness."

"Report."

"Two infiltrators accessed through the window of Princess Mina's bedroom. Professional climbing tools; hook marks on the sill, wax-treated rope to reduce noise. Lieutenant Chaeyoung detected the intrusion and neutralized both. One dead, one escaped. The dead one carries no identification, insignias, or visible marks. The clothing is generic, black-dyed linen, no stitching from any recognizable workshop. The knife is standard manufacture, no smith's marks." Jihyo spoke the way she wrote reports: clean, unadorned, each sentence a piece that fit with the next.

"Access was through the least guarded route in the wing. The window faces the south wall, where the patrol has a six-minute interval between rounds. They knew the pattern."

Nayeon didn't visibly react. But Chaeyoung saw the weight of that last phrase settle, _they knew the pattern,_ and what it meant: someone with access to internal information. Someone inside, or someone who had observed long enough.

"The one who escaped?"

"My men are searching the south perimeter. If he used the same entry route to exit, we'll find the marks. I've activated active threat protocol across the castle."

"I want him found, Captain."

"We'll find him, Your Highness."

The two looked at each other. Nayeon and Jihyo. There was something in that exchange Chaeyoung recognized, not animosity, not distrust, but neither the comfortable deference of a well-oiled relationship. It was functional tension.

Two women accustomed to control occupying the same space, respecting each other but aware that their jurisdictions touched without overlapping.

Nayeon turned. Her gaze passed over Chaeyoung. Over the shoulder. Over the blood.

"Is that blood yours or the infiltrator's?"

"Mine, Your Highness. A knife in the left shoulder. Not arterial."

"Do you need medical attention?"

"The Captain has already sent for the healer."

"Good."

That was all.

There was no thank you. No "you saved my sister." Nothing that acknowledged Chaeyoung had fought two professionals in the dark, taken a knife in the body, and killed a man with his own weapon, all so Mina could be sitting in that chair unharmed and with her composure rebuilt.

Chaeyoung didn't expect it. What she felt wasn't resentment; it was something more diffuse, more professional. The recognition that for Nayeon, this was the job working as it should. The guard protected. The princess survived. The gears turned correctly. You don't thank a gear for turning.

"Mina." Nayeon walked toward her sister. Stopped in front of the chair. Looked down at her with an expression that aimed to be soft but was made of the same material as everything else that night: control. "We're going to transfer you to another room. Your chambers will be sealed until Jihyo finishes the inspection. You'll sleep in the north wing tonight."

"Nayeon—"

"Tomorrow we'll discuss permanent measures. Tonight I need you somewhere safe."

"I'm already somewhere safe. Chaeyoung—"

"Knight Chaeyoung did her job. And we'll make sure she has everything she needs to keep doing it. But right now I need you to move to the north wing."

Chaeyoung watched the exchange from her three meters of distance. Watched the way Mina opened her mouth to say something more and closed it. Watched the way her shoulders, which had been straight, rebuilt, held up by sheer willpower, dropped half a centimeter. Not from relief. From surrender.

It was a movement Chaeyoung had already seen. In the library, when a servant interrupted and Mina closed the book without protest. At breakfast, when Nayeon changed the subject and Mina left her opinion half-said. On the terrace, when she talked about the sky and the walls and the ceilings with the quiet resignation of someone who has accepted that the cage is permanent.

_They didn't touch me,_ Mina had said when Nayeon asked. And it was true.

But Chaeyoung wondered, watching that half-centimeter in the shoulders, how many times she had been touched in ways that don't leave marks.

"All right," Mina said.

Nayeon nodded. Turned to the guards.

"Escort Princess Mina to the north wing. Third-floor room. Two guards at the door, one at each window." The guards moved. Mina stood.

And in the space between sitting and walking, that second where the chair no longer held her and the steps hadn't yet carried her, she looked at Chaeyoung.

It wasn't a long look. It wasn't dramatic. It was something that happened in the time it takes to blink, something the guards didn't register and Nayeon didn't see because she was already turned toward Jihyo with the next question on her lips.

But Chaeyoung saw it.

And what she saw disarmed her more than the knife.

Mina was looking at her the way you look at the only person in a room who has seen you. Not your title, not your crown, not your place in the line of succession. You. The person behind all of that. The one who trembles and rebuilds and trembles again where no one can see.

The look lasted one second.

Then Mina walked toward the guards, followed protocol, and disappeared down the north wing corridor with the perfect posture of a princess who knows exactly what obedience looks like.

Chaeyoung stayed where she was. Standing. Bleeding. With the ghost of that look etching itself into some place that was going to hurt her more than the shoulder.

To her left, inside the room, Jihyo inspected the window. To her right, down the corridor, Nayeon gave orders with the precision of a clockmaker adjusting mechanisms.

The castle kept waking. The night kept moving. And Chaeyoung had the certainty, with the same clarity that had shown her the infiltrator's pulse in the darkness, that nothing was going to be the same as before she opened that door.

Tzuyu arrived with her hands already prepared.

Chaeyoung saw her at the end of the corridor, the tall silhouette, the long stride that ate distance without needing to run, the leather bag she carried everywhere slung over her shoulder with a strap that had already taken the permanent shape of her body. She carried an oil lamp in one hand and an expression that, to anyone who wasn't her sister, would have appeared perfectly calm.

Chaeyoung was her sister. And she saw what was underneath.

Tzuyu looked at her shoulder from five meters away. Her mouth closed, a minimal movement, the lower lip pressing against the upper with the force of someone holding something back. The eyes stayed on the bloodstain a second longer than necessary. The hands, the hands that never trembled, that sutured wounds with a needleworker's precision and a clockmaker's patience, adjusted on the bag's strap with a grip that whitened the knuckles.

Then she let go. And when she reached Chaeyoung, she was Tzuyu the healer, not Tzuyu the little sister who had just seen the only family she had left bloodied at three in the morning.

"Sit down."

"I can stand—"

"Sit down."

Chaeyoung sat.

She did it on the stone bench beside the wall, the same one where guards rested during long shifts. Her legs thanked the solid surface with an honesty the rest of her body had refused to show. Only now, upon ceasing to bear her own weight, did she realize how much energy it had been costing her to stay upright.

Tzuyu set the lamp on the floor. Opened the bag. The movements were methodical, practiced: small vials lined up by size, bandages rolled with military precision, a set of needles and thread she kept in a soft leather case. Everything in its place. Every gesture with intention.

She cut the uniform fabric around the shoulder without asking permission. The scissors were sharp and the cuts clean. The wound was exposed under the lamplight, a gash about five centimeters long, deep in the center, with the irregular edges of a blade that had entered slowly instead of cleanly.

Tzuyu examined it. Said nothing for a long while, long enough for Chaeyoung to start getting nervous, which was ridiculous because she had endured the knife going in without making a sound but her little sister's silence was infinitely harder to tolerate.

"It's not arterial," she said finally.

"That's what I said."

"The muscle is compromised. Anterior deltoid, possibly part of the supraspinatus. You'll have reduced mobility for weeks if you take care of it." Pause. "Which means you won't take care of it."

"Tzuyu—"

"Was the knife clean?"

"Clean enough?" Chaeyoung hesitated. It's not like she had the time to check if the knife was clean.

"Clean enough or clean?"

"I didn't check..."

Tzuyu looked at her. It was a look Chaeyoung knew by heart, the one that said _you are the most exasperating person I know and I love you so much it hurts and if you do this to me again I'll be the one who stabs you._

She didn't say any of that. What she said was "I'm going to clean the wound with moon tincture. It's going to burn." "I've had worse."

"I know. I've treated all of them."

The moon tincture burned exactly as Tzuyu promised, a clean, transparent pain that climbed up her arm and made her clench her teeth hard enough for something to crack. Chaeyoung stayed still. Tzuyu worked without hurry, cleaned, disinfected, examined the depth with fingers that were firm and gentle at the same time, as if she could separate the healer from the sister and make them both collaborate without getting in each other's way.

"It needs sutures. Eight stitches, maybe ten."

"Do what you have to do."

Tzuyu threaded the needle. The lamp flame reflected off the metal for a second before the first stitch entered the skin. Chaeyoung didn't move. She had learned years ago to stay still while Tzuyu sewed, not from bravery but because the first time she'd moved during a suture, Tzuyu had told her with terrifying calm that if she did it again she would anesthetize her with a blow to the head and finish the job while she was unconscious. Chaeyoung never found out if it was a joke. She decided not to find out.

“What the hell happened?” Tyuzu murmured.

“Infiltration in Princcess Mina chambers.” the knight responded quickly.

"How many were there?" Tzuyu asked while she worked. Voice low. Just for them.

"Two. Professionals. One dead, one escaped."

"The princess?"

"Unharmed. They moved her to the north wing."

Second stitch. Third. Tzuyu's fingers didn't falter.

"And you?" The question sounded different from the ones before. It wasn't medical. It was personal. "How are you?" "Functional."

"That's not what I asked."

It was the second time she'd heard that phrase in the last hour. First Jihyo, now Tzuyu. The two people in the world who knew her best, both telling her exactly the same thing with the same words, as if there existed a secret manual titled _How to Deal with Son Chaeyoung When She Lies About Her Condition._

"I'm…" She searched for a word. Couldn't find one that was both true and acceptable at the same time. "I'm here." Tzuyu accepted that. Maybe because it was the most honest thing she was going to get, or maybe because she knew how to pick her battles, something Chaeyoung had never learned.

Fifth stitch. Sixth. The pain had become something rhythmic, predictable, almost comforting in its consistency.

"Chaeyoung?"

"Mm?"

"If I could tie you to this bench and leave you here until the wound closed, I would. I want you to know that." Chaeyoung just looked at her from the bench.

"But I can't. So what I'm going to do is bandage you, give you something for the pain that you're going to take, and remind you that if the stitches open, you come find me immediately. Not tomorrow. Not later. Immediately. Are we clear?"

"We're clear."

Tzuyu finished. Ten stitches. She cut the thread, cleaned the edges, applied an ointment that smelled of bitter herbs and something floral Chaeyoung didn't recognize. She bandaged the shoulder with an efficiency that turned every wrap of gauze into something that seemed choreographed.

When she finished, she placed her hand on Chaeyoung's healthy shoulder. One second. Two. The pressure was light but the message wasn't; it was the closest thing to a hug Tzuyu would allow herself in a corridor full of guards, and Chaeyoung received it with the same gravity with which she'd received every treatment, every suture, every loaded silence between the two of them.

"Thanks, Tzu."

"Don't thank me. Stop giving me work."

Tzuyu gathered her things. Every vial back in its place, every instrument clean and stored. She stood, slung the bag over her shoulder, and looked at Chaeyoung one more time.

Chaeyoung saw the exact moment her sister thought something and decided not to say it. The way she opened her mouth a fraction and closed it. The way her eyes lingered a second on the fresh bandage, white, that was going to be red in a few hours if Chaeyoung did what they both knew she was going to do.

_Sometimes Tzuyu looks at Chaeyoung and sees their father._

Tzuyu walked away down the corridor. Long strides, the bag on her shoulder, the straight back of someone who has just stitched up her sister for the umpteenth time and knows it won't be the last.

Chaeyoung watched her go. And wondered, as she wondered every time, if Tzuyu knew that half the wounds she brought her were the price of making sure her little sister never had to stitch her own.

Nayeon returned.

Chaeyoung was standing again, Tzuyu's fresh stitches pulling with every movement of the arm, reminding her they existed, that they demanded respect, that they weren't going to tolerate what she planned to put them through.

This time Nayeon had Jihyo with her. The two arrived together down the corridor, walking side by side, not as companions but as two forces advancing in the same direction for reasons that overlapped without being identical. Nayeon with the authority of the crown. Jihyo with the authority of the sword. Together they covered the full spectrum of power in Twiceland.

"Lieutenant." Nayeon stopped in front of her. Her eyes passed over the new bandage, registered it, filed it as processed data. "Captain Jihyo has informed me of her preliminary assessment. Security measures are going to change starting tonight."

Chaeyoung nodded. Waited.

"Additional guards in the royal wing. Two at each entrance, with four-hour rotations." Nayeon spoke with the cadence of someone carrying a mental list and checking it off. "The windows of Mina's bedroom will be sealed with reinforced internal locks. Patrol patterns change every three days instead of every week."

"I need someone on the south roof between midnight and dawn," Jihyo added. "The blind spot they exploited can't be covered from below."

Nayeon nodded.

"Do it. And one more thing." She turned to Chaeyoung. "Starting tonight, you sleep in the antechamber of Mina's quarters. I want someone inside, not just outside the door."

The antechamber. The small room connecting to the main chambers through an interior door, originally designed for ladies-in-waiting, used as a clothing storage room for the past several years. Fifteen steps from Mina's bed, separated by a door and a thin wall.

"Understood, Your Highness."

"The Captain will handle the logistics." Nayeon looked at her a moment longer. There was something there, not gratitude, not warmth, but perhaps the minimal acknowledgment that the person before her had just demonstrated that the decision to assign her to Mina had been correct. "Good work, Lieutenant."

Two words. The first that came close to recognition. Delivered with the same tone she'd use to approve a well-written report or a correct formation in the yard.

"Thank you, Your Highness."

Nayeon left. The guards followed. The corridor emptied of her presence the way a room empties of perfume, gradually, leaving a trace.

Jihyo stayed.

"The one who escaped," Chaeyoung said, before Jihyo could speak. "Did your men—"

"They caught him."

Chaeyoung raised her head.

"Fifteen minutes ago. On the south wall, as we predicted. He was trying to exit through the same entry route. He had a rope anchored at the base of the wall that we hadn't seen." Jihyo paused. "He's alive. He's in the cells." Something in Chaeyoung's chest loosened. Not relief; something closer to resolution. To the sensation that a piece that had been loose just clicked into place.

"Did he talk?"

"Not a word. They brought him in silent. He didn't resist capture, which tells me more than anything he could have said."

"Mercenary?"

"Perhaps," Jihyo said. The word weighed between them. "I'm going to interrogate him in a few hours. I want him to rest a bit first. To feel safe. To think we have time. And then I'm going to go in and explain exactly how much time he doesn't have."

Chaeyoung knew that tone. She'd heard it before every important battle, before every hard decision, the voice Jihyo used when she stopped being the competent captain and became something more frightening.

"Do you want me there?"

"No. I want you to go to the antechamber, lie down on something horizontal, and let your sister's stitches do their job for at least four hours."

"Jihyo—"

"I'm not asking, Lieutenant."

They looked at each other. The silent battle as always: Chaeyoung wanting to be everywhere at once, Jihyo knowing she couldn't and forcing her to choose.

"Four hours," Chaeyoung conceded.

"Four hours." Jihyo nodded. Turned to leave. Stopped. "Chaeyoung."

"Yes?"

"The knife thing. I can imagine what you did." A pause. Jihyo wasn't looking at her; she was looking down the corridor, at the door, at some middle point where certain things were easier to say. "Don't rip knives out of your body to throw at people. That's an order."

"I'll keep that in mind."

"No, you won't. But I wanted to say it anyway."

She left. The echo of her footsteps blended with the guards changing shift, with the distant sound of low-voiced orders, with the castle reorganizing itself around the wound someone had opened in it that night.

Chaeyoung stood alone in the corridor.

She allowed herself a second. Just one. Closed her eyes. Breathed. Her shoulder ached, her body was heavy, and somewhere in the north wing Mina was in a room that wasn't hers, surrounded by guards who weren't her, trying to sleep after having seen a man die for the first time.

_Dad. Everyone's alive…_

The voice didn't answer this time. Sometimes it didn't. The dead don't always have something to say, even the ones who live inside your head.

Chaeyoung opened her eyes. Straightened up. Walked to Mina's antechamber to serve the four hours she wasn't going to serve.

The castle cells were beneath the east wing, where the air changed temperature and smell, colder, damper, with that mineral density of stone that never sees the sun. The torches here were spaced farther apart than in the rest of the castle, not from neglect but by design. Darkness was part of the furniture.

Jihyo descended the stairs without hurry. Each step resonated against the walls with a clarity that didn't exist above; down here sound had nowhere to go, bouncing and amplifying, turning every footstep into an announcement.

The cell guard stood when he saw her.

"Captain. Cell three. He hasn't said a word."

"I know. You can leave."

"Captain?"

"Leave. Wait upstairs. No one comes down until I come up."

The guard hesitated for a second. A second was more than most took to question Jihyo. But something about the situation, the hour, the tension, the blood still staining the corridors above, gave him permission for the hesitation.

Jihyo didn't take it from him. She just waited. And the guard went upstairs.

Cell three was at the end of the corridor. Thick iron bars, stone floor with nothing but a bench bolted to the wall. The man was sitting on the bench. Hands bound in front with rope, not shackles, because the shackles had been under repair for a month, something Jihyo mentally noted as a problem she should have resolved sooner.

She stopped before the bars. Looked at him.

The infiltrator returned the look. No defiance, no fear, no curiosity. Flat eyes. The expression of someone who's been in cells before and knows the game ahead has rules he knows better than his captor.

Jihyo studied him. The black clothing was torn on the side, probably from the struggle during capture. Chaeyoung's blow had left a bruise on his temple that was beginning to darken. Middle-aged, solid build, large hands with calluses in the places weapons leave them. No rings, no visible tattoos, no notable scars. Generic. Designed not to be remembered.

"I'm going to be direct with you," Jihyo said. Voice low. She didn't need more. "Your partner is dead. Knife in the head, courtesy of the lieutenant who protects the princess you tried to kidnap. There's no one who can corroborate your version, which means the only story that exists is the one you decide to tell. Or the one I decide to write."

The man looked at her. Didn't move.

"I know you're a professional. I knew it the moment I saw the rope, wax-treated, military grade. I knew it from the access route, the south wall's blind spot, the six-minute interval between patrols. I'm not dealing with a thief who picked the wrong window. I'm dealing with someone who was hired, trained, and sent with specific information about this castle."

Silence. The man didn't even blink.

"What I want to know is who sent you. What I want to know is why they want Princess Mina alive. And what I want to know is how many more like you are out there."

Nothing. The cell returned the echo of her own voice and nothing else.

Jihyo waited. Ten seconds. Twenty. A full minute of silence that would have been unbearable for anyone who hadn't been trained to use it as a tool.

The man didn't speak. Didn't shift. Showed no discomfort, no impatience, no fear. He just sat on the bench with his bound hands and watched her with the empty patience of someone who knows silence is a weapon and that his is better than anything Jihyo could offer.

Jihyo saw it.

And understood.

This man wasn't going to talk. Not because he couldn't, not because he was calculating the best moment, not because he was waiting for an offer. He wasn't going to talk because not talking was part of the job. Because whoever had trained him had taught him that absolute silence was the final layer of defense, the one that worked when all others failed, and that death was preferable to what would happen if he opened his mouth.

That told Jihyo more than any confession.

"I understand," she said. Soft. Without rancor. "I hope you understand too that this doesn't end here."

She turned around. Walked toward the stairs. Her steps resonated with the same clarity with which they'd descended, but now the sound carried a different weight, that of someone who had just confirmed the problem was bigger than it appeared.

Nayeon was waiting in the council chamber.

At four thirty in the morning, the room felt different. Without the advisors, without the maps spread out, without daylight streaming through the windows, the space reduced to its essentials: a long table, two chairs, and two women accustomed to controlling everything sitting across from each other with something they couldn't control between them.

Nayeon had a cup of wine in front of her. She hadn't drunk it.

"Did he talk?"

"No." Jihyo sat without waiting for an invitation. The dynamic between them allowed it; it wasn't disrespect, it was economy of time. "Not a word. Not a gesture. He's professional in the most complete sense of the term. He's trained to be captured."

"What does that mean?"

"It means whoever sent him anticipated the possibility of failure. And prepared for it. The silence isn't an accident; it's a protocol. Someone taught him that staying quiet is more important than staying alive."

Nayeon processed. Jihyo watched her do it, the way her eyes moved slightly to the left when she was thinking, the index finger tapping the base of the cup without apparent rhythm.

"The clothing? The weapons?"

"Generic. Deliberately generic. No marks, no insignias, nothing pointing to a faction, a kingdom, or a group. The knives are standard manufacture; they could be from any smith on the continent. The rope is military grade but available at any supply port."

"Someone wants them to be untraceable."

"Someone who has the resources to equip professional operatives and the intelligence not to leave prints." Jihyo let the pause do its work. "And who has information about our security patterns."

The wine cup went very still under Nayeon's fingers.

"Are you suggesting there's someone inside the castle?"

"I'm saying they knew the exact interval of the south wall patrol. Six minutes. That can't be obtained by observing from the outside; the route isn't visible from any exterior point. Someone gave them that information, or someone with access to the patrol schedules leaked it."

The silence that followed was different from the one in the cell. That had been the silence of a professional doing his job. This was the silence of an heir processing that the walls of her own castle might have cracks that aren't made of stone.

"Who has access to the patrol schedules?"

"Guard officers. Shift coordinators. Royal wing administrators. And anyone who's been in the guard room when the schedules are laid out on the table, which includes more people than I'd like."

"Narrow it down."

"I will. But I need time. And I need it not to get out that we're looking inside. If there's a traitor and they learn we suspect, they'll disappear or change methods."

Nayeon looked at her. Jihyo held her gaze. There was tension there, not animosity, but the natural friction of two types of authority occupying the same problem. Nayeon wanted answers. Jihyo wanted time. Both knew the other was right and that it didn't make things easier.

"You have three days," Nayeon said.

"I need a week."

"You have five. And you report to me every day. I don't care if there's no progress. I want to know where you are." Jihyo nodded. Accepted it. Five days weren't enough and they both knew it, but it was the concession political power could give military power without feeling it was losing control.

"There's something else," Jihyo said.

"Tell me."

"They carried ropes. They came to take her, not to kill her."

Nayeon didn't react. Not visibly, but Jihyo saw the jaw clench a millimeter, the type of micro-movement only someone watching closely would notice.

"They want her alive," Jihyo continued. "Which rules out revenge, rules out terrorism, rules out half the motives we would have assumed. Someone wants Mina specifically, and they want her breathing. The question is what for." Nayeon's finger stopped tapping the cup.

"The prophecy," she said. Low. As if saying it out loud made it more real.

"It's a possibility."

"It's THE possibility, Jihyo. Who else would want the third daughter of the crown alive? She has no political position, no alliances, no value as a negotiation hostage. The only thing she has is…"

She didn't finish. She didn't need to.

The only thing Mina had was what the prophecy said she had. And if someone was willing to send professional operatives to scale the walls of a royal castle at night to take her, it meant someone believed in that prophecy enough to act.

"Strengthen security," Nayeon said. She stood. The wine cup remained full on the table. "Do what you have to do.

Find who sent them. And keep my sister safe."

She didn't say please. She didn't need to. The weight in the last sentence came with it included.

Jihyo stood.

"Your Highness." A pause. "Chaeyoung did more than her job tonight. The lieutenant fought two professionals in the dark, took a knife in the shoulder, killed one, and shielded the princess with her body. If she'd arrived three seconds later, this conversation would be very different."

Nayeon looked at her. Something crossed her expression, quick, hard to read, drowned before it could take full shape.

"I know," she said. And left the room.

Jihyo remained alone with the full cup of wine and the silence of a room that, in a few hours, would fill with advisors and maps and the collective fiction that everything was under control.

She looked at the cup. Considered drinking it.

She didn't. There was work to do.

Chaeyoung didn't sleep the four hours.

She slept two. Maybe less; there were no windows in the antechamber and the only way to measure time was the candle she'd left burning, which had consumed barely a third when she opened her eyes with her heart racing and her hand reaching for the sword she'd left beside the makeshift bed.

She didn't remember what had woken her. A sound, a dream, or simply the impossibility of staying still while the world moved without her. Probably the third.

She washed her face with water from the jug. Changed her uniform; the old one was stiff with dried blood, folded in a corner like evidence of something she didn't want to look at too long. The clean uniform was tight at the shoulder because of the bandage, but it worked. She tied her hair back. Fastened the belt. Took the sword.

And went out to the hallway to take her post.

The door to Mina's quarters, the temporary ones in the north wing, had two guards. Chaeyoung relieved them with a nod. She took her usual position, back to the wall, three steps from the door. As if it were any morning. As if nothing had changed.

Dawn came through the north wing windows with a softness that felt unjust after the night before. Golden light, fresh air, the distant song of birds in the gardens. The outside world hadn't learned that someone had tried to steal a princess. Her princess. Or if it had, it didn't care.

Chaeyoung waited.

When the door opened, Mina found her exactly as she found her every morning. Standing. Straight. Present. Mina stopped in the doorway.

The princess had dressed. Hair up, day clothes, composure reassembled with the meticulousness of someone who knows that form is the last thing you lose. But there were things form couldn't hide: the shadows under her eyes that said she hadn't slept either, the pallor the dawn couldn't disguise, the stillness subtly different from her usual stillness. This wasn't Mina's calm. It was the quiet of someone holding herself together.

Her eyes went to Chaeyoung's shoulder. To the bulge under the clean uniform where the bandage created a contour that hadn't existed yesterday.

Chaeyoung saw the path of that gaze, from shoulder to face, from face to the left arm hanging half a centimeter lower than the right, from arm to eyes, and she prepared herself for the professional, courteous _How are you?_ that morning protocol called for.

That wasn't what came out.

"You didn't rest," Mina said.

It wasn't a question. It was a reading. The same ability she had for knowing when a servant was lying, when Nayeon's smile tensed, when a verse was poorly written. Applied now to Chaeyoung with a precision that left no room for denial.

"I rested, Your Highness. A few hours."

"A few hours," Mina repeated. The tone was flat. Neutral. The kind of tone Chaeyoung was learning to dread because it meant Mina was choosing very carefully what she was going to say next.

"Enough to be operational."

"Operational." Another repetition. Mina took Chaeyoung's words and held them up in front of her as if they were objects that needed to be examined in the light. "Is that what you are when you have a hole in your shoulder and haven't slept? Operational?"

"It's what I need to be, Your Highness."

Mina looked at it. Looked at her. And Chaeyoung saw the exact moment something shifted in the princess's expression, something that moved from one place to another, like a chess piece repositioning, like the decision to turn from one page to the next.

"Last night," Mina said. Slowly. With the care of someone walking on ice. "I can't stop thinking about something." Chaeyoung waited.

"When the knife… when they hurt you. Before I woke up." Mina lowered her voice. Not for secrecy; for weight. The words were heavy and needed to be set down carefully. "You didn't scream."

Not a question. Another reading.

"No," Chaeyoung confirmed.

"You could have screamed. The guards would have come. They would have helped."

"They would have taken too long. And the noise would have changed the situation in ways I couldn't predict." "So you chose the knife."

"I chose the silence."

"You chose the pain. So I could keep sleeping a few seconds longer."

Chaeyoung didn't respond. There was no answer to give that wouldn't sound like justification or excuse, and neither served. The truth was simpler and more complicated than anything she could say: in the moment, there was no choice. No calculation. There was a knife entering her shoulder and the absolute certainty that the sound leaving her mouth could endanger the person sleeping on the other side of the room. And that was enough to swallow it. "I did it because it was the right thing to do," she said at last. Simple. True.

Mina looked at her for a time that felt longer than it probably was. Dark eyes, fixed, with that intensity Chaeyoung had only seen on the terrace when she talked about ceilings and open skies, the unfiltered version of Mina, the one that appeared in moments where composure wasn't enough to cover what was underneath.

"I'd like you not to do that," Mina said.

Chaeyoung blinked. "Your Highness?"

"Get hurt." Mina didn't look away. Didn't soften the tone. She said it the way you say things you've been thinking for hours, polished by internal repetition until they're smooth and exact. "I'd like you not to do that. Get hurt, I mean." The hallway went still.

Chaeyoung knew the shapes of gratitude. _Good work, Lieutenant,_ that was Nayeon. _If you faint I'll kill you,_ that was Jihyo. _Stop giving me work,_ that was Tzuyu. Every person in her life thanked her in a way that, at its core, accepted the premise that Chaeyoung was going to keep doing it. That she was going to keep taking knives and fighting in the dark and minimizing wounds with an _I'm fine_ no one believed.

No one had asked her to stop doing it.

No one had looked her in the eyes and said: _I'd like you not to._ Not _thank you for doing it._ Not _that was very brave._ Not _it's your duty._ But _I'd like you not to get hurt._ As if her pain were something that mattered to Mina on its own, not for the consequences, not for the logistics of having a functional guard, but because the idea of Chaeyoung suffering was unacceptable to her.

Chaeyoung didn't know what to do with that.

She stored it. In the same nameless place where she'd stored the cold of Mina's fingers releasing hers, the look across the hallway, the question about ceilings and the answer about training yards. A place that was filling fast and had no room for more, but kept accepting things anyway.

"I'll try, Your Highness," she said. And it was the most honest thing she could offer.

Mina nodded. Once. Accepting the answer with the same gravity with which she'd asked it.

"Thank you," she said. And that word, in her mouth, weighed more than the two Nayeon had given her that night.

The hallway started moving again. The sounds of the castle waking, footsteps, voices, the distant clatter of the kitchen preparing breakfast, filled the silence they had built between them. The day was beginning. They had to move, walk, function.

"Shall we have breakfast?" Mina asked. And the _shall we,_ the plural, the implied _us,_ was another small object Chaeyoung stored in the nameless place.

"After you, Your Highness."

They walked down the north wing corridor. Side by side. Not touching. With Chaeyoung's bandaged shoulder and Mina's sleepless eyes and the correct distance between a guard and her princess.

And if that distance felt a centimeter shorter than yesterday, neither of them mentioned it.

Jihyo went down to the cells four hours after the interrogation.

She had spent the morning working, reviewing the patrol shifts, interviewing the south wall guards, redrawing the access routes on a map she had spread across the guard room table. The red lines marking vulnerabilities multiplied every time she looked more closely. The castle she had believed secure was starting to look like something built on faith more than stone.

She had planned the second interrogation carefully. She was going to change the approach, fewer direct questions, more silences. She was going to sit inside the cell, not outside. She was going to bring food and water and leave them within the prisoner's reach without offering them. Patience was a weapon Jihyo knew how to wield better than most, and she was willing to sit in that cell for hours if necessary.

It wasn't necessary.

The guard intercepted her at the foot of the stairs. He was pale. His hands gripping the lance handle with a force that made the point tremble.

"Captain. There's a… there's a problem."

Jihyo knew before the guard finished speaking. She knew from the face, from the hands, from the way the man couldn't look at her directly, as if eye contact would make what he had to tell her more real.

"Show me," she said.

Cell three was open. Not forced, open. The lock intact, the bars unmarked, the door simply… open. As if someone with a key had entered and left without hurry.

The infiltrator was on the bench. Sitting. Eyes open. Head tilted slightly to the left, as if he'd fallen asleep looking at the wall.

Dead.

Jihyo entered the cell. Crouched before the body. Examined it with hands that didn't tremble because she couldn't afford that luxury.

No visible wounds. No blood. No signs of struggle, no skin under the nails, no indication he had tried to defend himself. The wrist bindings were intact, the same ropes she had checked four hours earlier.

The lips had a bluish tint. Subtle, but there.

_Poison._

Jihyo stood. Looked at the guard trembling in the doorway.

"Who came down to the cells after me?"

"N-no one, Captain. Only the morning shift guard. Jang, for the six o'clock change."

"Did Jang check on the prisoner?"

"He says… he says when he arrived he was already like that. Thought he was sleeping. Didn't approach until…" "Who else has a key to this cell?"

"You. The shift coordinator. And the backup copy in the key room of the east wing."

Three keys. Three access points. Any of them traceable, in theory. In practice, the key room was open during the day for officer access, and the backup copy had no usage log because they'd never needed one.

Because this had never happened before.

Jihyo looked at the dead man. The open eyes, vacant, staring at the stone wall with the same flat expression he'd shown during the interrogation. Even dead, the man maintained the silence. The final mission accomplished. Someone had entered their cells. Had passed their guards. Had used a key that was under her jurisdiction. And had silenced the only thread connecting the attack to whoever had ordered it.

The fury arrived cold. Not hot, not explosive; cold. The kind of fury that doesn't shout but calculates, that doesn't break things but dismantles them piece by piece looking for where the mechanism failed.

_I secured these cells. I placed the guards. I checked the bindings. And someone got in anyway._

It was professional. It was clean. It was exactly what an organization that treated failure as part of the plan would do, the same one that trained its operatives not to talk and had the resources to send someone to eliminate what might have talked.

And it meant something that churned her stomach more than the corpse:

The traitor was inside the castle. Not as a possibility. As a certainty.

Someone with access to the cells, the keys, and the knowledge that there was a prisoner who needed to be eliminated before dawn. Someone who walked the same hallways she did, ate in the same dining hall, perhaps slept in the same wing.

"No one touches this body," Jihyo said. Her voice came out level. Controlled. Everything she wasn't feeling. "I want Tzuyu to examine him before a single finger moves. And I want the access log for the key room: who entered, when, why. From yesterday at noon until now."

"Captain, there's no log of—"

"Then I want to know who was in the east wing between midnight and six. Every guard, every servant, every soul who walked that corridor. Names and schedules. On my desk before noon."

The guard left. Jihyo stayed alone with the dead man.

She looked at him one more time. The open eyes. The permanent silence. The mission accomplished even after death, because dead, the man was as useful to those who sent him as he had been alive. More, perhaps. Because alive, there was always the risk he might eventually talk. Dead, he was a door closed forever.

Jihyo climbed the stairs. Each step resonated against the walls with a clarity that no longer felt like design but like vulnerability, another one on a list that wouldn't stop growing.

She had five days. A corpse. A traitor. And the uncomfortable certainty that someone inside her own castle was outplaying her.

_Well, fuck._

That night, Mina closed the door of her temporary room and stood in the darkness.

She didn't light the candles. Didn't change her clothes. She just stood there, back against the door, listening to the sounds on the other side: the guards' footsteps, the distant murmur of the castle that kept functioning, and closer, in the antechamber, the sound of Chaeyoung settling onto the cot they'd brought that afternoon.

_She's there._

The thought should have comforted her. And it did, in part. The part that remembered the two figures in the dark, the blood, the sound of the body hitting the floor, felt better knowing that between her and the door there were three meters and a knight with a sword and the demonstrated willingness to use it.

But there was another part. The part that had spent the entire day walking through the castle with guards on every side, eating with Nayeon who watched her like a fragile object on a high shelf, listening to Sana try to make her laugh with an effort so visible it ached. The part that had nodded when they told her that her chambers were sealed, that she'd sleep here, that the guards at the windows were necessary, that the new measures were for her safety.

The part that had said _all right_ to everything because she didn't know what else to say.

Mina walked to the bed. Sat on the edge. The sheets were different from hers, rougher, with a different smell, the smell of a room that isn't yours and knows it.

_Someone tried to take me._

The phrase had accompanied her all day, silent, circling in her head with the insistence of a melody that won't leave. Not _someone tried to kill me_; that would have been terrible but simple. _Take me._ Pull her from her bed, her room, her castle, and carry her to a place she didn't know to do something she couldn't imagine. They wanted her alive. For something.

_For what?_

Nayeon hadn't told her. Nayeon never told her. Nayeon protected, shielded, controlled the edges of what Mina could and couldn't know with the same efficiency with which she controlled everything else. And Mina had allowed it, as always, because questioning Nayeon required an energy she'd never had and a courage she'd never found.

But tonight, in the darkness of a room that wasn't hers, with Chaeyoung's blood still fresh in her memory and the image of the falling body etched behind her eyelids, Mina felt something different.

It wasn't courage. Not yet. It was something that comes before courage, the precursor, the spark before the flame. It was the clear, sharp sensation, cutting as the knife that had been in Chaeyoung's shoulder, that this could not happen again.

Not the attack. Not the infiltration. Not the security breach.

This. Her, sitting on a bed, unable to do anything while someone else bled for her. Her, with empty hands, with a closed mouth, with a body frozen against the wall while Chaeyoung ripped a knife from her own shoulder and hurled it into a man's head to protect her.

_I'd like you not to do that. Get hurt, I mean._

She'd said that this morning. And she'd meant it with every fiber of her being. But saying it wasn't enough. Asking Chaeyoung to stop getting hurt was like asking water to stop flowing downhill; it went against her nature, her training, everything that made her who she was.

The only way for Chaeyoung to stop bleeding for her was for Mina to stop being someone who needed others to bleed for her.

She rose from the bed.

Walked to the wardrobe of the temporary room. It was smaller than hers, a functional piece of furniture without the elaborate carvings or the deep space of her royal chambers. But it held something she'd brought that afternoon, when the servants had allowed her to collect her belongings under the guards' supervision.

A cloth bag. Small. Heavy for its size.

She had slipped it between the clothes without anyone asking. The servants didn't inspect a princess's belongings. The guards didn't inspect what the servants didn't inspect. And Mina, who had spent months practicing the art of hiding things in plain sight, had done it with the ease of someone who's spent a lifetime being underestimated.

She took out the bag. Set it on the bed. Opened it.

The books were there. Three. The ones she'd been able to retrieve from the false panel in her chambers before they sealed them. They weren't the most important; those remained hidden behind the panel, and Mina would have to find a way to recover them. But these were enough to begin.

The first was thin, with a leather cover worn from use. _Principles of Elemental Energy,_ by an author who'd been dead for three hundred years. The second was thicker, with ink-stained page edges, margin notes from someone who'd studied it before her. _Theory of Flow and Form: Essays on the Nature of Power._ The third was the smallest, almost a notebook, with no title on the cover. She'd found it behind a library shelf months ago, handwritten in a tight, urgent script that looked like someone who needed to put ideas on paper before they escaped.

Mina ran her fingers over the covers. She knew every page of these books. She'd read them in secret, at night, with candles she blew out quickly when she heard footsteps. She had practiced the exercises in the first one, small, discreet things she could do with her palms under the sheets that no one would notice. She had memorized the theorems in the second. She had underlined the pages in the third that described something that felt too much like what she felt in her hands when she was alone.

She had never told anyone.

Because telling meant admitting that the quiet princess, the one who read poetry and drank tea and smiled when told to, had spent months secretly studying magic. That the manifestations she dismissed as coincidences, a cup that moved, a page that turned on its own, a breeze where there shouldn't have been one, weren't coincidences. That there was something inside her she didn't understand and that the books only half-explained.

But tonight, the quiet princess was sitting on a bed that wasn't hers, with her guard's blood in her memory and the certainty that the world had just shown her that passivity has a price and that others pay it.

Mina opened the first book.

_That cannot happen again._

She began to read.
