She wasn't going to sleep anymore. She knew it before she opened her eyes.

Chaeyoung lay still for a moment, staring at the ceiling of her room, listening to her own breathing and the thick silence of the sleeping castle. The bed was more comfortable than anything she'd ever had in Hwaren; thick mattress, clean sheets, a pillow that didn't smell like dust, and still she'd spent hours tossing and turning as if she were lying on stones.

She sat up slowly. Her left side reminded her it existed with a dull pull. Tzuyu had changed the bandage before bed with that silent efficiency of hers: steady hands, furrowed brow, no comment out loud but everything said in the deliberate tightness of the knots. _You deserve it for not dodging._ Chaeyoung could almost hear it.

One week of minimal rest, Tzuyu had said. No combat, no intense training.

She wasn't even going to last a day.

She dressed in the dark. She didn't need light; she knew every corner of her room with her eyes closed, which wasn't an impressive feat considering the entire room fit inside a sigh. Bed, trunk, coat rack, the chair where she left her boots. Somewhere at the bottom of the trunk, wrapped in cloth, her father's sword slept a sleep Chaeyoung didn't dare disturb.

She pulled on her shirt carefully, one arm first, then the other, the movement calculated to avoid stretching her side. Trousers, belt, boots. She tied her hair back, looked at her hands for a moment, knuckles still scabbed from the battle, and walked out.

The east wing corridor was empty. At this hour it always was: too late for the sleepless, too early for the early risers. Just the wall torches burning at half flame and the sound of her boots against stone.

Chaeyoung walked without hurry. She passed Momo's door, who even asleep found a way to make noise, a snore filtering through the wood that almost made her smile. She passed Dahyun's door, silent, which meant she was either asleep or had gone down to the forge before anyone else, both options equally likely.

The castle had a way of feeling different at night. Bigger. More honest, somehow. Without people filling the spaces, the corridors showed what they really were: old stone, torches, shadows. A place built to withstand things.

_Like you,_ her father said.

She descended the main staircase and crossed the general courtyard. The cold greeted her like a wall, the kind of cold that slips between clothes and skin and stays there, clinging, as if it had every right. The sky was at that point between night and dawn where everything turns dark blue. As if the world were holding its breath before deciding what kind of day it was going to be.

She could see her breath in the air. The castle towers stood sharp against the sky like teeth of stone, and beyond them, to the east, a thin line of light was beginning to peek above the hills. It wasn't dawn yet. It was the promise of one.

She crossed the courtyard with her hands in her pockets and her side protesting with every step. A cat crossed in front of her without deigning to look at her. From the stables, distant and soft, Cricket whinnied. The horse had an internal clock more precise than any bell in the castle.

The training yard was at the back, behind the armory. Smaller than the general courtyard, with compacted sand floor and straw dummies lined against the wall like a silent and patient audience. Racks of practice weapons along one side. A stone bench that nobody used because sitting in the training yard was admitting weakness, and here nobody admitted anything.

Chaeyoung didn't take a practice sword. Not this time. Tzuyu would kill her if she saw her swinging steel the day after getting hurt. Instead, she stood in front of one of the dummies and started with forms: slow, controlled movements, the imaginary sword tracing arcs in the cold air. Feet on the sand, weight shifting from one leg to the other, arms remembering what the body knew how to do in its sleep.

It was the gentlest she could train. And still, every time she turned her torso, her side bit back.

_Ignore it._

_You'll be fine,_ her father said. _You're strong._

She'd been at it for a while, long enough for sweat to run down the back of her neck despite the cold. A voice came from the entrance of the yard.

"It's been a while since I saw you doing forms. You've improved quite a bit."

Chaeyoung didn't turn. She didn't need to. Only one person in the entire castle had the habit of showing up at the training yard before the sun had the decency to rise.

"Couldn't sleep," she said, wiping the sweat from her forehead with the back of her hand.

"Me neither…" Jihyo walked over to the weapon rack and chose a practice sword with the familiarity of someone who's done it a thousand times. She weighed it, turned it once, nodded to herself. Then she looked at Chaeyoung, looked at the bandaged side under the shirt, and put the sword back on the rack. "Forms, then."

She placed herself beside Chaeyoung, an arm's length away, and began moving with her. No swords, no contact.

Just two bodies tracing the same patterns in the cold air of dawn, the language they shared better than any conversation.

The first form was slow. Basic positions, the kind of thing they did in their first year of training. But there was something comforting in the simplicity, in letting the muscles remember without demanding.

After a while, it was Jihyo who broke the silence. Between one position and the next, as if the question were part of the choreography.

"So? Did you meet her?"

"Yes. Nayeon took me to the terrace after dinner."

"And what's she like?"

Chaeyoung thought for a moment. The image of Mina under candlelight, the book in her hands, the cup of tea, that calm that seemed to belong to another world.

"Quiet," she said at last. "She reads. Drinks tea. Offered me a cup."

"And you accepted?"

"What was I going to do, say no to a princess?"

"You say no to everyone."

Another form. Arms up, the turn of the torso. Chaeyoung held back a groan, and though Jihyo noticed, she said nothing.

"When do you start?"

"Today."

Jihyo stopped moving.

"Today? With that?" She gestured toward Chaeyoung's side with her chin.

"I'm not going to fight anyone. I'm going to stand in a hallway."

"Knowing your luck, the hallway will collapse."

Chaeyoung almost smiled. Almost.

Jihyo resumed the forms, but slower now, as if she were thinking while she moved.

"You know that being a princess's personal guard isn't what you do, right? It's not tactics or formations. It's being there. All day. Every day. You and her in a room. You and her in a hallway. You and her in a library while she reads for hours and you can't even sit down."

"Sounds delightful," the younger one joked.

"Sounds like you're going to lose your mind in three days."

"Nayeon trusts me," Chaeyoung replied.

"Nayeon trusts your sword." Jihyo's voice had no edge, but it had weight. There's a difference.

The silence that followed was the kind that said things, and Chaeyoung heard it. Everything Jihyo wasn't saying: _why you and not me? I'm the captain. I should have been the first choice._ And underneath that, something simpler and harder: _be careful._

"They gave me the position because I'm the best sword, after all," Chaeyoung said, without arrogance. It was a fact.

"They need you where you are. Leading everyone, not guarding a princess."

Jihyo didn't answer right away. She resumed the form where they'd left off, the movements slow and precise, as if the answer were in the balance of the body.

"You're probably right," the captain replied in a sigh. Low but controlled.

"I'm always right." An innocent comment, trying to ease the tension.

"Don't push it."

Chaeyoung did smile this time. Small, quick, the kind of smile that Jihyo was one of the few people to see.

Jihyo looked at her for a moment. Then she nodded, just once, jaw tight and something that might have been pride crossing her face.

"Be careful, Chaeyoung."

"I always am."

"Lie." But there was something softer in her voice now. Something that resembled a blessing disguised as a scolding.

"You'd better do a good job. If Princess Mina complains, they're going to ask me why I recommended a lieutenant who fell asleep on duty."

"You recommended me?"

Jihyo was already walking toward the stone bench that nobody used for sitting. Apparently, being captain gave you permission to admit certain things.

"I didn't say that."

"You just said it," Chaeyoung protested.

"You heard wrong," Jihyo replied, firm, convincing in tone.

"I have excellent hearing."

"You have an excellent ability to make things up." Jihyo leaned back against the bench, crossing her arms. The first light of dawn hit one side of her face, leaving the other in shadow. "I mean it. Be careful. Not just with what's out there. The court is... different."

Chaeyoung nodded.

"I know."

"No. You don't. But you'll learn." Jihyo looked at her a second longer, as if making sure the message had landed.

Then something changed in her expression. The captain softened, barely, just enough to notice. "Congratulations, Chaeyoung. I mean it."

Chaeyoung opened her mouth to respond, but Jihyo was already standing, brushing the sand off her trousers.

"Go take a bath. You smell like dirt, sweat, and nerves."

"I'm not—"

"You smell like nerves, Chaeyoung. The princess is going to think they assigned her a wild animal," Jihyo declared firmly.

And she left. Without waiting for an answer, without giving Chaeyoung time to decide whether she should say thanks or make a joke. Typical Jihyo.

Chaeyoung stood alone in the yard, the dawn already more light than dark. The cold was still there but it had lost its edge, as if the light were softening it. The air smelled of damp earth and metal, and from the stables, Cricket whinnied again, louder this time, demanding a breakfast no one had promised him.

_You'll be fine,_ her father said.

This time, Chaeyoung wanted to believe him.

Chaeyoung stayed in the yard a moment longer after Jihyo left. The sky was slowly filling with pale tones and golden edges, the kind of sky that promised a good day without fully committing to it. The straw dummies watched her with their faceless faces, patient as always.

_Go take a bath. You smell like dirt, sweat, and nerves._

"I don't smell like nerves," she muttered under her breath, complaining, to no one.

She walked back to the general courtyard and crossed toward the east wing. The castle was waking up: a pair of servants crossed in front of her carrying baskets of laundry, a night watch guard yawned as he passed, and from the kitchens came the first smell of baked bread that Chaeyoung could have followed with her eyes closed.

Her room greeted her the same way she'd left it. Rumpled bed, boots at the foot of the chair, the wooden tiger figurine Tzuyu had given her watching from the desk with its expression of eternal feline disinterest. The ointment Tzuyu had given her sat on the nightstand, which she'd forgotten to apply before sleeping. _Another thing I'm not going to tell her._

She undressed slowly. The shirt first, with that careful movement, one arm, then the other, without turning her torso more than necessary. Tzuyu's bandage was still in place, tight and firm, doing its job without complaining, which was more than she could say for her own ribs.

She looked at herself in the washbasin's reflection. The water gave back a fragmented image: messy hair, faint dark circles, the line of the bandage peeking out below the collarbone. Not exactly the image of the kingdom's best sword.

_Doesn't matter how you look. Matters how you fight._

That's what her father would have said. But today she wasn't going to fight. Today she was going to stand in front of a princess, and for some reason she couldn't fully explain, that felt more terrifying than any monster.

She bathed quickly. The water was cold, as it always was in the east wing, another one of the "privileges" of living where those without noble titles lived, but it helped clear her head. She dried off carefully, checked that the bandage hadn't loosened, and stood in front of the coat rack.

"The uniform…" she sighed.

Not the full set; the steel breastplate and gauntlets were for combat, not for escorting a princess. But the light version was still more pieces than Chaeyoung was used to putting on before dawn.

She started with the base: a fitted dark undershirt that helped keep the bandage in place. Then the white uniform shirt, thick but flexible fabric, with the emblem of the ruling noble house embroidered in gold over the chest. She fastened it carefully, adjusting the front laces with more attention than usual. She tucked it into her trousers. Pulled it out a bit. Tucked it back in.

_You're getting dressed for work, not for a ball._

The dark trousers, reinforced at the knees. The boots she'd polished the night before when she couldn't sleep, not because she wanted to impress anyone, but because dirty boots are a lack of discipline. That's all. Then the hardened leather bracers on her forearms, tightened with straps her fingers knew by heart. The wide belt with the kingdom's buckle. The sword at her hip. Its familiar weight, the only thing that made sense this morning.

She tied her hair back. Adjusted the bracer straps. Adjusted the belt. Smoothed the shirt one more time.

She looked at herself in the washbasin.

The uniform fit her well; it always had, it was one of the few things that were made for someone like her. The golden emblem gleamed against the white fabric. The leather bracers gave her a more serious, more official look. Less village peasant, more royal guard.

_Best you're going to get,_ she thought, and after a long sigh, she left her room.

The walk from the east wing to the royal wing was a route Chaeyoung had memorized since last night, but under daylight it felt completely different. At night, with Nayeon guiding her, the hallways had been mysterious, solemn. Now they were simply obscene. The ceilings climbed higher and higher as she advanced, as if the architecture needed to remind her exactly where their world began and where hers ended. The iron torches of the east wing gave way to candelabras with more candles than Chaeyoung could count. The floor stopped being rough stone and became something polished that reflected the morning light like a cloudy mirror.

The tapestries on the walls showed scenes of ancient battles, knights mounted on white steeds charging against conveniently ugly enemies. Chaeyoung glanced at them as she passed and thought that none of those painted knights had mud on their faces or bandages on their ribs.

Everything smelled different. In the east wing it smelled of metal, sweat, and leather. Here it smelled of expensive candle wax and flowers Chaeyoung couldn't name.

She arrived at the door to Mina's chambers, recognized it by the carved vines on the frame, because apparently in the royal wing even the doors had decoration, and positioned herself to one side. Back straight. Hands at her sides.

The posture they'd taught her on the first day of training and that after years was as natural as breathing.

The hallway was empty. No servants, no guards, no sound. Just the distant murmur of the castle waking and the song of a bird somewhere in the garden.

Chaeyoung waited.

She adjusted the belt. Adjusted the sword. Adjusted the collar of the shirt. Adjusted the belt again.

_Stop fidgeting. You look nervous._

She wasn't nervous.

_Lie._

On the other side of the door, Mina had been awake longer than anyone would suspect.

It was an old habit. Waking before anyone knocked on her door, before the first maid arrived with hot water, before the castle decided what to do with her that day. A small pocket of time that was only hers, minutes in which she could lie still between the sheets, watching the light move across the ceiling, thinking things she didn't have to justify to anyone.

This morning she had thought about the terrace. About the cup of tea she had served the knight, Knight Chaeyoung, and the way she had accepted it. Carefully, as if the weight of the porcelain surprised her. As if no one had ever offered her something delicate before.

_Do you enjoy poetry?_

_I haven't had much time for reading, to be honest._

_Perhaps I can change that._

Had that been too much? Mina rewound the conversation for the third time, searching for the exact moment where she had crossed some line between courtesy and... something else. She didn't find the line. Which meant she hadn't crossed it, or that she had crossed it so cleanly that not even she noticed.

Three soft knocks on the door interrupted her thoughts.

"Your Highness, good morning."

Hana. The first of the three maids who took turns attending to her in the morning. Punctual as always, kind as always, efficient as always.

"Come in," Mina replied, rising to sit elegantly at the edge of the bed.

What followed was a ritual Mina could execute in her sleep, and some days, nearly did. Hana entered with hot water and towels while Soyeon followed with the day's dress already selected, pale blue, a choice Mina didn't remember making but that was probably decided the night before by someone who knew more about morning protocol than she did.

She washed her face. Dried it with the towel Hana offered with both hands extended, as if delivering a towel were a ceremonial act. Then came the dress, and this was the part that Mina, after twenty-six years, still couldn't make feel normal.

She didn't dress herself. She had never dressed herself. Soyeon unfastened her sleeping gown with practiced fingers while Hana held the dress open like someone presenting an offering. Mina raised her arms and the fabric fell over her, soft, light, cold for a second before taking the temperature of her pale skin. Soyeon adjusted the back laces with precise tugs, and Hana handled the details: the collar, the cuffs, the fold of the hem that had to fall at exactly ankle height.

Mina looked at herself in the full-length mirror. The woman who looked back was flawless. She always was. That was the function of mirrors in the royal wing: to confirm that everything was in its place. That the princess looked as she should. That the living portrait remained worthy of its frame.

"Your hair, Your Highness?" Hana asked.

"Up. But loose." Which was a contradiction that Hana, through years of practice, understood perfectly: a low bun, not tight, leaving some strands free around the face.

While Hana worked on her hair, Mina looked toward the door.

The new guard started today. The best sword in the kingdom according to her sister, the one who sat on the terrace with a rigid back and accepted a cup of tea as if it were a mission.

Mina wondered if she would already be outside.

Probably not. It's still early. Knights had their own schedules, their own routines. There was no reason for her to be there already.

But a part of her, the part that read people with the same attention she read her books, suspected that Chaeyoung was the type who arrived first. Something in the way she had accepted the position, in how she looked at her without blinking when she spoke, in that "protecting someone is protecting someone" said with the simple conviction of someone who truly believes it.

"Done, Your Highness," Hana said, stepping back.

Mina stood. Smoothed her dress. Picked up the book she was reading from the nightstand, _The Songs of Aelindor_, volume three, because two volumes weren't enough to tell a good tragedy, and walked toward the door.

When she stepped out, she was greeted by a nervous smile. Chaeyoung was there.

Mina paused mid-step. Not from surprise, but from the detail. The knight was exactly where she should be: at one side of the door, back straight, hands at her sides, the posture of someone who'd been waiting more than a few minutes but would rather swallow her sword than admit it. The white uniform with the golden hummingbird emblem suited her well, different from the dark green shirt of last night on the terrace, more official, more distant. Clean, neat, ready. _Pretty._

She had groomed herself. Mina noticed. Not as a minor detail, as something she filed away.

"Knight Chaeyoung."

"Good morning, Your Highness."

The voice was firm, formal, almost military. Different from the tone on the terrace. Mina registered the shift without commenting on it. _At night she relaxes. By day she armors up. Interesting._

"Have you been waiting long?" she asked, though she already knew the answer.

"Just long enough, Your Highness."

"You lie very poorly for someone who I assume has to be diplomatic with royalty."

Something crossed Chaeyoung's face, quick, contained, like a fish beneath the surface of the water. Amusement?

Discomfort? It was hard to tell. The knight had an interesting face to read: everything was there, but in very small print.

"I didn't intend to lie, Your Highness. Just… round the truth."

"Round the truth…" Mina repeated, tasting the words. "I like that expression. I'll use it the next time I meet with the royal court."

She turned briefly toward the interior of her chambers, the second book, the one she needed for the library after breakfast. She found it where she'd left it, on the corner of the desk, next to the pile of scrolls she probably should

have read the previous week. She picked it up and placed it on top of the first with the practiced balance of someone who always carries books.

When she turned, she noticed Chaeyoung was looking into the room. Not invasively, more like a reflex, eyes scanning the space by instinct before the mind could tell them it was impolite. Mina saw what the knight was seeing: the wall of books, the canopied bed, the desk, the light streaming through the tall windows.

Mina's world, seen from the doorway. A golden cage with excellent lighting.

Chaeyoung looked away. If she was embarrassed about looking, she hid it well.

"Ready," Mina said. "Shall we?"

Chaeyoung nodded and positioned herself at Mina's left, half a step behind. The distance was precise, almost measured, close enough to react, far enough not to intrude. _She was taught well,_ Mina thought. _Or she learned it on her own?_

They walked together down the long corridor.

The royal wing hallways unfolded before them and Mina did what she always did: walk without seeing. The tapestries, the candelabras, the portraits, all of it so familiar it had ceased to exist years ago. They were the background of her life, not the content.

But today she was conscious of the steps behind her. Of the rhythm of boots on polished floor, a sound different from the maids' slippers, from Sana's soft shoes, from Nayeon's calculated heels. Chaeyoung's boots sounded like what they were: something built for terrain that wasn't this.

"Do you always walk like that?" Mina asked without turning.

"Your Highness?" Chaeyoung's voice tinged with genuine doubt.

"Like you're expecting something to jump out of the walls."

A brief silence. Mina could hear Chaeyoung choosing between the real answer and the correct one.

"It's part of the job, Your Highness. Staying alert."

"Even when you're not working?"

"I'm always working," the younger one replied softly.

Mina turned her head to look at her. Not with surprise, with the curiosity of someone noting a piece of data that confirms a hypothesis.

"That sounds exhausting."

"You get used to it."

"That doesn't mean it's not exhausting."

Chaeyoung didn't respond. Mina didn't press. She had learned long ago that people's silences said more than their words, and Chaeyoung's silence was saying _certainly, but it's the life I was dealt._

They continued walking and descended the marble staircase that connected the royal wing with the north corridor, passing the portraits of the royal family. Generations of faces looking out from gilded frames with expressions ranging from severe to vaguely sad. Mina didn't look at a single one. She passed them like someone passing furniture that had always been there.

"The royal dining hall is in the north wing," she said, because the silence was beginning to be too aware of itself. "My sister, Princess Nayeon, has breakfast there when she doesn't have early meetings. Princess Sana arrives late, but today she might be punctual. I told her there would be pastries."

"And that works? Motivating her with pastries?"

"Without fail," Mina replied, with a faint smile that nearly went unnoticed by Chaeyoung.

Something crossed Chaeyoung's face again, that gesture that was almost a smile but stopped just before becoming one. Mina wanted to ask why she held back, but she didn't.

_Time,_ she told herself. _There's time._

They arrived at the dining hall. The double doors were wide open, and the aroma of freshly baked bread, fruit, and hot tea came out to greet them like an overly enthusiastic host.

Mina paused a moment before entering and breathed.

At her side, half a step behind, Chaeyoung did the same, attentive to the princess's actions.

With a slight nod from Mina, the guards opened the dining hall doors.

The royal dining hall was smaller than the name suggested.

It wasn't the great banquet hall; that was in another wing, reserved for events, celebrations, and the occasions when the royal family needed to impress someone. This was the private dining room, the one for breakfasts and family dinners, and it was just the right size to be intimate without ceasing to be absurdly luxurious by royal standards. A dark wood table polished to look like glass occupied the center, surrounded by chairs upholstered in crimson velvet.

Tall windows let in the morning light, which fell on the white porcelain dishes with gold edges and the silver cutlery that probably cost more than Chaeyoung's annual salary. Platters of fresh fruit, freshly baked bread, cheeses, jams, and a steaming teapot covered the table with the careless generosity of those who have never had to worry about whether there would be enough.

Princess Nayeon was already seated at the head.

The heir had a scroll in one hand and a cup of tea in the other, reading with a slight frown, the expression of someone who was already working before the sun had finished rising. Her hair was done with more formality than Mina's, her dress was a darker, more serious blue, and there was something in the way she occupied the chair that made it seem more throne than dining chair.

Mina knew that posture. It was the posture of _I have fourteen things on my mind and breakfast isn't one of them._ "Good morning, sister," Mina greeted as she entered.

Nayeon looked up from the scroll. Her eyes went first to Mina, a quick, automatic scan, the silent check she did every time she saw her, as if making sure she was still in one piece, and then to Chaeyoung, who had positioned herself behind Mina's chair with the precision of someone who had rehearsed the position in her head.

A nod. Brief, professional. _Good. You're here._

That was all. Nayeon went back to the scroll.

"Good morning. Did you sleep well?" she asked without looking up, in the tone of someone who asks the question out of habit rather than expecting a revealing answer.

"Yes," Mina replied. Which was a rounded truth, using the expression she had just learned.

She sat in her usual place, the second seat to Nayeon's right, with an empty seat between them. Sana's seat. Empty, as always at this hour.

"Sana?" Mina asked, pouring herself tea.

"I sent word that breakfast was served twenty minutes ago." Nayeon took a sip of her cup without looking up. "That means she'll arrive in ten."

"I told her there would be pastries."

Nayeon looked at her over the scroll. One eyebrow raised.

"Five, then."

Mina felt the shadow of a smile and let it live. These were the moments she liked about Nayeon, the brief ones, the ones that slipped through the cracks of the crown. When she was her sister and not the heir. When the scroll could wait a second.

She served herself fruit and a piece of bread. She spread jam with slow, deliberate movements, not because she particularly cared about the distribution of the jam, but because the alternative was turning to look at Chaeyoung, and that required a reason she didn't have.

She could feel her there. Behind her, to her left, standing. Still as a statue with a heartbeat. Mina wondered if she'd been offered breakfast, if she'd eaten anything before coming, if she was hungry. She was probably hungry. She would probably not admit it under torture.

Before the thought went anywhere, the dining hall door burst open.

"Good morning, family!"

Sana entered the way she entered everywhere: as if the place had been waiting for her. She wore a pale pink dress that moved with every step, her hair loose and slightly wavy falling over her shoulders, and a smile that could light a hall without the need for candelabras. She was, as always, too bright for the hour.

"You're late," Nayeon said.

"I'm on time. Time just works differently for me." Sana dropped into her chair with grace and scanned the table with hunter's eyes. "Where are the pastries?"

"Good morning," Mina said quietly.

"Good morning, little sister. Where are the pastries?"

Mina gestured toward the platter at the far end of the table with her head. Sana reached for it with an enthusiasm unbefitting of someone of royal blood, nearly knocking over the teapot in the process.

"Careful with the—" Nayeon started.

"Got it!" Sana steadied the teapot with one hand while grabbing a pastry with the other, a balancing act she had perfected over years of chaotic breakfasts. "Mmm. Apple. The chef loves me."

"The chef is afraid of you," Nayeon corrected.

"Fear and love are very similar if you think about it," Sana said, preparing to take another bite.

"I'd rather not go deeper into that," Nayeon replied before returning to her reading.

Mina bit into her bread and let the conversation flow around her, as she always did. This was her place at the table, literally and figuratively. Nayeon at the head, carrying the weight. Sana beside her, lightening it. And Mina observing, listening, cataloguing.

She liked this. She didn't always admit it, not even to herself, but she liked the mornings when the three of them were together and the outside world hadn't yet come through the door. When Nayeon could set aside the scroll for a moment and Sana could be loud without anyone judging her and Mina could simply be there, being the third piece of something that worked.

"So," Sana turned toward Mina with the pastry half-eaten, and her eyes moved from her sister to something behind her. "Is that her?"

She didn't need to specify. Mina knew exactly who she meant.

"Knight Chaeyoung, my new personal guard," Mina introduced, with the formal tone she used when she needed Sana not to do exactly what she was about to do.

Sana turned in her chair to look at Chaeyoung directly. Without pretense. Without protocol. With the brazen appraisal of someone who doesn't know the word subtle.

"Oh. She's smaller than I imagined…"

Mina closed her eyes for half a second. _Please, Sana._

"But she has presence," Sana continued, tilting her head as if evaluating a sculpture. "I like the uniform. Do you always carry the sword or is it just to impress?"

"Always, Your Highness," Chaeyoung replied. Her voice was firm, neutral. Professional. If Sana's evaluation made her uncomfortable, she didn't show it.

"Fascinating. And is it true what they say, that you killed a basilisk on your own?"

"It wasn't alone, Your Highness. Captain Jihyo—"

"Yes, yes, but the part where you drove the sword through its eye was yours, right?" Sana bit another piece of pastry.

"That's what I heard…"

"Sana," Nayeon warned without looking up from the scroll.

"I'm just getting to know the person who's going to spend all day with our sister. It's diligence, sister. Family responsibility," she declared with confidence, but the sly smile gave away her intentions.

"It's harassment."

"It's interest."

Mina stepped in before Chaeyoung had to endure anything else.

"I'm sure Knight Chaeyoung appreciates your… interest, Sana. But perhaps we can let her breathe." Sana gave her an innocent smile that had nothing innocent about it.

"Of course. Just one more question." She turned to Chaeyoung. "Do you like pastries?" The briefest of silences.

"Y-Yes, Your Highness," she replied respectfully.

"She's cute…" Sana whispered to Mina as she turned back to her breakfast as if nothing had happened.

Nayeon shook her head slightly, an exhale that might have been laughter in another life. Mina took a sip of tea to hide whatever was happening on her face.

Behind her, Chaeyoung remained standing. Still. Professional. But Mina would have bet her entire book collection that the knight, somewhere beneath that steel composure, was wondering what she had gotten herself into.

Breakfast continued. Nayeon spoke briefly about a meeting with the advisors that afternoon. Sana spoke extensively about a new dress she was expecting from the seamstress. Mina listened, nodded when appropriate, and ate her bread with jam in the comfortable silence that was her specialty.

The library of Twiceland was, according to what Chaeyoung had overheard once at dinner, one of the largest on the continent. Three stories of shelves rising to a vaulted ceiling painted with constellations that probably no longer existed. Dark wood staircases connected the levels, with carved railings and small balconies where one could stand and gaze into the void between the books. The windows were tall and narrow, designed to let in light without the wind, and at this hour of the morning the sun's rays fell at oblique angles that made the dust particles in the air glow like tiny stars floating aimlessly.

There were reading tables distributed across the first floor, thick wood, chairs with cushions worn by generations of scholarly bottoms, standing candelabras for the long nights. And books. Books everywhere. On shelves, on tables, stacked in corners, some open like birds that had fallen asleep mid-flight. The smell was unmistakable: old paper, leather, ink, and something else Chaeyoung couldn't name but associated with expensive silence. And Mina moved through this space like a fish in water.

Chaeyoung had followed her from the dining hall through two corridors, another staircase, and a hallway with more tapestries of invented battles, until arriving here. Mina hadn't hesitated a single step. Hadn't consulted any direction.

She had walked directly to the back of the first floor, turned left at the third shelf, run her hand along the spines of several books like someone caressing old friends, and pulled out three volumes with the certainty of someone who knew exactly where every word in this place lived.

She had sat at a table by the window, her table, clearly, because there was an empty cup waiting for her and an extra cushion on the chair that nobody else would have the nerve to leave there. With the distinctive elegance of the princess, Mina sat, and opened the book to the first page with great care, beginning to read.

And that had been two hours ago.

Chaeyoung stood a few meters from the table, against the wall, between two shelves. The position gave her sight lines to the main door, the stairs to the second floor, and the windows. Tactically, it was solid. Practically, it was torture.

Not because it was uncomfortable, well, yes, her legs were beginning to voice opinions about how long they'd been bearing the same weight without moving, and her side sent periodic reminders that it was there and wasn't happy. But the real problem wasn't the body. It was the mind.

There was nothing to do.

Chaeyoung was trained for action. For assessing threats, making split-second decisions, moving. Her brain was a machine designed to solve problems that involved things trying to kill her. And here, in the most silent library in the world, with the most tranquil person in the world reading meters away from her, that machine was spinning in idle.

She had counted the books on the shelf to her right. Two hundred and forty-three. Then the ones on the left. Two hundred and seventy-one. Then the floor tiles she could see from her position. One hundred and twelve complete and seven cut by the walls. Then the dust particles in a ray of sunlight, but she gave up after thirty because they kept appearing.

Jihyo had been right. _You and her in a library while she reads for hours and you can't even sit down._

She shifted her weight from one leg to the other. Imperceptible. Professional.

_This is your job now. Get used to it._

She would get used to it. She just needed to… adjust. Recalibrate. Find something to do with the mind while the body did its thing.

As her "luck" would have it, what her mind decided to do was watch Mina.

It wasn't a conscious decision. It was the natural result of having absolutely nothing else to watch. The door hadn't moved in two hours. The windows were still windows. The shelves hadn't changed position. The only thing moving in the entire library was Mina, and she moved so little that the movement became something that deserved attention.

Her fingers turned the pages with a rhythm of their own. Not fast; Mina didn't read in a hurry. But it wasn't passive reading either. Every so often her left hand would pause on the page, her index finger tracing something, a line, a shape, a character, with the concentration of someone deciphering rather than simply reading. Sometimes her lips moved. Almost imperceptible, the shadow of a word forming and dissolving without sound, something Chaeyoung didn't recognize, something that didn't sound like anything she'd heard in the villages or the camps. She was translating, perhaps, or memorizing something that deserved to be savored rather than consumed.

Chaeyoung noted the detail. _She furrows her brow when she reads._ Professional information. Part of the job.

Every now and then, Mina would raise her left hand and tuck a strand of hair behind her ear. The gesture was so automatic she probably didn't know she was doing it. The strand would fall again a few minutes later, and she would tuck it back, and Chaeyoung would notice it again, and the cycle repeated with a regularity that was starting to resemble something hypnotic.

_Professional information,_ she repeated to herself. _Knowing your charge's habits is part of—_ The light from the window shifted.

Not suddenly; the sun had been changing angle during the two hours they'd been there, so gradual that Chaeyoung hadn't registered it. But now, in this specific moment, the light fell directly on Mina's table. On the book, on her hands, on her face.

And something happened.

Nothing dramatic. Nothing Chaeyoung could point to or name or explain if someone asked her later. Just that the sunlight hit Mina in a particular way and Chaeyoung was looking at her and for a second, just one, brief, insignificant, she wasn't assessing threats or counting tiles or thinking about her job.

She was just looking.

The way the light made her hair seem almost brown instead of black. The lashes casting tiny shadows on her cheeks.

The curve of concentration in her brow, not a furrow, but something softer, as if the thoughts she was having were heavy but not unpleasant. The line of her jaw. The absolute calm that emanated from her, as if nothing existed outside of this table, this book, this page.

_She is…_

Chaeyoung cut the thought before it went anywhere.

_Professional. You're being professional. This is observation. This is your job._

She straightened. Shifted her weight again. Looked at the door. Still a door.

Looked at Mina again.

_Damn it._

The problem, and Chaeyoung recognized it with the same cold clarity with which she evaluated a battlefield, was that the library was too peaceful. Too warm. Too silent. The kind of silence that didn't alert the senses but dulled them, that crept in at the edges of awareness and softened them one by one.

Her eyes closed for half a second.

She opened them. Looked around. No one had seen her. Mina was still reading.

_Don't fall asleep. Don't fall asleep. It's your first day. If you fall asleep on your first day—_

The light was still warm. The silence was still thick. The smell of old paper and leather was, Chaeyoung discovered, extraordinarily soporific when you had nothing to do with your hands.

Another head nod. Longer this time. Her head fell forward a centimeter before she jerked it back with a sharp movement that pulled at her side and made her clench her teeth.

_Great. Spectacular. Knight Chaeyoung, defeated by a book and a princess._

The minutes stretched. Or did they shrink? It was hard to tell. Time in the library worked differently than anywhere else, slower, thicker, like honey. Chaeyoung fought against the drowsiness with the same determination she fought against everything: in silence, out of pure stubbornness, refusing to lose.

She was already losing.

Her eyelids were heavy. The wall behind her felt comfortable, since when are walls comfortable?, and her breathing had become slow, deep, the kind of breathing that precedes sleep and that the body does without asking permission. Another head nod. This time her head dropped far enough for her chin to touch her chest. She snapped upright, heart racing, eyes opening as if someone had shouted her name.

No one had shouted her name.

But someone was watching her.

Mina had been observing her for a while.

Not from the beginning; the first hours had been genuinely absorbing. _The Alphabets of Lost Civilizations_ had that quality: every page was a puzzle, every symbol a door that opened to three more, and when you were inside the outside world ceased to exist. But at some point between the second and third chapters, her attention had begun to drift.

First it was the sounds. The nearly inaudible creak of the leather of Chaeyoung's bracers when she shifted weight from one leg to the other, a movement the knight probably thought imperceptible but that in the silence of the library was as clear as a bell. Then the rhythm of her breathing, which Mina had learned to distinguish from the ambient silence. And then, the change.

The breathing slowed. The creaks of the leather grew further apart. And when Mina looked up from the book for the first time in what must have been hours, she saw Chaeyoung standing against the wall, eyes half-closed and jaw clenched in a silent battle against sleep.

Mina said nothing. She went back to the book.

But she wasn't reading anymore. She was listening. Waiting.

The next head nod was longer. Mina caught it from the corner of her eye, the way Chaeyoung's head fell forward and then snapped back with a jerk that made her wince. _Is she hurt?,_ Mina thought. _And yet she's been standing for hours without moving._

And she hadn't said anything. Of course she hadn't said anything. Mina was beginning to recognize the pattern: Chaeyoung didn't mention the things that hurt her. She absorbed them. Ignored them. Rounded them.

The final head nod was the definitive one, chin touching chest, the startle, eyes snapping open like an animal that detects a predator. And meeting Mina's.

For an instant, half a second, maybe less, Chaeyoung wore the most honest expression Mina had seen on her: pure panic. The kind of panic that doesn't come from fighting some monster or creature, but from something far worse, your boss catching you asleep on your first day.

Mina let her suffer for exactly two seconds.

Then she closed the book with a sharp snap that echoed through the library like a gunshot.

Chaeyoung straightened as if someone had plugged her in.

"I think," Mina said, rising from the chair and stretching her arms with the ease of someone who hadn't noticed a thing, "I need to walk a bit. Would you accompany me to the garden, Knight Chaeyoung?"

Chaeyoung's eyes studied her. Evaluating. Searching for the trap, the comment, the reprimand. She found nothing. Just Mina standing, three books under her arm and a perfectly neutral expression.

"Of course, Your Highness."

Mina walked toward the door without hurry. Behind her, she heard Chaeyoung peel herself off the wall and follow, steps a bit faster than usual, as if trying to compensate for something.

She didn't turn. She didn't smile. She didn't say anything.

But as she walked between the shelves toward the exit, with the afternoon light streaming through the tall windows and the dust particles floating around her like a universe in miniature, Mina had to bite the inside of her lip to stop the quiet laugh forming in her chest.

The garden was the closest thing Mina had to freedom.

It wasn't large, not compared to the hunting grounds north of the castle or the training fields to the south. But it was hers in the way a place can belong to someone without anyone having decreed it: by habit, by presence, by being the only person who went often enough to know every corner.

It was a walled garden, nestled between the royal wing and the lesser tower, shielded from the wind by stone walls covered in ivy. Gravel paths wound between flower beds that changed with the seasons, and now, in early autumn, it was golden chrysanthemums and red dahlias that ruled, scattered with lavender that refused to surrender to the cold. A weeping willow occupied the center like a hunched elder, its branches touching the surface of a small pond where orange fish lived their lives oblivious to the problems of royalty. Stone benches were scattered along the paths, half-hidden by the bushes, designed for contemplation or for conversations that couldn't happen within the walls.

Mina walked slowly. She was never in a hurry, never in a hurry in the garden. It was the only place where the speed of her steps matched the speed of her thoughts, and both were deliberately slow.

Behind her, half a step to the left, Chaeyoung followed.

The knight was more awake now. The fresh outdoor air seemed to have revived her like a plant after rain; her posture was more alert, her steps firmer, and her eyes did that constant sweep of the surroundings that Mina was already learning to recognize. Left, right, up, behind. A cycle that repeated every few seconds, automatic as a heartbeat.

But today, Mina noticed something different in the pattern.

The sweep wasn't constant. It was interrupted. Chaeyoung's eyes kept returning, again and again, to the same point. And that point was Mina.

She didn't notice it right away. It was gradual, like the light changing angle in the library, something that accumulated until it became evident. Mina was looking at a row of particularly stubborn chrysanthemums that had decided to grow outside the flower bed when she felt the weight of the gaze. Not the professional weight, the threat-assessment one, the one that meant _I'm doing my job._ It was a different weight. Softer. More lingering.

She turned slightly, as if to admire a rosebush at the side of the path, and confirmed it from the corner of her eye: Chaeyoung was looking at her. Not at the garden, not at the entrances, not at the walls. At her. With that expression Mina had glimpsed in the library, focused, attentive, as if she were trying to memorize something she didn't want to forget.

When Chaeyoung noticed that Mina had turned, her eyes immediately shifted to a bush on the right with the conviction of someone who had always been looking at that bush, not at the princess, never at the princess, what princess?

Mina kept walking. She let a few seconds pass.

"That bush is not going to attack me, Knight Chaeyoung."

The silence that followed was eloquent.

"I-I was evaluating the perimeter, Your Highness."

"The perimeter of the bush?" A faint smile appeared on Mina's face, quickly replaced by a formal and attentive expression befitting a princess.

"Bushes can be… deceptive."

Mina stopped and turned fully this time, facing Chaeyoung on the path. The knight looked at her with an expression that was a study in professional neutrality, jaw firm, shoulders straight, posture impeccable. Everything perfect. Except the ears, which were a shade pinker than they'd been a minute ago.

_The ears,_ Mina thought. _That's where she hides it._

"You look at me a lot," the princess said. Not as an accusation. As an observation. The same tone she would use to note that it was raining or that the tea had gone cold.

Chaeyoung didn't flinch, doing her utmost to project confidence.

"It is my duty to observe you, Your Highness. To ensure your safety."

"I see. And does safety require you to observe me with so much… attention?"

Another silence. Longer. Mina could see the gears turning behind Chaeyoung's eyes, the same speed she probably used to calculate attack angles and weak points, now applied to the far more complex problem of what to tell a princess who just caught you staring.

"I'm familiarizing myself with your routine, Your Highness. The better I know your patterns and movements, the more effectively I can—"

"My patterns and movements," Mina repeated.

"Yes, Your Highness."

"And what patterns have you observed so far?"

Chaeyoung opened her mouth. Closed it. Opened it again.

"That… you read a lot, Your Highness."

Mina looked at her. Chaeyoung looked at her. The weeping willow dropped a leaf into the pond with a soft plop that neither of them heard.

"That I read a lot…" Mina said in a soft tone, mildly unimpressed by her knight's answer.

"Yes…"

"Fascinating observation. I truly am in good hands."

It was a second. Half a second. A tiny fraction of time in which Chaeyoung's professional composure cracked and something real peeked through, something that looked dangerously like a smile. It didn't quite become one. It stayed in the anteroom, in that territory between seriousness and surrender where the things Chaeyoung wouldn't allow herself lived.

But Mina saw it. And filed it away.

_There it is,_ she thought. _Underneath all of that, there it is._

She resumed the walk. The path curved to the right, bordering the pond, passing beneath the willow branches that hung like curtains of an empty stage. The afternoon sun, because it was already afternoon, the day had moved forward without asking permission, bathed the garden in warm tones that made the golden chrysanthemums look like little suns themselves.

They walked in silence. But it was a different silence from before, lighter, as if something had loosened half a turn. Mina didn't know if it was her imagination or if the distance between them had shortened by half a step. Probably her imagination.

"May I ask you a question?" Mina said, without turning.

"Of course, Your Highness." Chaeyoung's reply came too quickly, perhaps surprised by the sudden curiosity.

"Have you always been a knight?" the princess finished softly, still not looking at Chaeyoung, taking in the beautiful garden.

And after a moment, Chaeyoung answered, "Since I was fifteen, Your Highness. My father trained me before that." "Was your father a knight?"

"Yes." The answer was shorter than the ones before. More closed. Mina registered the shift in tone but didn't press. She had read enough books to know that the stories people don't want to tell are the ones that matter most, and that the worst way to hear them is to insist.

"My father wanted me to learn fencing," Mina said, shifting the weight of the conversation toward herself, offering something of her own to balance. "Nayeon convinced him it wasn't appropriate for a princess. Sana volunteered to take the lessons in my place. She lasted two days."

"What happened?"

"She broke a nail."

The sound that came from Chaeyoung was involuntary. Brief, contained, immediately stifled, but it was laughter. Real laughter, surprised, the kind of sound that escapes before a person can stop it.

Chaeyoung coughed. Cleared her throat and composed herself.

"I'm sorry to hear that, Your Highness."

"I'll be sure to pass along your sympathies to my sister, knight."

"Thank you, Your Highness." A genuine smile from Chaeyoung, gentle, charming.

And there it was, the first unrounded truth Chaeyoung had given her. Small, insignificant, a confession wrapped in a laugh that shouldn't have happened. But Mina took it and kept it the way one keeps a rare coin without yet knowing what it's worth.

The path led them to the farthest part of the garden, where the hedges were taller and the benches were half-hidden among the greenery. It was the most private area, the one Mina preferred for reading on days when the library felt too enclosed. Here the world shrank to the sounds of the garden, the water in the pond, the birds, the wind through the leaves, and everything else ceased to exist.

She stopped beside a bed of lavender and breathed deeply. The scent was clean, soft, the kind of scent that made thoughts organize themselves.

"I like this place," the princess said, though she didn't know why she said it out loud. It wasn't information her guard needed.

"It's beautiful, Your Highness," Chaeyoung replied. And then, as if the words had escaped before she could catch them, "You walk differently here."

Mina turned. "Differently?"

Chaeyoung seemed to immediately regret having spoken. But it was too late.

"I-in the hallways you walk… contained. As if every step had a purpose. Here it's…" she searched for the word, her jaw working, "slower… Looser. As if it didn't matter where you're going."

Mina looked at her. The knight held her gaze with an expression that clearly said _please stop looking at me like that, I shouldn't have said that, how do I erase the last ten seconds._

"I thought your only observation was that I read a lot," Mina said.

Chaeyoung didn't respond. Her ears betrayed her again.

Mina was about to say something more, intrigued by whatever other observations the knight might have, when a voice arrived from the other side of the bushes, preceded by the unmistakable sound of someone walking on gravel with the determination of someone bearing news that couldn't wait.

"Mina! There you are!"

Sana appeared through the hedges like a sun that decided to rise from the wrong side. Hair loose, pink dress flowing, a grin from ear to ear and a radiant energy.

Her eyes went from Mina to Chaeyoung, who had straightened even more than she already was, which shouldn't be anatomically possible, and back to Mina. Her smile widened with that mischief her sister already knew, much to her misfortune.

"Sorry, am I interrupting something?" she asked, malice camouflaged in her sweet, soft voice.

"A walk," Mina said, with all the neutrality she could muster.

"A walk." Sana came closer, hooking her arm through Mina's with the familiarity of a lifetime. "With your new guard. In the most hidden part of the garden."

"It's the part with the best lavender," Mina rescued quickly.

"Of course it is." Sana gave her arm a squeeze that said more than any words, and then turned to Chaeyoung with that smile that was half sun and half predator. "Knight Chaeyoung, are you enjoying your first day?"

"Yes, Your Highness," Chaeyoung replied, with the rigidity of a person who'd just been ambushed and didn't have her sword ready.

"I'm glad. My sister needs company that isn't books." Sana turned her head toward Mina and lowered her voice to a whisper that was no whisper at all. "She's quite attentive, isn't she? She hasn't taken her eyes off you since I arrived." Mina felt the heat climbing up her neck. "Sana."

"What? It's a compliment. It means she's good at her job." The innocence in Sana's voice was so artificial it could be sold as costume jewelry. "Or are you going to tell me you hadn't noticed?"

"Sana, did you need something?" _Let's get this over with._

Sana looked at her for a moment with that expression that meant _I know exactly what I'm doing and I'm having a great time,_ and then decided, out of mercy or boredom, to change the subject.

"Yes! I needed to talk to you about something important." She adjusted her hair with a dramatic gesture. "My birthday."

"Your birthday is in three weeks," Mina announced.

"Exactly. Three weeks. Which is practically tomorrow. And we still haven't discussed the celebration."

"I thought Nayeon handled the celebrations."

"Nayeon handles the events. I'm talking about the celebration. They are very different things, Mina. One has protocol and the other has fun," Sana commented as if it were the most obvious thing in the world.

Mina sighed, but it was the sigh she reserved for Sana, the one that held no real exasperation but rather the resigned acceptance of someone who knows the storm is going to pass over her whether she likes it or not.

"What do you have in mind?"

What followed was a detailed monologue about decorations, music, dresses, the guest list, "Everyone, Mina, absolutely everyone!", the menu, "the apple pastries are mandatory, non-negotiable," and the possibility of a ball, "a real ball, with an orchestra, not those boring dinners Nayeon organizes where everyone looks like they're at an elegant funeral."

Mina listened. Nodded when appropriate. Asked the right questions at the right moments. She was good at this, at being the audience Sana needed, the calm anchor around which the hurricane could spin.

Behind her, Chaeyoung remained standing. Mina didn't look at her, she couldn't, not with Sana there, not after the comment, but she felt her presence the way you feel the heat of a fireplace you're not looking at.

She wondered if Chaeyoung had heard what Sana said. The comment. The _she hasn't taken her eyes off you._ She wondered if the knight was staring straight ahead with professionalism or if her ears were pink again.

She didn't dare check.

When Sana finally exhausted her inventory of plans, or rather paused it, because Sana never truly finished planning anything, she just went into standby until the next idea, she kissed Mina on the cheek and left the way she'd come, leaving behind a trail of perfume and energy that the garden took several seconds to absorb.

The silence that remained felt different. Heavier. More aware of itself.

Mina stood looking at the chrysanthemums for a moment.

"Knight Chaeyoung."

"Yes, Your Highness."

"What Princess Sana said…"

"I didn't hear anything, Your Highness."

Mina turned to look at her. Chaeyoung was staring straight ahead. Perfect posture. Neutral expression. Absolutely, completely, suspiciously professional.

Her ears were pink.

Mina said nothing more. She turned toward the path that led back to the castle and began walking, the afternoon sun at her back and a smile that refused to stay hidden.

Behind her, half a step to the left, Chaeyoung followed.
